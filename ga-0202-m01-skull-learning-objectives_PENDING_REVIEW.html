1
Divide the skull into neurocranium vs viscerocranium, and name the bones (and which four are pneumatized).
content: L01 · Skull divisions & bones
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Split the skull two ways: the neurocranium (cranial bones that surround the brain) vs the viscerocranium (facial skeleton that supports the visceral organs — nasal & oral cavities). (L01 · divisions)
  • Name the cranial bones: frontal, two parietal, two temporal, occipital, ethmoid, sphenoid — and know ethmoid & sphenoid are cranial even though they sit deep. (L01 · cranial bones)
  • Place the face bones: zygomatic = cheekbone, nasal = bridge, lacrimal = tiny bone of the medial orbital wall, vomer = lower nasal septum, maxilla = upper jaw, palatine, inferior concha, mandible. (L01 · facial bones)
  • Flag the trap-worthy ones: hard palate = anterior 2/3 maxilla + posterior 1/3 palatine; inferior concha is its own bone (superior & middle conchae belong to ethmoid). (L01 · palate & conchae)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“We divide the bones into two parts … bones which surround the brain, we call those bones as neurocranium … and the bones of the face, which provide support to the visceral organs of the head and neck region … those bones are called as visceral cranial … sphenoid is a bat-shaped bone, looks like a bat.”— Osteology of the Skull (L01) · skull divisions
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · Skull Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which two “deep” bones are still cranial (neurocranium), and which four bones are pneumatized (have air sinuses)?
Ethmoid & sphenoid are cranial despite lying deep. The four pneumatized bones = frontal, maxillary, ethmoid, sphenoid. Neurocranium = brain-box (frontal, 2 parietal, 2 temporal, occipital, ethmoid, sphenoid); viscerocranium = facial skeleton.
2
Name the sutures by their articulating bones, and state why a suture is the body’s most stable joint.
content: L01 · Sutures & joints
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Define a suture: a fibrous joint between adjoining skull bones — the most stable / least mobile joint in the body. (L01 · sutures)
  • Use the rule — sutures are named after the bones they join: sagittal (two parietals), coronal (frontal + parietal), lambdoid (parietal + occipital), squamous (temporal + parietal), occipitomastoid (occipital + temporal/mastoid), frontonasal (nasal + frontal). (L01 · named sutures)
  • Handle the two special ones: the cruciform suture on the hard palate = intermaxillary + palatomaxillary + interpalatine combined; the metopic suture = a remnant of the frontal suture. (L01 · cruciform; L02 · metopic)
  • Answer her exam shape: “How would you name this suture?” — read off the two bones it lies between. (L01 · suture naming)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“It’s a joint between the adjoining skull bones … sutures are the most stable kind of joints in our body. It’s [the] least mobile, most stable … sutures, they are named after the articulating bones.”— Osteology of the Skull (L01) · sutures
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · Skull Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which suture joins the temporal to the parietal, and what three sutures combine to form the cruciform suture?
The squamous suture joins temporal + parietal. The cruciform suture = intermaxillary + palatomaxillary + interpalatine. Remember: coronal = frontal+parietal, sagittal = the two parietals, lambdoid = parietal+occipital.
3
Locate the cranial landmark points (nasion, bregma, lambda, pterion, asterion, vertex, inion, gonion, glabella) and know why they are fragile.
content: L01 · Cranial points & landmarks
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Define each junction: nasion (internasal meets frontonasal), bregma (coronal + sagittal), lambda (sagittal + lambdoid), asterion (squamous + lambdoid + occipitomastoid). (L01 · junction points)
  • Nail pterion — the meeting of frontal + parietal + temporal + sphenoid, with the middle meningeal artery running deep to it. (L01 · pterion)
  • Place the non-junction points: vertex = highest point (just behind bregma), inion = external occipital protuberance, gonion = angle of the mandible, glabella = part of the frontal bone. (L01 · vertex/inion/gonion/glabella)
  • State why they matter: these are points where multiple sutures meet, so they are fragile fracture sites. (L01 · fragile points)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“This is a meeting point of frontal, parietal, temporal, and sphenoid[al] bone … that means this is a very, very fragile point … [right] deep to this point, you have the branches of middle meningeal artery … which one of the following structure is associated with Pterion?”— Osteology of the Skull (L01) · lateral-view landmarks
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · Skull Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which four bones meet at the pterion, and what does “lambda” join? What is another name for the inion?
Pterion = frontal + parietal + temporal + sphenoid (middle meningeal artery deep to it). Lambda = sagittal + lambdoid sutures. Inion = the external occipital protuberance. Bregma = coronal + sagittal; asterion = squamous + lambdoid + occipitomastoid.
4
Identify the anterior & lateral view features — and, for each, which bone it belongs to.
content: L01 / L02 · Anterior & lateral view
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Read the anterior foramina by bone: supraorbital foramen (supraorbital nerve/artery/vein), infraorbital foramen = maxilla, mental foramen = mandible. (L01/L02 · anterior foramina)
  • Name the openings/spines: anterior nasal aperture = piriform (pear-shaped); anterior nasal spine = the two maxillae in the midline; frontal eminence = a primary ossification center. (L01/L02 · anterior view)
  • Build the zygomatic arch: temporal process of the zygomatic bone + zygomatic process of the temporal bone (they meet like two hands shaking). (L01 · zygomatic arch)
  • Catch the classic trap: the temporal lines belong to the PARIETAL bone, not temporal; and external acoustic meatus & styloid process both belong to the temporal bone. (L01/L02 · temporal lines / EAM / styloid)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“How is this zygomatic arch formed? … temporal process of zygomatic bone … and zygomatic process of temporal bone … it’s like my hand and your hand when we are shaking our hands … these [temporal] lines are not the part of temporal bone, they’re the part of parietal bone.”— Osteology of the Skull (L01) · lateral view
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · Skull Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
The temporal lines are part of which bone? And the zygomatic arch is built from which two processes?
The temporal lines belong to the parietal bone (a favorite trap). The zygomatic arch = temporal process of the zygomatic bone + zygomatic process of the temporal bone. Infraorbital foramen = maxilla; mental foramen = mandible; EAM & styloid = temporal.
5
Identify the posterior & superior (calvarial) view features — mastoid, nuchal lines, emissary/parietal foramina, wormian bones, and the sagittal sinus sulcus.
content: L01 · Posterior & superior view
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Place the posterior landmarks: mastoid process = temporal bone; the nuchal lines (superior/inferior) = attachment sites for neck muscles. (L01 · posterior view)
  • Explain the parietal (emissary) foramina: they transmit emissary veins, which connect extracranial and intracranial veins. (L01 · emissary veins)
  • Recognize wormian (sutural) bones — extra bones that ossify within the suture lines (a normal variant). (L01 · wormian bones)
  • Read the inner calvaria: the midline sulcus = groove for the superior sagittal sinus; the paramidline pits = depressions for arachnoid granulations. (L01 · inner calvaria)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Emissary means something coming from … another place … these foramina, they are for the passage of emissary veins. Emissary veins actually connect extracranial veins with the intracranial veins.”— Osteology of the Skull (L01) · posterior view
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · Skull Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
What do the parietal foramina transmit, and what forms the midline groove on the inside of the skull cap?
The parietal foramina transmit emissary veins (extracranial ↔ intracranial). The midline calvarial sulcus is the groove for the superior sagittal sinus; the paramidline pits are for arachnoid granulations. Wormian bones = extra bones within sutures.
6
Map the base-of-skull foramina to their bone and their contents — and beat the “next to the jugular foramen” view trap.
content: L01 / L02 · Base of skull · foramina
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Match the big ones: foramen magnum = spinal cord / medulla junction; carotid canal = internal carotid artery; jugular foramen (temporal + occipital) = internal jugular vein; stylomastoid foramen (between styloid & mastoid) = facial nerve. (L01/L02 · base foramina)
  • Do the bone-ownership questions: rotundum / ovale / spinosum = sphenoid; foramen lacerum = sphenoid + temporal + occipital; foramen spinosum is named for the spine of the sphenoid next to it. (L01/L02 · sphenoid foramina / lacerum)
  • Cover the palate & posterior landmarks: incisive foramen (in the incisive fossa), greater/lesser palatine foramina, pharyngeal tubercle (basilar occiput), pterygoid hamulus (sphenoid), occipital condyles articulate with C1 (atlas), digastric notch = digastric muscle. (L01/L02 · palate/posterior base)
  • Beat the trap: in the inferior view, the foramen next to the jugular foramen is the carotid canal; in the base-of-skull (internal) view, it is the internal acoustic meatus. (L02 · 00:52 & 01:06)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“In the inferior view, carotid canal is lying right next to jugular foramen … please write this in [bold] — in the inferior view only … [in the base-of-skull view], this foramen lying right next to jugular foramen … internal acoustic meatus. Good.”— Week 1 Lab (L02) · ~00:52:50 & ~01:06:00
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · Skull Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
The foramen right next to the jugular foramen — is it the carotid canal or the internal acoustic meatus? Which nerve exits the stylomastoid foramen?
It depends on the view: inferior view → carotid canal; base-of-skull (internal) view → internal acoustic meatus. The facial nerve exits the stylomastoid foramen. Rotundum/ovale/spinosum = sphenoid; lacerum = sphenoid + temporal + occipital.
7
Work through the three cranial fossae — the anterior floor, the sella turcica, and the SOF–rotundum–ovale–spinosum arch.
content: L01 · Cranial fossae
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State the division: the base of the skull = three cranial fossae — anterior, middle, posterior. (L01 · three fossae)
  • Build the anterior fossa floor: orbital plate of the frontal bone + cribriform plate of the ethmoid (transmits olfactory nerve fascicles) + lesser wing of the sphenoid; with crista galli (ethmoid) and the foramen cecum (blind — no structure passes). (L01 · anterior fossa)
  • Own the middle fossa: the sella turcica sits in the body of the sphenoid and holds the pituitary gland (parts: tuberculum sellae, hypophyseal fossa, dorsum sellae); the chiasmatic sulcus houses the optic chiasma; the carotid sulcus carries the internal carotid. (L01 · middle fossa / sella)
  • Draw the sphenoid arch: SOF → foramen rotundum → ovale → spinosum (rotundum is NOT seen inferiorly; ovale & spinosum are). Posterior fossa: clivus = inner basilar occiput; the S-shaped groove = sigmoid sinus. (L01 · sphenoid arch / posterior fossa)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Base of the skull is divided into 3 cranial fossae — anterior, middle, and posterior … floor is formed by orbital plate of frontal bone, cribriform plate of the ethmoid, and lesser wing of the sphenoid … all these four foramina, they make an arch — superior orbital fissure, foramen rotundum, ovale, foramen spinosum.”— Osteology of the Skull (L01) · cranial fossae
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · Skull Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which three plates form the anterior cranial fossa floor, and what sits in the sella turcica? Give the order of the sphenoid arch.
Anterior floor = frontal orbital plate + ethmoid cribriform plate + sphenoid lesser wing. The pituitary gland sits in the sella turcica (body of the sphenoid). Arch order: SOF → rotundum → ovale → spinosum.
8
Break down the sphenoid and ethmoid bones — their parts, and what each one does NOT include.
content: L01 / L02 · Sphenoid & ethmoid
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Split the sphenoid into its parts: lesser wing + greater wing + pterygoid process; the pterygoid process has a medial and a lateral plate; the sphenoid sinus lies under the sella turcica. (L01/L02 · sphenoid parts)
  • Track the ethmoid — “it’s everywhere”: cribriform plate (roof of nasal cavity) + perpendicular plate (upper nasal septum) + superior & middle conchae. (L01/L02 · ethmoid parts)
  • Guard the NOT: the ethmoid does not form the lower nasal septum (that is the vomer), and the inferior concha is its own separate bone. (L02 · ethmoid vs vomer/inferior concha)
  • Note the petrous temporal — the dense part of the temporal bone that houses the internal ear (ossifies by cartilage). (L01 · petrous temporal)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“If you look at the structure of sphenoid, there’s a lesser wing, greater wing, and pterygoid process … ethmoid is everywhere … it forms a roof of the nasal cavity … a lateral wall … and the superior part of your nasal septum, the perpendicular plate.”— Osteology of the Skull (L01) · sphenoid & ethmoid
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · Skull Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Name the three parts of the sphenoid. Which bone forms the LOWER nasal septum, and is the inferior concha part of the ethmoid?
Sphenoid = lesser wing + greater wing + pterygoid process (medial + lateral plates). The lower nasal septum = vomer (the ethmoid’s perpendicular plate is the upper part). The inferior concha is its OWN bone — only the superior & middle conchae are ethmoid.
9
Explain the paranasal sinuses & pneumatization — the four sinus bones, their function, and the mastoid air-cell exception.
content: L01 / L02 · Paranasal sinuses
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Name the four paranasal sinus bones: frontal, maxillary, ethmoid, sphenoid — they are lined by mucous membrane and communicate with the nasal cavity. (L01 · paranasal sinuses)
  • State the function: sinuses lighten the skull and add resonance to the voice. (L01/L02 · sinus function)
  • Flag the exception: mastoid air cells are closed — they do not communicate with any other cavity. (L01 · mastoid air cells)
  • Read the radiology: air appears black (radiolucent) on X-ray, so a sinus reads as a dark shadow. (L01 · air on X-ray)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Four bones, they have sinuses, namely frontal, maxillary, ethmoid, and sphenoid … these sinuses not only make the bones lighter, they add resonance to our voice … mastoid process also has air cells, but these air cells are not connected to any other cavity.”— Osteology of the Skull (L01) · pneumatization
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · Skull Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which four bones have paranasal sinuses, and how are the mastoid air cells different?
Paranasal sinuses = frontal, maxillary, ethmoid, sphenoid (they communicate with the nasal cavity, lighten the skull, add voice resonance). Mastoid air cells are closed — no communication with any cavity. Air = black on X-ray.
10
Explain skull ossification & development — the two ossification types, the embryonic origins, and the growth timeline.
content: L02 · Skull ossification & development
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Pair bone shape to ossification: flat bones (calvaria) = intramembranous ossification; irregular bones / skull base = endochondral (cartilaginous) ossification. (L02 · 00:12)
  • Give the embryonic origins: the chondrocranium (cartilaginous base) comes from neural crest + occipital somites; the viscerocranium (face) comes from the first two pharyngeal arches. (L02 · 00:09–00:10)
  • Explain why the base is dense: it is petrous / tough to support and protect the delicate brain; the fibrous sutures let the brain expand during infancy. (L02 · 00:22)
  • Give the timeline: the two frontal halves fuse by ~8 years; the calvaria keeps growing until ~16 years. (L02 · 00:17 & 00:23)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“All the flat bones, they are formed by membranous ossification; all the irregular bones are formed by cartilaginous [endochondral] ossification … the fibrous sutures of the calvaria, they allow for growth of the brain during infancy and childhood.”— Skull Development (L02) · ~00:12:58 & ~00:22:47
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · Skull Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Flat skull bones form by which ossification? Where does the facial skeleton (viscerocranium) come from developmentally?
Flat bones = intramembranous ossification; irregular/base bones = endochondral. The viscerocranium comes from the first two pharyngeal arches; the chondrocranium from neural crest + occipital somites. Frontal halves fuse ~8 yrs; calvaria grows to ~16 yrs.
11
Master the fontanelles — their shapes, the landmark each becomes, closure times, and what a sunken vs bulging fontanelle tells you.
content: L02 · Fontanelles
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Match each fontanelle to its future landmark: anterior = diamond-shaped → future bregma; posterior = triangular → future lambda; anterolateral → pterion; posterolateral (mastoid) → asterion. (L02 · 00:16–00:20)
  • Give the closure times: the anterior fontanelle closes by ~18 months (it is the last to close, so it is the one most palpated); the posterior closes within the first few months. (L02 · 00:16 & 00:19)
  • Read the clinical signs: a sunken/depressed anterior fontanelle = dehydration; a bulging one = raised intracranial pressure. (L02 · 00:15)
  • Interpret delayed closure = a sign of rickets / vitamin D or calcium deficiency (slow bone growth). (L02 · 00:16)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Anterior fontanelle is a diamond-shaped future site of bregma, closes by 18 months of age … posterior fontanelle, which is triangular … future site of lambda … if the baby is in severe dehydration, anterior fontanelle will be sunken; if there is increased intracranial pressure … this fontanelle will be bulging.”— Skull Development (L02) · ~00:15:02–00:19:05
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · Skull Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
The anterior fontanelle is what shape, becomes which landmark, and closes when? What does a bulging fontanelle mean?
Anterior fontanelle = diamond-shaped → future bregma, closes by ~18 months (last to close, most palpated). A bulging fontanelle = raised intracranial pressure; sunken = dehydration; delayed closure = rickets / vit-D deficiency. Posterior → lambda; anterolateral → pterion; posterolateral → asterion.
12
Recognize the developmental anomalies — molding, the craniosynostoses (scaphocephaly, plagiocephaly, oxycephaly), acrania, and microcephaly.
content: L02 · Developmental anomalies
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Explain molding: because the bones are not yet fused, they overlap to ease the head through the birth canal, then spring back. (L02 · 00:18–00:20)
  • Match the craniosynostoses (premature suture closure): scaphocephaly = boat-shaped head (“scapha = boat”); plagiocephaly = one-sided premature fusion; oxycephaly / turricephaly = coronal suture closes early. (L02 · 00:24–00:25)
  • Define acrania: the skull-cap bones fail to form, leaving the brain exposed (with meroanencephaly, incompatible with life). (L02 · 00:25–00:27)
  • Define microcephaly: an abnormally small head resulting from abnormal CNS / brain development (usually with severe intellectual impairment). (L02 · 00:26)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Scapha means a boat … the child has a boat-shaped head [scaphocephaly] … plagiocephaly is premature closure of coronal or lambdoid suture on one side only … acrania means missing skull bones … microcephaly … smaller head … a result of abnormal CNS development.”— Skull Development (L02) · ~00:24:14–00:26:24
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · Skull Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which anomaly is a boat-shaped head, and which one is a one-sided premature suture fusion? What is acrania?
Scaphocephaly = boat-shaped head; plagiocephaly = one-sided premature fusion; oxycephaly = early coronal-suture closure. Acrania = skull-cap bones fail to form (brain exposed). Microcephaly = small head from abnormal CNS development. Molding = unfused bones overlap at birth.
13
Describe the mandible — its regions, the TMJ process, and the path of the inferior alveolar nerve (with the lingula landmark).
content: L02 · The mandible
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Divide the mandible: body + ramus + angle, with an alveolar process holding the teeth and a central mental protuberance (chin). (L02 · 00:00–00:01)
  • Pick the TMJ process: the condylar process (head + neck) forms the temporomandibular joint (not the coronoid); the pterygoid fovea on it = lateral pterygoid muscle. (L02 · 00:01–00:02)
  • Trace the inferior alveolar nerve: enters the mandibular foramen (with the lingula as the nerve-block landmark) → runs through the body → exits the mental foramen as the mental nerve. (L02 · 00:02–00:05)
  • Place the inner-surface landmarks: mylohyoid line (mylohyoid muscle = floor of the mouth) + groove (mylohyoid nerve), sublingual fossa (sublingual gland), submandibular fossa (submandibular gland), mental spine / genial tubercle, and the digastric fossa (anterior belly of digastric). (L02 · 00:05–00:08)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“We divide mandible into the body, the ramus, and then angle … which of the two processes takes part in the formation of temporomandibular joint? Condyl[ar] … inferior alveolar nerve … passes through mandibular foramen … and exits through the mental foramen … your dentist is going to palpate for lingula … so it serves as a landmark.”— The Mandible (L02) · ~00:00:13–00:04:29
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · Skull Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which mandibular process forms the TMJ, and what is the lingula the landmark for? Where does the inferior alveolar nerve exit?
The condylar process (head + neck) forms the TMJ. The lingula is the landmark for the inferior alveolar nerve block at the mandibular foramen. The nerve exits the mental foramen as the mental nerve. Mandible = body + ramus + angle.
14
Apply the clinical correlations — pterion fracture & epidural hemorrhage, the glabellar tap sign, and the trans-sphenoidal approach.
content: L01 / L02 · Clinical correlations
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Trace the pterion emergency: a hard blow to the side of the skull fractures the thin pterion → ruptures the middle meningeal artery → epidural (extradural) hemorrhage — the classic boxing-injury bleed. (L01 · pterion clinical)
  • Perform the glabellar tap sign: tap the glabella while the patient tries not to blink — persistent blinking suggests Parkinsonism. (L01 · glabellar tap)
  • State Maini’s clinical rule: never diagnose on a single finding — correlate clinically and investigate further. (L01 · glabellar tap)
  • Explain the trans-sphenoidal approach: because the sella turcica sits on top of the sphenoid sinus, a pituitary tumor can be reached through the nose / sphenoid sinus instead of opening the skull. (L02 · 01:00)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“If there is a hard blow to the lateral side of the skull, and if [pterion] is fractured, there is rupture of middle meningeal artery, leading to … epidural hemorrhage … glabellar tap sign … if they still blink, it can be a sign of Parkinsonism … never rely on a single clinical finding to diagnose.”— Osteology of the Skull (L01) · clinical correlations
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · Skull Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
A blow to the pterion ruptures which artery, causing which type of hemorrhage? What does a positive glabellar tap suggest?
A pterion fracture ruptures the middle meningeal artery → epidural (extradural) hemorrhage. A positive glabellar tap (persistent blinking) suggests Parkinsonism — but never diagnose on one finding. The trans-sphenoidal approach reaches the pituitary through the sphenoid sinus.
