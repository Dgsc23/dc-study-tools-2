1
Delineate the temporal fossa — its four boundaries, its floor, what fills it, and the vessels that cross it.
content: L10 · Temporal fossa
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Give the boundaries: superior & posterior = the temporal lines (which sit on the parietal bone); anterior = frontal & zygomatic bones; lateral = the zygomatic arch (making it a shallow pocket); inferior = the infratemporal crest. (L10 · boundaries)
  • State the floor: the same four bones that meet at the pterion (frontal, parietal, temporal, greater wing of sphenoid). (L10 · floor / pterion)
  • Name the contents: the fossa is almost entirely occupied by the temporalis muscle (under the temporal fascia). (L10 · contents)
  • Track the vessels: the superficial temporal artery is one of the two terminal branches of the external carotid; the superficial temporal vein unites with the maxillary vein to form the retromandibular vein. (L10 · superficial temporal vessels)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Which bone has temporal lines? … parietal, right? … Superficial temporal artery is one of the two terminal branches of … external carotid artery, good.”— Temporal & Infratemporal Fossa (L10) · ~00:05:46
🎯 Cement it
🧩 Drill it · Everything-MC
🧪 Quick self-check
Which bone bears the temporal lines, what muscle fills the fossa, and where do its four floor-bones meet?
The temporal lines are on the parietal bone; the temporalis fills the fossa; the four floor-bones meet at the pterion. The lateral wall is the zygomatic arch, the inferior boundary is the infratemporal crest, and the superficial temporal artery is a terminal branch of the external carotid.
2
Map the boundaries and contents of the infratemporal fossa — the six walls and what lives inside.
content: L10–L11 · Infratemporal fossa
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Give the walls: roof = greater wing of the sphenoid (NOT the zygomatic arch); lateral = ramus of the mandible; medial = lateral pterygoid plate; anterior = posterior surface of the maxilla; posterior = styloid + mastoid process + tympanic plate; floor = the medial pterygoid muscle. (L10–L11 · boundaries)
  • List the contents: the two pterygoid muscles, the maxillary artery + pterygoid venous plexus, the mandibular nerve (V3) and its branches, the chorda tympani, and the otic ganglion. (L10–L11 · contents)
  • Flag the trap: the parotid gland is NOT a content of the fossa, and the chorda tympani is a branch of the facial nerve (CN VII), not V3. (L10–L11 · chorda tympani)

🎯 Cement it
🧩 Drill it · Everything-MC
🧪 Quick self-check
What forms the roof vs the floor of the infratemporal fossa, and is the chorda tympani a branch of V3?
Roof = greater wing of the sphenoid; floor = medial pterygoid muscle. The chorda tympani is a branch of the facial nerve (VII), not V3. Lateral wall = ramus of mandible; medial = lateral pterygoid plate; anterior = posterior maxilla; and the parotid gland is not a content.
3
Identify the four muscles of mastication — their common arch and nerve, and each muscle’s attachments.
content: L10 · Muscles of mastication
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State the shared origin: all four develop from the first pharyngeal arch and are innervated by the mandibular nerve (V3). (L10 · overview)
  • Temporalis: from the temporal fossa → inserts on the coronoid process & anterior border of the ramus. (L10 · temporalis)
  • Masseter: from the zygomatic arch + maxillary process of the zygomatic bone → inserts on the angle & lateral surface of the ramus. (L10 · masseter)
  • Medial pterygoid: from the medial surface of the lateral pterygoid plate, pyramidal process of the palatine, & tuberosity of the maxilla. Lateral pterygoid: from the infratemporal surface/greater wing of the sphenoid + the LATERAL surface of the lateral pterygoid plate → inserts on the pterygoid fovea plus the joint capsule & articular disc of the TMJ. (L10 · pterygoids)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“V3, muscles of mastication, first pharyngeal arch. … This muscle [lateral pterygoid], it originates from lateral surface of pterygoid plate, and then it moves forward … inserted into the joint capsule and articular disc.”— Temporal & Infratemporal Fossa (L10) · ~00:33:32, ~00:39:30
🎯 Cement it
🧩 Drill it · Everything-MC
🧪 Quick self-check
From which arch and nerve do all four muscles of mastication come, and where does the lateral pterygoid uniquely insert?
All four are first-pharyngeal-arch muscles innervated by V3. The lateral pterygoid inserts on the pterygoid fovea of the condyle plus the joint capsule and articular disc of the TMJ — the reason it can pull the disc forward.
4
Apply Maini’s four movement rules — which muscles elevate, depress, protrude, and retract the mandible.
content: L10–L11 · Muscle actions
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Elevation: temporalis, masseter, medial pterygoid — but NOT the lateral pterygoid. (L10 · elevation)
  • Depression: the lateral pterygoid is the sole depressor. Retraction: the temporalis is the only retractor (its horizontal posterior fibers; the vertical anterior fibers elevate). (L10–L11 · depression / retraction)
  • Protrusion (protraction): lateral pterygoid, medial pterygoid, masseter — but NOT the temporalis, because it is a retractor. (L10 · protrusion)
  • Use Maini’s pairings: the masseter & medial pterygoid run parallel, so they share the same two actions — elevate + protract; and by her chair analogy, the lateral pterygoid pulling the head forward while the body drops gives protraction with depression. (L10–L11 · pairings)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Temporalis is the retractor. Only retractor … Lateral pterygoid is the sole depressor … Temporalis is not a protruder, because it is what? It is a retractor. … Masseter and medial pterygoid, they only elevate and protract.”— Temporal & Infratemporal Fossa (L10–L11) · muscle actions
🎯 Cement it
🧩 Drill it · Everything-MC
🧪 Quick self-check
Which single muscle is the sole depressor of the mandible, and which is the only retractor?
The lateral pterygoid is the sole depressor; the temporalis is the only retractor. Because the temporalis retracts, it is not a protruder — the protruders are lateral pterygoid, medial pterygoid, and masseter.
5
Describe the structure of the TMJ — joint type, stability rank, articulating surfaces, disc, tubercle, ligaments, and supply.
content: L10–L11 · TMJ structure
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Classify it: a synovial joint, one of the most mobile and least stable in the body — second only to the shoulder (glenohumeral). (L10 · ~00:16:02)
  • Name the surfaces: the mandibular fossa of the temporal bone + the head of the condylar process, with an articular disc that divides the cavity into a superior and inferior compartment; the articular tubercle just anterior limits anterior dislocation of the head. (L10 · disc / tubercle)
  • Give the two extrinsic ligaments: sphenomandibular (spine of sphenoid → lingula) and stylomandibular (styloid process → angle of the mandible). (L11 · ligaments)
  • State supply: synovial fluid lubricates AND nourishes the avascular hyaline cartilage; sensation is mainly the auriculotemporal nerve (plus masseteric & deep temporal); blood from the maxillary + superficial temporal arteries. (L11 · supply)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“This joint also is one of the most mobile and least stable joint in our body, second to shoulder joint … Sphenomandibular ligament … it is lingula. Because lingula is more internal. … Auriculotemporal … is the best choice, good.”— Temporal & Infratemporal Fossa (L10–L11) · ~00:16:02 & TMJ
🎯 Cement it
🧩 Drill it · Everything-MC
🧪 Quick self-check
The TMJ is second in mobility to which joint, and where does the sphenomandibular ligament attach on the mandible?
Second only to the shoulder (glenohumeral) joint. The sphenomandibular ligament runs from the spine of the sphenoid to the lingula (the stylomandibular runs to the angle). The articular disc splits the cavity into superior + inferior compartments; innervation is the auriculotemporal nerve.
6
State the movements of the TMJ and Maini’s flexion/extension convention — including lateral deviation.
content: L10 · TMJ movements
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • List the movements: gliding (protrusion/retraction), rotation, and elevation/depression. (L10 · movement repertoire)
  • Use her convention: at the TMJ, flexion = elevation and extension = depression (used interchangeably) — flexion decreases the joint angle, extension increases it. (L10 · ~00:18:35)
  • Work lateral deviation: when the mandible deviates to the right, the left condyle glides forward (protracts). (L10 · lateral deviation)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“There is flexion. Flexion is elevation … flexion and elevation, when it comes to TMJ … they are used interchangeably … angle decreases, that is flexion. When angle increases, that is extension.”— Temporal & Infratemporal Fossa (L10) · ~00:18:35
🎯 Cement it
🧩 Drill it · Everything-MC
🧪 Quick self-check
At the TMJ, flexion corresponds to which movement — and if the LEFT condyle protracts, which way does the jaw deviate?
Flexion = elevation (extension = depression). If the left condyle glides forward (protracts), the jaw deviates to the right. The three movement families are gliding, rotation, and elevation/depression.
7
Trace the maxillary artery and its key branches — and the middle-meningeal / pterion bleed.
content: L11 · Maxillary artery
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Place its origin: the maxillary artery is the LARGER of the two terminal branches of the external carotid, arising within the parotid gland behind the neck of the mandible. (L11 · ~00:03:45)
  • Flag the clinical branch: the middle meningeal artery enters the cranium via the foramen spinosum to supply the dura; rupture — classically a pterion fracture — gives an extradural (epidural) hemorrhage. (L11 · middle meningeal)
  • Add the other named branches: the inferior alveolar (gives a mylohyoid branch before the mandibular foramen), the deep auricular & anterior tympanic (to the ear), and the sphenopalatine (medially into the nasal cavity). (L11 · branches)
  • Finish the course: it terminates as the infraorbital artery and leaves the fossa to enter the pterygopalatine fossa through the pterygomaxillary fissure. (L11 · ~00:52:16)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Larger of the two terminal branches … very frequently asked question is, which one of the following is terminal branch of maxillary artery? That is infraorbital. Which artery passes from pterygopalatine fossa to the nasal cavity is [s]phenopalatine.”— Temporal & Infratemporal Fossa (L11) · ~00:03:45 & ~00:52:16
🎯 Cement it
🧩 Drill it · Everything-MC
🧪 Quick self-check
What is the terminal branch of the maxillary artery, and which branch bleeds in an extradural hematoma?
The terminal branch is the infraorbital artery. The middle meningeal artery (through the foramen spinosum, torn at the pterion) causes an extradural / epidural hemorrhage. The maxillary artery itself is the larger terminal branch of the external carotid.
8
Explain the pterygoid venous plexus — where it sits, where it drains, and why it spreads infection.
content: L11 · Pterygoid venous plexus
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Locate it: a venous network between the temporalis and pterygoid muscles, surrounding the maxillary artery. (L11 · location)
  • State its drainage: it drains posteriorly into the maxillary vein. (L11 · drainage)
  • Give the danger: it communicates with the cavernous sinus and with the facial vein, so it is a route for infection to spread from the face to the cavernous sinus. (L11 · ~00:13:07)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Cavernous sinus on one side, and with facial vein … a via media for spread of infections from the facial region to the cavernous sinus, right?”— Temporal & Infratemporal Fossa (L11) · ~00:13:07
🎯 Cement it
🧩 Drill it · Everything-MC
🧪 Quick self-check
Into which vein does the pterygoid venous plexus drain, and why is it clinically dangerous?
It drains into the maxillary vein. Because it connects to the cavernous sinus and the facial vein, a facial infection can travel through it to the cavernous sinus.
9
Dissect the mandibular nerve (V3) — its exit, its branches by division, and the auriculotemporal signature.
content: L11 · Mandibular nerve (V3)
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Give its exit and nature: V3 leaves the skull through the foramen ovale as a mixed (sensory + motor) nerve, then descends between the tensor veli palatini (medial) and the lateral pterygoid (lateral). (L11 · ~00:14 / foramen ovale)
  • Read the main trunk: the meningeal branch + the nerve to medial pterygoid. (L11 · trunk)
  • Split the divisions: the anterior division is largely motor, with the buccal nerve as its one sensory branch; the posterior division gives the inferior alveolar (continues as the mental nerve at the mental foramen), the lingual, and the auriculotemporal. (L11 · divisions)
  • Nail the auriculotemporal signature: it has two roots pierced by the middle meningeal artery — the “nerve with a passage” on a diagram. (L11 · auriculotemporal)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“V3 passes through foramen ovale … V2 passes through foramen rotundum. Rotundum means round. … Anytime you look at a nerve which has a passage … it’s auriculotemporal, because it is pierced by middle meningeal artery.”— Temporal & Infratemporal Fossa (L11) · V3 branches
🎯 Cement it
🧩 Drill it · Everything-MC
🧪 Quick self-check
Through which foramen does V3 exit, and what identifies the auriculotemporal nerve on a diagram?
V3 exits the foramen ovale. The auriculotemporal nerve is the one with two roots pierced by the middle meningeal artery. Its anterior division is mostly motor (buccal = the one sensory branch); the posterior division carries the inferior alveolar, lingual, and auriculotemporal.
10
Follow the chorda tympani + lingual nerve to the submandibular ganglion — the taste-and-saliva pathway.
content: L11 · Lingual nerve & chorda tympani
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Start with the lingual nerve (a branch of V3): before it is joined, it carries general sensation (GSA) from the anterior two-thirds of the tongue. (L11 · lingual GSA)
  • Add the chorda tympani: a branch of the facial nerve (VII) that reaches the lingual nerve through the petrotympanic fissure, carrying taste (SVA) from the anterior two-thirds + preganglionic parasympathetic fibers. (L11 · chorda tympani)
  • Route the parasympathetics: those preganglionic fibers synapse in the submandibular ganglion; the postganglionic fibers then supply the submandibular and sublingual salivary glands. (L11 · submandibular ganglion)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Is chorda tympani a branch of V3 or not? … No, it’s a branch of facial, good.”— Temporal & Infratemporal Fossa (L10–L11) · chorda tympani
🎯 Cement it
🧩 Drill it · Everything-MC
🧪 Quick self-check
The chorda tympani carries which two fiber types, and its parasympathetics relay in which ganglion?
Taste (SVA) from the anterior two-thirds of the tongue + preganglionic parasympathetic fibers. They relay in the submandibular ganglion, whose postganglionic fibers supply the submandibular + sublingual glands. The lingual nerve alone carries GSA from the anterior two-thirds.
11
Trace the otic-ganglion pathway to the parotid gland — CN IX in, auriculotemporal out.
content: L10–L11 · Otic ganglion
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Place it and its target: the otic ganglion lies deep (medial) to V3, just below the foramen ovale, and is secretomotor to the parotid gland (Maini’s rhyme: parotid / otic). (L10 · ~00:14:50)
  • Give the input: preganglionic fibers from the glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX), with cell bodies in the inferior salivatory nucleus of the medulla. (L10 · ~00:15:05, L11 · ~00:26:01)
  • Trace the relay: tympanic nerve (IX) → tympanic plexus → lesser petrosal nerve → otic ganglion. (L11 · lesser petrosal)
  • Finish the ride: the postganglionic fibers hitch-hike on the auriculotemporal nerve to reach the parotid. (L11 · otic → auriculotemporal)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“parotid gland, otic ganglion … Ninth nerve carries preganglionic parasympathetic fibers to this ganglion. Postganglionic parasympathetic fibers, they go to parotid gland.”— Temporal & Infratemporal Fossa (L10) · ~00:14:50–00:15:05
🎯 Cement it
🧩 Drill it · Everything-MC
🧪 Quick self-check
The otic ganglion’s preganglionic fibers come from which nerve, and its postganglionic fibers ride which nerve to the parotid?
Preganglionic fibers come from CN IX (glossopharyngeal) via the lesser petrosal nerve (cell bodies in the inferior salivatory nucleus). Postganglionic fibers hitch-hike on the auriculotemporal nerve to the parotid gland.
12
Command the COPS mnemonic — the four parasympathetic ganglia of the head, their nerves, and the pterygoid-canal trap.
content: L11 · Parasympathetic ganglia (COPS)
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Spell Maini’s mnemonic: COPS = Ciliary, Otic, Pterygopalatine, Submandibular — the four parasympathetic (cranial) ganglia of the head/neck. (L11 · ~00:25:01)
  • Assign the nerves: Ciliary ← CN III; Otic ← CN IX; Pterygopalatine & Submandibular ← CN VII. (The head/neck/body preganglionic outflow rides CN III, VII, IX, and X.) (L11 · COPS nerves)
  • Dodge the trap: the trigeminal (semilunar) ganglion is NOT a COPS ganglion — it is a sensory ganglion; and the deep petrosal nerve is sympathetic (from the internal carotid plexus), NOT a branch of VII. (L11 · ~00:42:27)
  • Assemble the pterygoid canal: the nerve of the pterygoid canal = greater petrosal (parasympathetic, from VII) + deep petrosal (sympathetic), and the pterygopalatine ganglion supplies the lacrimal, nasal, and palatine glands. (L11 · pterygoid canal)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“there are 4 parasympathetic ganglia in the head and neck region … a [mnemonic] corpse, right? C, O, P, and S. … C is ciliary. Ciliary ganglion is associated with third nerve.”— Temporal & Infratemporal Fossa (L11) · ~00:25:01
🎯 Cement it
🧩 Drill it · Everything-MC
🧪 Quick self-check
What does COPS stand for, and which cranial nerve supplies the ciliary vs the otic ganglion?
Ciliary, Otic, Pterygopalatine, Submandibular. Ciliary ← CN III; otic ← CN IX; pterygopalatine & submandibular ← CN VII. The deep petrosal nerve is sympathetic (not a branch of VII), and the trigeminal ganglion is a sensory ganglion.
13
Situate the pterygopalatine fossa — its boundaries, its three communications, and its contents.
content: L11 · Pterygopalatine fossa
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Locate it: between the pterygoid process of the sphenoid (posterior) and the posterior surface of the maxilla (anterior); roof = greater wing of the sphenoid; floor = pyramidal process of the palatine bone. (L11 · ~00:45:14)
  • Give the three communications: laterally with the infratemporal fossa via the pterygomaxillary fissure; medially with the nasal cavity via the sphenopalatine foramen; posteriorly with the middle cranial fossa via the foramen rotundum (which transmits V2). (L11 · ~00:46:38)
  • List the contents: the pterygopalatine ganglion, the maxillary nerve (V2), and the terminal (third) part of the maxillary artery. (L11 · ~00:48:12)
  • Add V2’s continuations: V2 continues as the infraorbital nerve (through the infraorbital foramen), and its zygomatic nerve divides into zygomaticofacial + zygomaticotemporal. (L11 · V2 branches)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“posteriorly, with middle cranial fossa through foramen rotundum … So, V2 passes through forum[en] rotundum. Rotundum means round. … Medially, with the nasal cavity, through sphenopalatine foramen.”— Temporal & Infratemporal Fossa (L11) · ~00:46:10–00:54:49
🎯 Cement it
🧩 Drill it · Everything-MC
🧪 Quick self-check
The pterygopalatine fossa talks to the nasal cavity through which opening, and to the middle cranial fossa through which foramen?
To the nasal cavity via the sphenopalatine foramen (where the sphenopalatine artery passes); to the middle cranial fossa via the foramen rotundum, which transmits V2. Its contents are the pterygopalatine ganglion, V2, and the terminal maxillary artery.

🔗 How to use these 13 to study

1. Read each objective out loud, then try the self-check before revealing the answer. If you nailed it, tick Got it.

2. For any objective you miss, jump into the Everything-MC drill and grind exactly that topic.

3. The parasympathetic story (Objectives 10–12 — chorda tympani, otic ganglion, and COPS) is where Maini’s “which nerve carries which fibers to which gland” questions live. Spend extra reps there.

⚠️ Don’t mix up the “which nerve?” traps. The chorda tympani is a branch of the facial nerve (VII), not V3; the deep petrosal is sympathetic, not a branch of VII; the otic ganglion takes CN IX in and sends postganglionics out on the auriculotemporal to the parotid; and the auriculotemporal is the nerve pierced by the middle meningeal artery. On the muscles: the lateral pterygoid is the sole depressor and the temporalis is the only retractor.
