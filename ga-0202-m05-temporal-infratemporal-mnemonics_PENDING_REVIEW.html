Topic 1 — The two fossae & their walls

Read the boundary, name the wall. These are the lists Maini quizzes cold in class.
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Temporal fossa boundaries
🗺️ UP/BACK = LINES · FRONT = FRONTAL+ZYGO · SIDE = ARCH · DOWN = CREST
The temporal fossa boundaries: superior & posterior = the temporal lines; anterior = frontal + zygomatic bones; lateral = zygomatic arch (a shallow pocket); inferior = infratemporal crest. Its floor = the four bones of pterion. Almost the entire fossa is filled by the temporalis muscle.
💡 Walk the compass: lines up-top-and-back, the two face bones out front, the cheek-arch on the side, the crest underneath. Trivia hook: the same four bones that meet at pterion pave the floor.
L10 · temporal fossa boundaries & floor (Bank Q1–Q5)
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Which bone has the temporal lines?
🦴 TEMPORAL LINES = PARIETAL (not temporal)
A favourite trap: the temporal lines are on the PARIETAL bone, NOT the temporal bone. They form the superior/posterior boundary of the fossa and the origin of temporalis.
“Which bone has temporal lines? … parietal, right?”🎓 Dr. Maini, Lecture 10
L10 · temporal fossa boundaries (Bank Q1)
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Infratemporal fossa — the 6 walls
🧱 LAT=RAMUS · MED=PLATE · ANT=MAXILLA · POST=STYLOID · ROOF=SPHENOID · FLOOR=MEDIAL PTERYGOID
Lateral = ramus of the mandible; medial = lateral pterygoid plate; anterior = posterior surface of the maxilla; posterior = tympanic plate + mastoid + styloid processes; roof/superior = greater wing of the sphenoid; floor = medial pterygoid muscle.
💡 Picture a shoebox behind the cheek: jaw-plate = outer wall, pterygoid plate = inner wall, maxilla in front, styloid/mastoid behind, sphenoid as the lid, and the medial pterygoid muscle as the rug on the floor.
L10–L11 · ITF boundaries (Bank Q8–Q13)
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Roof-of-ITF trap
🎩 ROOF = GREATER WING OF SPHENOID (not the arch)
On her live in-class quiz Maini flags the classic wrong pick: the roof of the infratemporal fossa is the greater wing of the sphenoid; the zygomatic arch is NOT the roof. The floor is the medial pterygoid muscle.
“Greater wing of the sphenoid is the right answer. Zygomatic arch cannot be the roof.”🎓 Dr. Maini, Lecture 11 (quiz)
L11 · Maini quiz (Bank Q8, Q13)
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Infratemporal fossa contents
📦 INSIDE: 2 MUSCLES · MAX ARTERY + PLEXUS · V3 · OTIC GANGLION
Contents: inferior temporalis + both pterygoid muscles; the maxillary artery & branches; the pterygoid venous plexus; the mandibular nerve (V3) & branches; the otic ganglion; and chorda tympani. The parotid gland is NOT a content (a tested distractor).
💡 A crowded junction box: two chewing muscles, an artery with its venous net wrapped around it, the whole V3 nerve tree, and a tiny relay station (otic ganglion). The parotid stays outside the box.
L10 · ITF contents (Bank Q14–Q15)

Topic 2 — Muscles of mastication: the actions

Everything about the four muscles is identical — except the actions. Maini: learn the table, memorise the two exceptions.
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
The study strategy
📋 ALL 4 ARE THE SAME — MEMORISE 2 EXCEPTIONS
All four (temporalis, masseter, medial pterygoid, lateral pterygoid) share one story: muscles of mastication, first pharyngeal arch, motor from V3. The ONLY thing that trips you is the actions — and only two of those are exceptions.
“Write down these muscles, and write the names of the movements, because everything else about these muscles is easy … If you remember two exceptions, you’ll be good.”🎓 Dr. Maini, Lecture 10
L10 · muscles of mastication overview (Bank Q18)
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Exception #1 — temporalis
⬅️ TEMPORALIS = THE ONLY RETRACTOR
Temporalis is the sole retractor of the mandible: its horizontal posterior fibers pull the jaw back, while its vertical anterior fibers elevate. Because it retracts, it is NOT a protruder.
“Temporalis is the retractor. Only retractor.” … “Temporalis is not a protruder, because it is what? It is a retractor.”🎓 Dr. Maini, Lectures 10–11
L10–L11 · Bank Q19, Q22, Q23
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Exception #2 — lateral pterygoid
⬇️ LATERAL PTERYGOID = THE ONLY DEPRESSOR
Lateral pterygoid is the sole depressor (it opens the jaw) and also protracts. Its horizontal fibers pull the condylar head forward. Because it depresses, it is NOT an elevator.
“Lateral pterygoid is the sole depressor.”🎓 Dr. Maini, Lectures 10–11
L10–L11 · Bank Q20, Q21
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Why lateral pterygoid opens the jaw
🪑 THE CHAIR: PULL THE BACK, THE SEAT DROPS
Maini’s own picture for WHY the lateral pterygoid depresses: it pulls only the head (condyle) forward, so the rest of the mandible (the body) swings down. Protraction of the head = depression of the jaw.
“If you pull the chair from the back, the back will move forward, but the seat will go downwards. That is how lateral pterygoid causes depression.”🎓 Dr. Maini, Lecture 10
L10 · chair analogy (Bank Q30)
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
The parallel pair
🟡 MASSETER = MEDIAL PTERYGOID (parallel fibers)
Masseter (outside the ramus) and medial pterygoid (inside the ramus) run parallel, so they share the same two actions: elevation + protraction. Neither depresses. (Masseter is one of the body’s strongest muscles.)
“Masseter and medial pterygoid, they only elevate and protract.”🎓 Dr. Maini, Lecture 11
L10–L11 · Bank Q25, Q26
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Elevators vs the odd one out
⬆️ ELEVATE = TEMPORALIS + MASSETER + MEDIAL (not lateral)
Elevation (closing / biting) = temporalis, masseter, medial pterygoid. Protrusion = lateral pterygoid, medial pterygoid, masseter (NOT temporalis). Cross-check against the two exceptions and every action question falls out.
💡 “Which does NOT elevate?” = the depressor (lateral pterygoid). “Which does NOT protrude?” = the retractor (temporalis). The two exceptions are exactly the two trap answers.
L10 · muscle actions (Bank Q21, Q22)

Topic 3 — TMJ: structure, movements & ligaments

A synovial joint, second only to the shoulder for mobility. Position the ligament, name the motion.
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Flexion / extension terminology
🔁 FLEXION = ELEVATE · EXTENSION = DEPRESS
In Maini’s TMJ vocabulary, flexion = elevation (closing) and extension = depression (opening). When a question mixes the terms, translate first: prime extensor = the depressor = lateral pterygoid.
“Depression means extension … elevation means flexion.”🎓 Dr. Maini, Lecture 11
L10–L11 · TMJ terminology (Bank Q32)
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Side-to-side jaw movement
🚪 THE DOOR: MOVE RIGHT, THE LEFT SWINGS FORWARD
For lateral (side-to-side) jaw movement: to deviate the jaw to the RIGHT, the LEFT condyle glides forward (protracts) while the right side rotates. Contralateral protraction drives the deviation.
“Like a door … the side you’re trying to move, the opposite side moves in the opposite direction.”🎓 Dr. Maini, Lecture 10
L10 · lateral jaw movement (Bank Q31)
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
The two extrinsic ligaments
🧿 SPHENO → LINGULA (inner) · STYLO → ANGLE (outer)
The TMJ’s two extrinsic ligaments: sphenomandibular (spine of sphenoid to the lingula) and stylomandibular (styloid process to the angle of the mandible). Maini’s logic hook: the internal origin (sphenoid) goes to the internal landmark (lingula); the external origin (styloid) goes to the external landmark (angle).
“Sphenoid is more internal, so … lingula, because lingula is more internal. Stylomandibular goes to the angle of the mandible, outside.”🎓 Dr. Maini, Lecture 11
L10–L11 · TMJ ligaments (Bank Q39–Q41)
🧩 Study aid (mine)
TMJ quick facts
🦴 SYNOVIAL · 2nd-MOST-MOBILE · DISC = 2 CHAMBERS
TMJ = synovial joint between the mandibular fossa (temporal bone) and the head of the condylar process. One of the most mobile / least stable joints — second only to the shoulder. Its articular disc divides the cavity into superior + inferior compartments; the articular tubercle limits anterior dislocation.
💡 Think of it as the shoulder’s little cousin: hugely mobile, poorly stable, and the only other joint that protracts/retracts. The disc is a double-decker divider inside the capsule.
L10 · TMJ structure (Bank Q33–Q38)
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Nerve to the TMJ
👂 TMJ SENSATION = AURICULOTEMPORAL
Best single answer for TMJ innervation: the auriculotemporal nerve (with the masseteric & deep temporal nerves also supplying the capsule). Distractor traps: buccal (cheek), lingual (tongue), chorda tympani (taste).
“Auriculotemporal … is the best choice, good.”🎓 Dr. Maini, Lecture 11 (quiz)
L10–L11 · TMJ innervation (Bank Q43, Q44)

Topic 4 — Maxillary artery: the branch lists

The larger terminal branch of the external carotid. Two rooms of branches — the fossa, and the fissure-beyond.
🧩 Study aid (mine)
The whole course
🩸 MAX ARTERY: PAROTID → ITF → PPF → INFRAORBITAL
The maxillary artery is the larger of the two terminal branches of the external carotid (the other = superficial temporal). It arises in the parotid gland, crosses the infratemporal fossa, leaves through the pterygomaxillary fissure into the pterygopalatine fossa, and ends as the infraorbital artery.
💡 Follow one river through three rooms: born in the parotid, running the fossa, squeezing the fissure, ending at the eye-socket floor (infraorbital).
L11 · maxillary artery course (Bank Q46–Q49)
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
The two one-liners she asks
🎯 TERMINAL = INFRAORBITAL · TO-NOSE = SPHENOPALATINE
Two frequently-asked one-liners: the terminal branch of the maxillary artery is the infraorbital artery; the branch that leaves the pterygopalatine fossa medially into the nasal cavity is the sphenopalatine artery.
“Which one of the following is the terminal branch of maxillary artery? That is infraorbital. … Which artery passes from pterygopalatine fossa to the nasal cavity is sphenopalatine.”🎓 Dr. Maini, Lecture 11
L11 · Bank Q49, Q54
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Branches given off IN the fossa
🏠 IN THE FOSSA: EAR-PAIR · MIDDLE MENINGEAL · INF. ALVEOLAR · MUSCULARS
Branches given off inside the infratemporal fossa: the ear pair (deep auricular + anterior tympanic), middle meningeal, inferior alveolar, plus the muscular branches (masseteric, deep temporal, pterygoid) and the buccal.
💡 Group by destination: two to the ear, one up to the dura (middle meningeal), one down into the jaw (inferior alveolar), and the rest to the chewing muscles and cheek.
L11 · ITF branches (Bank Q50, Q52, Q53)
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Middle meningeal — the clinical one
🧠 MIDDLE MENINGEAL → SPINOSUM → DURA (pterion bleed)
The middle meningeal artery enters the cranium through the foramen spinosum to supply the dura mater. It is the classic ruptured artery after a pterion fracture, causing an extradural (epidural) hemorrhage. It also pierces the auriculotemporal nerve (see Topic 5).
💡 Spinosum for the little “spiny” hole; a blow to the thin pterion snaps it and blood pools outside the dura (extra-dural).
L11 · middle meningeal clinical (Bank Q50, Q51)
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Branches in the pterygopalatine fossa
5️⃣ BACK ROOM: PSA · INFRAORBITAL · DESC. PALATINE · SPHENOPALATINE · PTERYGOID-CANAL
Within the pterygopalatine fossa the maxillary artery gives off five branches: posterior superior alveolar, infraorbital (terminal), descending palatine, sphenopalatine (to the nose), and the artery of the pterygoid canal.
💡 Five exits from the tiny back room: to the back teeth (PSA), to the eye (infraorbital), down to the palate (descending palatine), into the nose (sphenopalatine), and straight back through the pterygoid canal.
L11 · PPF branches, ~51–52 min (Bank Q54)

Topic 5 — Mandibular nerve (V3): the branch tree

V3 through the oval hole. Trunk, then anterior (mostly motor) and posterior (mostly sensory).
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
The foramen clue
⭕ ROTUNDUM = ROUND (V2) · OVALE = OVAL (V3)
Maini’s spelling clue for the two divisions: V2 (maxillary) exits foramen ROTUNDUM (round); V3 (mandibular) exits foramen OVALE (oval). V3 is a mixed nerve (sensory + motor).
“V3 passes through foramen ovale … V2 passes through foramen rotundum. Rotundum means round … you can use this as a clue.”🎓 Dr. Maini, Lecture 11
L11 · V3 foramen (Bank Q58, Q59, Q90)
🧩 Study aid (mine)
The V3 tree, at a glance
🌳 TRUNK → ANTERIOR (motor) · POSTERIOR (sensory)
After foramen ovale, V3 splits. Trunk: meningeal branch + nerve to medial pterygoid. Anterior division = mostly motor (masseteric, deep temporal, nerve to lateral pterygoid) with ONE sensory branch, the buccal. Posterior division = mostly sensory: auriculotemporal, lingual, inferior alveolar (whose nerve to mylohyoid is the motor exception).
💡 Two branches, two personalities: the front branch feeds the chewing muscles (motor) and sneaks in one sensory buccal; the back branch is the sensory branch (ear, tongue, teeth) hiding one motor twig to mylohyoid.
L11 · V3 divisions (Bank Q60–Q63)
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Spot the auriculotemporal nerve
👂 AURICULOTEMPORAL “HAS AN EAR” (buttonhole for the MMA)
Identify the auriculotemporal nerve by its buttonhole: it splits into two roots that the middle meningeal artery passes through. “Auricle” = ear, so Maini says to imagine the nerve literally has an ear-hole for the artery. It also relays parotid secretomotor fibers from the otic ganglion.
“This nerve has an ear … you can think it has an ear. It has a passage for middle meningeal artery … anytime you look at a nerve which has a passage, you’re going to be sure it’s auriculotemporal.”🎓 Dr. Maini, Lecture 11
L11 · auriculotemporal ID (Bank Q64, Q66)
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Posterior division + the mental nerve
🦷 POSTERIOR 3: AURICULOTEMPORAL · LINGUAL · INF. ALVEOLAR → MENTAL
The posterior division’s three named branches: auriculotemporal, lingual, inferior alveolar. The inferior alveolar enters the mandibular foramen (giving the nerve to mylohyoid just before) and exits the mental foramen as the mental nerve, supplying the chin and lower lip.
💡 The back-branch trio hits ear, tongue and teeth. The teeth-nerve tunnels through the jaw and pops out at the chin as the “mental” nerve.
L10–L11 · posterior division (Bank Q16, Q17, Q63, Q65)

Topic 6 — Ganglia, chorda tympani & the tongue

Four parasympathetic relay stations. Maini’s COPS. Match each to its cranial nerve.
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
The four parasympathetic ganglia
👮 COPS = CILIARY · OTIC · PTERYGOPALATINE · SUBMANDIBULAR
Maini’s own mnemonic for the four parasympathetic ganglia of the head: COPS = Ciliary, Otic, Pterygopalatine, Submandibular. (The trigeminal ganglion is NOT one of them — it is a sensory ganglion.)
“There are four of them. A mnemonic [COPS], right? C, O, P, and S. C is ciliary …”🎓 Dr. Maini, Lecture 11
L11 · COPS ganglia (Bank Q79, Q83)
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Which cranial nerve feeds each ganglion
🔌 C → III · O → IX · P & S → VII
Wire each COPS ganglion to its preganglionic cranial nerve: Ciliary = CN III; Otic = CN IX; Pterygopalatine & Submandibular = CN VII. Overall, the four cranial nerves that carry parasympathetic outflow are III, VII, IX, X.
💡 Two of the four (P and S) share the facial nerve — learn them together. The eye-ganglion rides III; the parotid-ganglion (otic) rides IX. The rest is one-each.
L11 · cranial parasympathetics (Bank Q80–Q82)
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Otic ganglion ↔ parotid
👂 OTIC ganglion → PAROTID (both near the ear)
Maini pairs the otic ganglion with the parotid gland by sound and position (both sit near the ear). CN IX brings preganglionic fibers (cell bodies in the inferior salivatory nucleus; relay: tympanic nerve → tympanic plexus → lesser petrosal → otic ganglion); postganglionic fibers hitch-hike on the auriculotemporal nerve to the parotid.
“It rhymes with otic ganglion … parotid gland, otic ganglion … it’s lying in front of the ear, so otic ganglion is also lying close to the ear.”🎓 Dr. Maini, Lecture 10
L10–L11 · otic ganglion (Bank Q73–Q77)
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Chorda tympani rides the lingual nerve
👅 CHORDA TYMPANI HOPS ON THE LINGUAL (taste + spit)
The lingual nerve (V3) starts as pure general sensation (GSA) to the anterior two-thirds of the tongue. The chorda tympani (a branch of CN VII, exiting via the petrotympanic fissure) joins it, adding taste (SVA) from the anterior two-thirds AND preganglionic parasympathetic fibers that relay in the submandibular ganglion to the submandibular & sublingual glands.
💡 The lingual nerve is a taxi; the chorda tympani is the passenger who hops on carrying two parcels (taste + saliva) and hops off at the submandibular ganglion.
L11 · lingual & chorda tympani (Bank Q68–Q72)
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
The CN VII trick question
❌ DEEP PETROSAL ≠ CN VII (it’s sympathetic)
Maini’s trap: the deep petrosal nerve is NOT a branch of the facial nerve. It carries postganglionic SYMPATHETIC fibers from the internal carotid plexus, joining the greater petrosal (CN VII) to form the nerve of the pterygoid canal.
“Which one of the following is not a branch of 7th nerve? … deep petrosal … coming from ICA plexus, carrying postganglionic sympathetic fibers.”🎓 Dr. Maini, Lecture 11
L11 · nerve of pterygoid canal (Bank Q84, Q85)

Topic 7 — Pterygopalatine fossa: the crossroads

A slit you can’t fit a fingernail into — but it opens into the surrounding rooms.
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
The whole fossa in one image
🚪 THE MIDDLE ROOM, CONNECTED TO ALL THE ROOMS
Maini frames the pterygopalatine fossa as a tiny central hub that opens into the surrounding cavities — you must know each opening and what passes through it. It sits just below the orbital apex, between the pterygoid process (behind) and the maxilla (in front); floor = pyramidal process of the palatine bone; roof = greater wing of sphenoid.
“It’s like that middle room, which is connected to all these rooms.”🎓 Dr. Maini, Lecture 11
L11 · PPF location (Bank Q87, Q88)
🧩 Study aid (mine)
The four communications
🧭 LAT=FISSURE · MED=SPHENOPALATINE · SUP=IOF · POST=ROTUNDUM
Laterally to the infratemporal fossa via the pterygomaxillary fissure; medially to the nasal cavity via the sphenopalatine foramen; superiorly to the orbit via the inferior orbital fissure; posteriorly to the middle cranial fossa via foramen rotundum (which transmits V2).
💡 Four doors, four rooms: side-door to the fossa you just left, inner-door to the nose, up-door to the eye, back-door to the brain-box. Rotundum-in-the-back carries V2.
L11 · PPF communications, ~44–54 min (Bank Q89, Q90)
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Contents of the PPF
📦 PPF: V2 · PTERYGOPALATINE GANGLION · TERMINAL MAX ARTERY
Contents: the maxillary nerve (V2) with its branches, the pterygopalatine ganglion (relays CN VII parasympathetics to the lacrimal, nasal & palatine glands), and the terminal part of the maxillary artery. V2 gives the zygomatic nerve (splitting into zygomaticofacial + zygomaticotemporal) and continues as the infraorbital nerve.
💡 The back room holds one nerve (V2), one relay station (the ganglion of the same name), and the tail of one artery. V2 exits forward as the infraorbital nerve — the sensory partner of the infraorbital artery.
L11 · PPF contents (Bank Q86, Q91–Q93)
