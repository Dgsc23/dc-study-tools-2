Topic 1 — The three layers & what their names MEAN

Maini teaches these layers by the meaning of each word. Learn the meaning, keep the layer.
Layer order, outside → in
🥪 DURA · ARACHNOID · PIA
🧩 Illustrative — my study aid
Three meningeal layers wrap the brain and spinal cord: DURA (outermost, tough) → ARACHNOID (middle, web) → PIA (innermost, hugs the brain). They are continuous with the spinal meninges, but the spaces are arranged differently cranially vs spinally.
💡 Think of it like a 3-layer sandwich around the brain: tough bread (dura), stringy web filling (arachnoid), cling-film pressed on the meat (pia). Read them big-and-tough to thin-and-delicate.
L13 · Three meningeal layers (bank Q6, Q11)
What “dura” means
🛡️ DURA = “TOUGH MOTHER”
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Maini: “What does the word dura mean? Tough. Durable, right? Tough mother.”
Dura mater = the outermost, toughest layer, made of dense connective tissue. “Mater” = mother; the tough mother shields the CNS.
💡 The name IS the mnemonic: dura shares its root with durable. Tough mother = the bouncer at the door of the brain.
L13 · Meaning of dura (bank Q7, Q43)
What “arachnoid” means
🕸️ ARACHNOID = SPIDER WEB
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Maini: “It has those thread-like extensions… it’s like a spider web, so that is why it’s given the name arachnoid.”
The arachnoid is a delicate, transparent membrane of web-like tissue, with thread-like extensions spanning across toward the pia.
💡 Arachne = spider (Greek). Picture Spider-Man webbing strung between the middle layer and the brain — those threads are why it’s named for a spider.
L13 · Meaning of arachnoid (bank Q8, Q17)
What “pia” means
🧅 PIA = “TENDER MOTHER,” ONION-PEEL TIGHT
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Maini: “Pia means… tender, soft, tender mother… just like the onion peel.”
Pia mater = innermost, thin, highly vascular loose connective tissue that invests the brain so closely it dips into every sulcus and gyrus. On a cadaver you need sharp forceps to peel it off.
💡 Two Maini hooks in one: tender mother (soft, delicate) and onion peel (thin skin welded to the surface). Pia = plastic wrap pressed into every wrinkle.
L13 · Pia characteristics (bank Q9, Q12, Q15)
Pia dips in, arachnoid drapes over
🪑 ARACHNOID DRAPES, PIA TUCKS IN
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Maini: “It’s like you’re trying to cover the chair with a sheet… you can tuck the sheet in, or you can just cover it with the sheet.”
Pia follows the contours (down into the sulci and gyri); arachnoid passes over them. That “passing over” is exactly what leaves the gaps — the subarachnoid space and its cisterns.
💡 Maini’s chair analogy: pia = a fitted sheet tucked into every dip; arachnoid = a flat sheet thrown over the top. The air pockets under the flat sheet become your cisterns (Topic 6).
L13 · Pia vs arachnoid contours (bank Q10, Q24)

Topic 2 — Brain surface: gyri, sulci, gray & white matter

Why the surface is wrinkled, and what the two colors are made of.
Ridges vs grooves
⛰️ GYRI = RIDGES · SULCI = GROOVES
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Maini: “The ridges are called gyri; the dips, the grooves, are called sulci.”
Gyri = the raised folds; sulci = the grooves between them (sulcus/sulci, gyrus/gyri). A deep groove is a fissure.
💡 My add on top of her definition: Gyrus = the Ground going up (a hill); Sulcus = the Sunken valley.
L13 · Gyri & sulci (bank Q1)
Why so wrinkled?
🧠 WRINKLES = 10,000× MORE NEURONS
🧩 Illustrative — my study aid
More folds = more cortical surface = more gray matter. [Maini:] “Your brain accommodates almost 10,000 times more neurons than if the brain was smooth.” Loss of the folds (surface smoothing) tracks with dementia — fewer cortical neurons.
💡 Think of it like crumpling a big sheet of paper to fit a small box: the wrinkles pack far more surface (neurons) into the same skull.
L13 · Cortical convolutions (bank Q2, Q3)
Gray vs white matter
🎨 GRAY = CELL BODIES · WHITE = MYELIN WIRES
🧩 Illustrative — my study aid
Gray matter = the outer cortex, the cell bodies. White matter = the deep myelinated fibers. [Maini:] “Anything that looks white in the nervous system has a myelin sheath.”
💡 Think of it like a city: gray = the offices (cell bodies) on the surface; white = the fatty insulated cables (myelin) wiring them together underneath.
L13 · Gray vs white matter (bank Q4, Q5)

Topic 3 — Dura mater: two layers, spaces & pain

Cranial dura is a two-ply sheet glued to bone. Its “spaces” only appear when something bleeds.
Two layers (cranial only)
📕 CRANIAL DURA = 2 PLIES (PERIOSTEAL + MENINGEAL)
🧩 Illustrative — my study aid
Cranial dura = two layers: outer periosteal (against the bone) + inner meningeal. Spinal dura = meningeal layer only. The meningeal layer is the one that folds inward to make the septa (Topic 4) and splits to form the sinuses (Topic 7).
💡 Think of it like wallpaper on a wall: the periosteal ply is glued to the “bone wall”; the meningeal ply is the free inner sheet that peels inward to make partitions (septa) and blood-filled pockets (sinuses).
L13 · Dura layers (bank Q44, Q45, Q50, Q51)
No natural cranial spaces
🚫 EPIDURAL & SUBDURAL = “BLEED-ONLY”
🧩 Illustrative — my study aidclinical
Cranial dura is stuck to the skull’s periosteum AND to the arachnoid — so there is no natural epidural or subdural space in the head. If a scan shows dura lifted off the bone, that gap is an abnormal collection (blood/pus). Note: the spinal epidural space IS real (fat + veins).
💡 In the skull these “spaces” only exist when something bad fills them. Empty in health, present in disease = your red flag on the image.
L13 · Cranial meningeal spaces (bank Q46, Q75, Q76, Q77)
Dura is the pain layer
🤕 DURA = WHERE THE HEADACHE LIVES
🎓 Dr. Maini’s ownclinical
Maini: “Dura has a large number of pain receptors… whenever it is stretched, there is going to be intense pain.”
The dura is loaded with pain receptors. The brain itself feels no pain, but stretching the dura hurts intensely — e.g., a pituitary tumor stretching the diaphragma sellae causes an intense headache.
💡 The brain is numb; the dura is the smoke detector. Pull on the tough mother and she screams — that scream is the headache.
L13 · Dura pain receptors (bank Q49, Q82)

Topic 4 — The four dural septa

The meningeal dura folds inward into four partitions. Maini gives you two of the names outright.
All four, grouped
⛺ 2 SICKLES, 1 TENT, 1 TRAPDOOR
🧩 Illustrative — my study aid
The meningeal dura folds into FOUR septa: falx cerebri, tentorium cerebelli, falx cerebelli, and diaphragma sellae.
💡 My grouping: two sickles (falx cerebri + falx cerebelli), one tent (tentorium cerebelli over the cerebellum), one trapdoor (diaphragma sellae, the lid on the pituitary). 2 + 1 + 1 = 4.
L13 · Number of dural septa (bank Q47)
Falx cerebri
🌙 FALX = SICKLE (splits L & R cerebrum)
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Maini: “Falx means a sickle — a sickle-shaped vertical partition in the longitudinal fissure between the left and right cerebral hemispheres.”
Falx cerebri = a sickle-shaped vertical sheet in the longitudinal fissure between the two cerebral hemispheres. Attaches anteriorly to the crista galli / frontal crest, posteriorly to the internal occipital crest.
💡 “Falx” = Latin for sickle (a curved blade). Picture a curved blade standing on edge down the midline slot between the two hemispheres.
L13 · Falx cerebri (bank Q53, Q54)
Tentorium cerebelli
⛺ TENTORIUM = TENT OVER THE CEREBELLUM
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Maini: “Tentorium means a tent over the cerebellum… it separates the occipital lobe of the cerebrum from the cerebellum.”
Tentorium cerebelli = a wide, arched, tent-like shelf separating the occipital lobe above from the cerebellum below. Attaches to the posterior clinoid processes, the superior petrous temporal bone, and the grooves for the transverse sinuses.
💡 “Tentorium” = tent. Pitch a little tent over the cerebellum; the occipital lobe rests on the roof.
L13 · Tentorium cerebelli (bank Q55, Q56, Q60)
Falx cerebelli + diaphragma sellae
🌒 SHALLOW SICKLE + 🕳️ PITUITARY LID
🧩 Illustrative — my study aid
Falx cerebelli = a small, shallow sickle between the two cerebellar hemispheres (they aren’t split as widely as the cerebral ones — [Maini:] the cerebellum is “a small brain,” so its falx is small). Diaphragma sellae = a small horizontal roof over the sella turcica, with a central hole for the pituitary stalk.
💡 The cerebral falx is a deep blade; the cerebellar one is a butter-knife (shallow). The diaphragma sellae is a manhole cover over the pituitary, with a hole for the stalk to poke through.
L13 · Falx cerebelli & diaphragma sellae (bank Q57, Q58, Q59)

Topic 5 — CSF: made, pooled, drained

One pump, one basin, one drain. Follow the fluid and the whole system falls into place.
The CSF pipeline
🚰 MAKE → POOL → DRAIN
🧩 Illustrative — my study aid
CSF is made by the choroid plexus (roof of each ventricle) → circulates in the ventricles and the subarachnoid space (pooled) → drained by the arachnoid granulations into the superior sagittal sinus, back to venous blood. Make-rate must equal drain-rate.
💡 Think of it like a fountain with a pump and a drain: choroid plexus is the pump, the subarachnoid space is the basin, the arachnoid granulations are the drain. Clog the drain → the basin overflows (raised intracranial pressure).
L13 · CSF production & absorption (bank Q19, Q26, Q32, Q40, Q41)
Subarachnoid space
⬇️ SUB-ARACHNOID = “BENEATH THE ARACHNOID”
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Maini: “Subarachnoid means beneath the arachnoid layer — between the arachnoid and the pia.”
The subarachnoid space sits between arachnoid and pia and holds the CSF (plus the cerebral veins). It is the only natural CSF-filled meningeal space.
💡 The prefix does the work: sub = under. Beneath the spider-web layer, above the cling-film pia. That’s where the fluid lives.
L13 · Subarachnoid space (bank Q27, Q42)
Secretion & the one-way valve
🏭 EPENDYMA MAKES IT · GRANULATIONS = ONE-WAY VALVES
🧩 Illustrative — my study aidboard Q
Ependymal cells of the choroid plexus secrete CSF (pia + ependyma = the choroid plexus, a filtration membrane). Arachnoid granulations act as one-way valves that absorb CSF — and only the superior sagittal sinus receives it. [Maini flagged this as a “frequently asked board question.”]
💡 A one-way valve = a turnstile: CSF exits into the sinus, but venous blood can’t back-flow into the CSF. Blood cells or debris can jam the turnstile → pressure builds.
L13 · Granulations & choroid plexus (bank Q18, Q20, Q21, Q37, Q38)
The four glial cells
🧫 4 GLIA: A-O-M-E (E = the CSF maker)
🧩 Illustrative — my study aid
Four glial (neuroglia) types protect neurons: Astrocytes, Oligodendrocytes, Microglia, Ependymal cells. The Ependymal cells are the ones that secrete CSF. Neurons are non-mitotic (not replaced if damaged), so they lean hard on glia and CSF for protection.
💡 Say “AOME” (AH-oh-mee) and park the E last on purpose: Ependymal = the Effluent (fluid) maker.
L13 · Glial cells (bank Q31, Q34, Q35, Q36)

Topic 6 — Cisterns

Cisterns are just the big puddles left where the arachnoid skips over a dip.
What a cistern is
🫧 CISTERNS = POCKETS WHERE ARACHNOID SKIPS OVER
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Maini: “Arachnoid passes over the brain structures, and those spaces are created — they are called cisterns.”
Cisterns = enlarged pools of the subarachnoid space, created wherever the arachnoid drapes over a dip instead of following it. All are CSF-filled.
💡 Back to the flat-sheet-over-a-chair: the big air pockets under the sheet = cisterns. Same fluid as the subarachnoid space, just deeper puddles.
L13 · Cisterns (bank Q70, Q71)
The largest one
🏆 CISTERNA MAGNA = “MAGNA” = LARGEST
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Maini: “The largest is between the cerebellum and medulla — the cerebellomedullary cistern, or cisterna magna.”
The cerebellomedullary cistern (a.k.a. cisterna magna) sits between the cerebellum and the medulla, and is the largest cistern.
💡 Magna = magnificent = biggest (think “Magnum”). Located where the cerebellum meets the brainstem.
L13 · Cisterna magna (bank Q72)
The other named cisterns
📍 CHIASMATIC · INTERPEDUNCULAR · PONTINE · SUPERIOR
🧩 Illustrative — my study aidclinical
Besides cisterna magna, Maini names the chiasmatic, interpeduncular, pontine, and superior cisterns. Clinically, a subarachnoid bleed spreads through all the cisterns if it isn’t stopped in time.
💡 My front-to-back-to-top walk: the optic chiasm (chiasmatic) → between the peduncles (interpeduncular) → on the pons (pontine) → up top (superior). Name each rest-stop along the CSF highway.
L13 · Named cisterns (bank Q73, Q74)

Topic 7 — Dural venous sinuses & air embolism

Decode the names, then respect the vacuum: these blood channels can suck in a fatal air bubble.
What “sinus” means
🕳️ SINUS = “A SPACE”
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Maini: “The literal meaning of the word sinus is a space.”
A dural venous sinus is a venous, blood-filled space formed where the two dura layers (periosteal + meningeal) separate.
💡 Don’t overthink “sinus” — it just means a hollow. Here it’s a blood-filled channel tucked between the two plies of dura.
L13 · Meaning of sinus (bank Q61, Q63)
Superior sagittal sinus
🧭 SUPERIOR = TOP · SAGITTAL = FRONT-TO-BACK
🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
Maini: “Superior means on the top; sagittal means it goes anteroposteriorly.”
The superior sagittal sinus = a midline channel along the top of the head running front-to-back. It is the sinus that receives CSF via the arachnoid granulations.
💡 Decode the name and you know the anatomy: superior (roof) + sagittal (the arrow-line, front to back). Location and orientation are baked into the label.
L13 · Superior sagittal sinus (bank Q21, Q62)
Negative pressure & air embolism
💨 NEGATIVE PRESSURE = AIR-SUCKING SINUSES
🧩 Illustrative — my study aidclinical
Dural sinuses have negative pressure (venous pressure is ~0 at the heart, about +95 mmHg at the feet, but below zero up in the head). A nick in a sinus can suck air in → air embolism that travels to the heart and arteries (stroke, MI). Same reason the external jugular is normally collapsed.
💡 Think of it like a straw with a hole above the liquid line: instead of leaking out, it sucks air in. A tiny cut in a head sinus can pull in a fatal air bubble on the operating table.
L13 · Sinus pressure & air embolism (bank Q64, Q65, Q66, Q67, Q68, Q84)

Topic 8 — Meningeal bleeds (clinical)

Match the tempo of the bleed to the vessel that’s leaking.
Arterial vs venous tempo
⏱️ ARTERIAL = FAST & DEADLY · VENOUS = SLOW (days–weeks)
🧩 Illustrative — my study aidclinical
Arterial bleeds collect fast under high pressure — they compress structures “in no time.” Venous bleeds are slow, sometimes noticed only over days or weeks. A subarachnoid hemorrhage is usually venous, because the cerebral veins run on the pia within the subarachnoid space.
💡 Arterial = a burst fire-hose (emergency now); venous = a slow drip (sneaks up over days). Read the tempo and you can guess the vessel.
L13 · Arterial vs venous bleed (bank Q78, Q79, Q80)
