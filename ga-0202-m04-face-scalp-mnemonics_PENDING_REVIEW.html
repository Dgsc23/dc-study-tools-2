Topic 1 — The 5 layers of the scalp (SCALP)

Maini’s five-letter walk, the “dangerous” layer, and why side-to-side cuts gape.
The 5 layers, in order
🧠 S · C · A · L · P
Skin · Connective tissue (dense subcutaneous) · Aponeurosis (the epicranial aponeurosis = galea aponeurotica, holding the two bellies of occipitofrontalis) · Loose areolar connective tissue · Pericranium (periosteum of the skull). The word SCALP itself spells the five layers, outer to deep.
🎙️ Dr. Maini (Lecture 7, ~06:05)“S for skin, connective tissue, aponeurotic layer, loose areolar tissue layer, and then pericranium. These five layers, in that order, they make up your scalp.”
Maini L07 00:35, 01:05, 03:39, 06:05 · Bank: Scalp — Layers (SCALP)
The dangerous layer
🩸 LAYER 4 (LOOSE AREOLAR) = THE FREE-SPACE LAYER
The loose areolar connective tissue (4th layer) is loosely arranged with abundant potential space, so pus (folliculitis/furunculosis) and blood/haematoma collect there. It is also the scalping plane — a hard pull separates the outer three layers from the pericranium along it.
🎙️ Dr. Maini (Lecture 7, ~04:33 & 05:02)“Along this layer you can have collections like pus... and there can be haematoma formation in that layer.” — “You can actually separate outer three layers from periosteum along this layer.”
💡 Think of it like a loose bedsheet over a mattress: the tight layers above (skin/connective/aponeurosis) move as one, and the roomy gap underneath is where fluid pools — and where a scalp gets peeled off in one flap.
Maini L07 04:33, 05:02 · Bank: Scalp — Layers / Clinical
Why side-to-side wounds gape
↔️ GALEA FIBERS RUN FRONT–BACK
The galea aponeurotica is dense regular connective tissue whose collagen fibers run longitudinally (front-to-back, along the sagittal suture). A cut across those fibers (side-to-side) lets the frontalis and occipitalis pull the edges apart, so the wound gapes and needs suturing; a cut along the fibers heals better.
Illustrative💡 Think of it like ripping paper against the grain vs with it: cut across the grain and the sheet springs open; cut with the grain and the edges stay put.
Maini L07 02:43, 15:39 · Bank: Scalp — Clinical

Topic 2 — Scalp vessels & nerves

Two arteries from the front (ICA) vs three from behind (ECA), and who feels the scalp.
Arterial supply — the front pair
👁️ SUPRA-TROCHLEAR + SUPRA-ORBITAL = ICA (via ophthalmic)
The two anterior scalp arteries — supratrochlear and supraorbital — are branches of the ophthalmic artery, which is a branch of the internal carotid. They exit the orbit through the supratrochlear/supraorbital foramina onto the forehead.
🎙️ Dr. Maini (Lecture 7, ~08:47)“Supraorbital and supratrochlear are branches of ophthalmic, which is a branch of internal carotid.”
Illustrative💡 Think of it like “SUPRA = above the eye = fed from inside (ICA)”: the two “supra” arteries climb up out of the orbit, so they come from the internal carotid, not the external.
Maini L07 06:45, 08:47 · Bank: Scalp — Vessels & Nerves
Arterial supply — the back three
🔙 ST · PA · OCC = ECA (Superficial Temporal, Posterior Auricular, Occipital)
The other three scalp arteries — superficial temporal, posterior auricular, and occipital — are branches of the external carotid. So the scalp is fed by both carotids that meet over the surface: ICA in front, ECA behind and to the sides.
Illustrative💡 Think of it like a lawn watered from two hoses: the front sprinkler is the internal carotid (two jets), the back/side sprinkler is the external carotid (three jets). They overlap in the middle — which is exactly why the scalp bleeds so much.
Maini L07 08:19, 08:51 · Bank: Scalp — Vessels & Nerves
Why scalp wounds bleed & heal
🚿 BLOOD FROM BOTH CUT ENDS
Because those arteries form a rich anastomosis, a cut vessel bleeds from both ends (not just the proximal end like a limb). Result: profuse bleeding — but also excellent healing thanks to the same rich vascularity.
🎙️ Dr. Maini (Lecture 7, ~14:29)“On the scalp you have blood flowing from both directions... because there is connection between the arteries.”
Maini L07 13:52, 14:29 · Bank: Scalp — Clinical
Sensory supply of the scalp
🗺️ TRIGEMINAL IN FRONT · OCCIPITALS BEHIND
Front / most of the scalp = trigeminal (CN V) branches. Back = the occipitals: lesser occipital (C2–C3, from the cervical plexus / ventral rami), plus greater occipital and third (least) occipital (from the dorsal rami of C2/C3 down the midline).
Illustrative💡 Think of it like a hoodie: the trigeminal is the front visor covering the forehead, and the occipital nerves are the hood at the back — and the midline strip (greater/third occipital) rides the dorsal rami, exactly like the seam runs down the hood’s spine.
Maini L07 09:21, 11:05, 11:17 · Bank: Scalp — Vessels & Nerves

Topic 3 — Muscles of facial expression

The one rule that unlocks the whole group, plus name-decoding hooks.
The one rule for the whole group
7️⃣ ALL FACIAL-EXPRESSION MUSCLES = CN VII
Every muscle of facial expression is supplied by the facial nerve (CN VII), carrying SVE (branchial motor) fibers. Group them and one fact answers a dozen questions. (Contrast: levator palpebrae superioris opens the eye and is CN III — NOT a facial-expression muscle.)
🎙️ Dr. Maini (Lecture 7, ~22:59)“What is one thing common between the muscles of facial expression? Innervation — seventh nerve, facial nerve.”
Maini L07 22:59, 24:58, 30:42 · Bank: Muscles of Facial Expression — General
The two sphincters
⭕ ORBICULARIS = “ORBIT” (it circles the opening)
Orbicularis oculi circles the eye and closes the eyelid (its loss = lagophthalmos → dry xerophthalmia). Orbicularis oris circles the mouth and closes the lips (whistle / pout). Maini decodes the name itself.
🎙️ Dr. Maini (Lecture 7, ~31:59)“The word orbicularis means orbit. They orbit around.”
💡 Also her literal decoding: lagophthalmos = lid lag (eye won’t close); xerophthalmia = dry eye. The Latin IS the mnemonic.
Maini L07 29:56, 31:59; L08 00:26 · Bank: Muscles of Facial Expression — Specific
Read the Latin name = get origin + action
🔤 LEVATOR/DEPRESSOR + LABII/ANGULI + SUPERIORIS/ORIS
Maini’s method for the lip muscles: decode the name. Levator = elevates, depressor = depresses; labii = of the lip, anguli = of the angle; superioris = upper, oris = the (single) mouth angle. So levator labii superioris lifts the upper lip; depressor anguli oris pulls the mouth-angle down. Levator labii superioris alaeque nasi = the long one that lifts the upper lip AND flares the nostril (ala).
🎙️ Dr. Maini (Lecture 7, ~30:42)“Levator — the word itself says levator elevates. Palpebrae is the scientific word for eyelid. Superioris is upper.”
Maini L07 30:42; L08 02:31, 05:00–07:50 · Bank: Muscles of Facial Expression — Specific
Buccinator, the deep one
🎺 BUCCINATOR = “TRUMPETER’S MUSCLE”
Buccinator compresses the cheek against the teeth (chewing, blowing, swallowing); paralysis → food pockets in the cheek & drooling. Two ID tricks: it is pierced by the parotid duct, and its nickname is the trumpeter’s muscle.
🎙️ Dr. Maini (Lecture 8, ~13:00)“Also called trumpeteer’s muscle, because you need to do that action to play your trumpet.”
💡 Picture puffing your cheeks to blow a trumpet — that is buccinator squeezing the cheek flat. The parotid duct spearing through it is your dissection give-away.
Maini L08 12:39, 13:00 · Bank: Muscles of Facial Expression — Specific

Topic 4 — Facial nerve motor branches

Maini’s own five-branch mnemonic, the platysma trap, and the “nearest muscle” rule.
The 5 branches on the face
🚗 “TO ZAMBIA BY MOTOR CAR”
The five branches of CN VII fanning out of the parotid, top to bottom: Temporal · Zygomatic · Buccal · Marginal mandibular · Cervical. (Before the face it also gives the posterior auricular and the branch to posterior belly of digastric + stylohyoid.)
🎙️ Dr. Maini (Lecture 8, ~31:20)“To Zambia by motor car... T temporal, zygomatic, buccal, marginal mandibular, and cervical.”
💡 To Zambia By Motor Car → T-Z-B-M-C, read from the temple down to the neck. (Some books say “Zanzibar” — use whichever place sticks; the letters are what matter.)
Maini L08 31:20–32:01 · Bank: Facial Nerve — Motor Branches
The cervical-branch trap
🧣 CERVICAL BRANCH → PLATYSMA (the only neck one)
There is exactly one muscle of facial expression in the neck: platysma, supplied by the cervical branch. Maini flags this as a commonly-missed question (only ~50% got it last term).
🎙️ Dr. Maini (Lecture 8, ~36:04)“There’s only one muscle of facial expression in the neck — platysma.”
Maini L08 35:58, 36:04 · Bank: Facial Nerve — Motor Branches
Which branch supplies which muscle?
📍 A BRANCH FEEDS THE MUSCLES NEAREST IT
Maini’s common-sense rule: a facial-nerve branch innervates the muscles in its vicinity, not the ones farthest away. So the temporal branch serves upper-face muscles (frontalis, orbicularis oculi, corrugator, auricularis) — and does NOT supply mentalis (that’s down at the chin).
Illustrative💡 Think of it like local mail routes: each branch delivers to its own neighborhood. Temporal is uptown; mentalis lives downtown — wrong zip code.
Maini L08 34:46–35:35 · Bank: Facial Nerve — Motor Branches

Topic 5 — Trigeminal sensory zones (V1 / V2 / V3)

Three floors of the face, and the lip/chin trap examiners love.
Three divisions, three levels
🏢 V1 OPHTHALMIC (top) · V2 MAXILLARY (mid) · V3 MANDIBULAR (low)
Trigeminal (CN V) = general sensory to the face in three stacked zones. V1 = ophthalmic (forehead, upper eyelid, bridge/tip of nose). V2 = maxillary (cheek, upper lip). V3 = mandibular (lower lip, chin, jaw). Trigeminus = “three divisions.”
🎙️ Dr. Maini (Lecture 7, ~10:09)“What is the name of V1? Ophthalmic. V2 is maxillary. And V3 is mandibular.”
Illustrative💡 Think of it like a 3-storey building: eyes on the top floor (V1), cheeks on the middle (V2), jaw in the basement (V3). Count top-to-bottom, 1-2-3.
Maini L07 09:21, 10:09; L08 01:06:37 · Bank: Trigeminal — Sensory Zones
The lip & nose trap
👄 UPPER LIP = V2 · LOWER LIP + CHIN = V3 · NOSE-TIP = V1
The tested boundary: the upper lip is V2, but the lower lip AND chin are V3. And despite the nose sitting mid-face, the tip/bridge of the nose is V1 (external nasal branch). Trigeminal neuralgia (tic douloureux) hits V2 or V3 most; herpes zoster ophthalmicus hits V1 (threatens the cornea).
Illustrative💡 Think of it like a zipper meeting at the lips: the seam splits there — top lip stays with the cheek (V2), bottom lip drops to the jaw (V3). The nose-tip is the odd one out, reaching up to V1.
Maini L08 01:06:48–01:07:02, 01:10:44 · Bank: Trigeminal — Sensory Zones / Clinical

Topic 6 — Parotid gland: the secretomotor chain (CN IX)

The long parasympathetic relay list, and the CN-IX-not-VII trap.
Parotid parasympathetic pathway
🚉 IX → TYMPANIC → LESSER PETROSAL → OTIC → AURICULOTEMPORAL → PAROTID
Saliva-to-parotid relay: inferior salivatory nucleus → CN IX (glossopharyngeal) → tympanic nerve → tympanic plexus → lesser petrosal nerve → synapse in the otic ganglion → postganglionic fibers ride the auriculotemporal nerve (V3) → parotid gland.
🎙️ Dr. Maini (Lecture 8, ~01:32:27)“It’s ninth nerve, glossopharyngeal... tympanic plexus, lesser petrosal, otic ganglion, auriculotemporal, parotid gland.”
Illustrative💡 Think of it like a train with a transfer: board the 9 line, change at the middle-ear station (tympanic plexus), catch the lesser petrosal express, transfer at Otic junction, then hitch the auriculotemporal shuttle to the parotid terminal.
Maini L08 20:44–25:57, 01:31:41–01:32:41 · Bank: Parotid Gland & Secretomotor Pathway
The exam trap
⚠️ PAROTID = CN IX, NOT CN VII
Every OTHER salivary gland in this module runs on CN VII — so students reflexively pick 7 for the parotid too. But the parotid’s preganglionic parasympathetics are CN IX. Maini put this as the last Menti question precisely because “seventh” is the tempting wrong answer.
Illustrative💡 Think of it like the one sibling who doesn’t share the family car: submandibular & sublingual take the CN VII carpool (chorda tympani); the parotid insists on its own CN IX ride.
Maini L08 20:23, 01:32:41 · Bank: Parotid Gland & Secretomotor Pathway
CN VII’s intracranial branches
💧 GREATER PETROSAL · 🔇 NERVE TO STAPEDIUS · 👅 CHORDA TYMPANI
Before it exits the stylomastoid foramen, CN VII gives three branches — each maps to a lesion sign: greater petrosal (parasympathetic → lacrimal/nasal/palatine glands; lesion = dry eye), nerve to stapedius (lesion = hyperacusis), chorda tympani (taste, anterior 2/3 tongue + preganglionic to the submandibular ganglion).
Illustrative💡 Think of it like a checklist that localizes the lesion: dry eye + sound-sensitivity + lost front-tongue taste means the damage is high (inside the canal), not just at the exit.
Maini L08 39:34, 47:55, 49:10–52:14 · Bank: Facial Nerve — Intracranial Course
Three parasympathetic ganglia today
🎯 OTIC (IX) · PTERYGOPALATINE (VII) · SUBMANDIBULAR (VII)
Maini names three ganglia in one lecture: Otic (parotid, via CN IX), Pterygopalatine (lacrimal + nasal + palatine glands, via greater petrosal of CN VII), Submandibular (submandibular + sublingual glands, via chorda tympani of CN VII). The fourth, ciliary (eye, CN III), comes later.
Illustrative💡 Think of it like three switchboards: Otic routes the parotid (9), Pterygopalatine routes tears & nose (7-greater petrosal), Submandibular routes the two floor-of-mouth glands (7-chorda tympani).
Maini L08 57:25 · Bank: Parotid / Facial Nerve — Intracranial Course
Naming hook
🦵 GENICULATE = AT THE GENU (the bend)
The geniculate ganglion of CN VII sits at the sharp bend the nerve makes in the facial canal. “Genu” = knee/bend (same root as genuflect), so the ganglion at the bend is geniculate.
🎙️ Dr. Maini (Lecture 8, ~44:48)“It is called geniculate because it is at the genu, the bend.”
Maini L08 44:48 · Bank: Facial Nerve — Intracranial Course

Topic 7 — Facial vessels, the danger area & the ECA list

Two vein traps, the danger triangle, and the eight-branch ECA list (illustrative).
Branches of the facial artery (5)
🅰️ SUBMENTAL · INF. LABIAL · SUP. LABIAL · LATERAL NASAL · ANGULAR
The facial artery (a branch of the ECA) supplies most of the face via five named branches, chin-to-eye: submental → inferior labial → superior labial → lateral nasal → angular (ending near the medial eye, where it meets the ophthalmic system).
Illustrative💡 Think of it like climbing the face from chin to eye: under the chin (submental), lower lip, upper lip, side of the nose, then the corner of the eye (angular). Follow the artery’s own path upward.
Maini L08 01:14:44–01:15:33 · Bank: Facial Vessels — Arteries
Artery trap
🎣 TRANSVERSE FACIAL ≠ FACIAL ARTERY
Despite the name, the transverse facial artery is NOT a branch of the facial artery — it is a branch of the superficial temporal artery (you can feel its pulse along the zygomatic arch). Maini calls this a board-exam trick question.
🎙️ Dr. Maini (Lecture 8, ~01:17:33)“Transverse facial is not a branch of facial... it’s a branch of superficial temporal.”
Maini L08 01:17:33–01:18:06 · Bank: Facial Vessels — Arteries
Vein trap
🩸 COMMON FACIAL VEIN → INTERNAL JUGULAR
The facial vein joins the anterior division of the retromandibular to form the common facial vein, which drains into the INTERNAL jugular — not the external, even though the matching artery is the external carotid. That mismatch is the trap.
🎙️ Dr. Maini (Lecture 8, ~01:19:43)“Where does common facial drain? Internal jugular, not external jugular.”
Maini L08 01:19:43–01:20:02 · Bank: Facial Vessels — Veins & Danger Area
The danger triangle of the face
☠️ NOSE + UPPER LIP → CAVERNOUS SINUS (valveless)
The facial vein communicates with the cavernous sinus — directly via the superior/inferior ophthalmic veins, and indirectly via the deep facial vein → pterygoid venous plexus. Because these veins are valveless, infection in the nose/upper-lip “danger triangle” can spread intracranially → cavernous sinus thrombosis, meningitis/encephalitis.
🎙️ Dr. Maini (Lecture 8, ~01:22:06)“This infection can spread to the cavernous sinus, leading to intracranial infection... meningitis, encephalitis.”
Illustrative💡 Think of it like a backdoor with no lock: squeeze a pimple in the triangle and bacteria can flow backward through valveless veins straight into the skull.
Maini L08 01:20:56–01:22:55 · Bank: Facial Vessels — Veins & Danger Area
The 8 branches of the ECA
🧾 “Some Anatomists Like Freaking Out Poor Medical Students”
Maini says you must know all eight ECA branches; three feed the scalp (superficial temporal, posterior auricular, occipital). The classic list, superior to inferior-ish: Superior thyroid, Ascending pharyngeal, Lingual, Facial, Occipital, Posterior auricular, Maxillary, Superficial temporal.
Illustrative💡 “Some Anatomists Like Freaking Out Poor Medical Students” is a widely-used hook, not stated by Maini in these lectures — but she winked at it herself: “I’ve become that anatomist who freaks out medical students... I gave you the mnemonic also” (L07 ~09:12). She referenced a mnemonic without spelling one out here, so treat the acronym as mine.

Topic 8 — Facial nerve lesion (Bell’s palsy)

The whole-side LMN picture — how it differs from a central lesion.
Bell’s palsy signs
😶 WHOLE HALF-FACE DROPS (LMN pattern)
A lower-motor-neuron CN VII lesion (idiopathic = Bell’s palsy, often post-cold/viral) paralyses the entire half of the face: loss of forehead wrinkling, can’t close the eye (lagophthalmos + lost nasolabial fold), and the mouth droops / deviates to the normal side. Advise eye-strapping, massage, and stimulation to limit residual paralysis.
Illustrative💡 Think of it like the whole light circuit tripping on one side — forehead included. (A central/stroke lesion spares the forehead, because the upper face has backup wiring from both hemispheres.)
