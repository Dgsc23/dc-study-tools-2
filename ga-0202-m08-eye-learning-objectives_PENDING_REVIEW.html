1
Describe the bony orbit — its pyramidal shape, apex vs base, and the prominent bone(s) of each of the four walls.
content: L19 · 00:38–07:06
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Call the orbit a quadrilateral pyramid with a posterior apex (essentially the optic canal, where the optic nerve leaves for the cranial cavity) and an anterior base (the opening). (L19 01:22–01:59)
  • Name the three bones of the margin/base: frontal, zygomatic, maxilla. (L19 01:59–02:29)
  • Give the prominent bone of each wall: roof = orbital plate of frontal + lesser wing of sphenoid; medial wall = ethmoid (air cells) plus the papery-thin lacrimal (“papyrus”) bone; floor = mostly maxilla; lateral wall = zygomatic + greater wing of sphenoid. (L19 02:29–06:41)
  • State Maini’s test rule: know the prominent bones (she won’t test the tiny palatine contribution to the floor), but do know which bone each fissure/foramen belongs to. (L19 06:41–07:06)

“That is the apex. So that means your optic canal … optic nerve leaves through optic canal and enters the cranial cavity … that is the apex of the orbit … you can say quadrilateral pyramidal structure with an apex and a base.”— The Orbit and Its Contents (L19) · ~00:01:27–00:01:59
🎯 Cement it

🧪 Quick self-check
What structure marks the apex of the orbit, and which three bones form its base (margin)?
The optic canal marks the posterior apex (optic nerve exits to the cranial cavity). The base/margin = frontal, zygomatic, maxilla. Roof = frontal plate + lesser wing of sphenoid; medial wall = ethmoid (+ lacrimal); floor = maxilla; lateral wall = zygomatic + greater wing of sphenoid.
2
Map the fissures, canals & foramina of the orbit — the three “doorways,” what each connects to, and the local landmarks.
content: L19 · 07:06–15:00
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Name the three doorways: superior orbital fissure, inferior orbital fissure, and the optic canal. (L19 10:33–11:03)
  • SOF (between greater & lesser wings of sphenoid): CN III, IV, VI, the three V1 branches (lacrimal, frontal, nasociliary), superior ophthalmic vein — communicates with the middle cranial fossa. (L19 11:42–12:53)
  • IOF (between maxilla & greater wing of sphenoid): V2 (maxillary), inferior ophthalmic vein, and the infraorbital artery — opens to the pterygopalatine / infratemporal fossa, not the cranial cavity. (L19 10:33–13:37)
  • Optic canal (lesser wing of sphenoid): optic nerve + ophthalmic artery, to the middle cranial fossa. Plus local landmarks — supraorbital notch/foramen, infraorbital groove, and the lacrimal fossa (holds the lacrimal sac, not the gland). (L19 07:06–09:36, 14:04–14:20)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Fissures are actually the inlets, or the doorways to the orbit. So there are 3 doorways to the orbit … there is superior orbital fissure … the next one is inferior orbital fissure … and then there is optic canal.”— The Orbit and Its Contents (L19) · ~00:10:33–00:11:03
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M08 Eye & Orbit Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which doorway does NOT communicate with the cranial cavity, and what fossa does it open into instead?
The inferior orbital fissure does not reach the cranial cavity — it opens into the pterygopalatine / infratemporal fossa (carrying V2, inferior ophthalmic vein, infraorbital artery). The SOF and optic canal both connect to the middle cranial fossa.
3
Work through the eyelids & lacrimal apparatus — the glands (and their inflammations), plus the path a tear travels to the nose.
content: L19 · 19:30–37:00 · L20 · 05:00–09:00
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Sort the marginal glands from the tarsal glands: sebaceous glands of Zeis + sweat glands of Moll sit at the lid margin (inflame → external hordeolum / stye); the tarsal (meibomian) glands sit inside the tarsal plate and keep the closed lids airtight (inflame → internal hordeolum / chalazion, often scooped out). (L19 21:16–25:06)
  • Trace a tear: lacrimal gland → ducts across the eye → lacrimal lake (with the central caruncle and lateral plica semilunaris) → lacrimal punctum on each papilla → canaliculi → lacrimal sac → nasolacrimal duct → inferior meatus of the nose. (L19 25:12–30:08 · L20 05:11–06:44)
  • Split the two blockages/inflammations: dacryoadenitis = inflammation of the gland (autoimmune); dacryocystitis = inflammation of the sac (infectious). No tears at all = xerophthalmia → corneal ulcers. (L19 30:22–31:08 · L20 06:59–08:58)
  • Peel the eyelid layers: skin → superficial fascia with orbicularis oculi (closes the eye, CN VII) → levator palpebrae superioris tendon → tarsal plate with meibomian glands → palpebral conjunctiva. (L19 31:33–35:11)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“There’ll be a swelling at the lid margin, which you call external hord[eolum], or stye … sometimes there can be inflammation of the tarsal gland … if they get inflamed, you will get internal hord[eolum], or [chalazion].”— The Orbit and Its Contents (L19) · ~00:23:29–00:23:53
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M08 Eye & Orbit Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
A swelling on the eyelid (not at the margin) — which gland is inflamed and what is it called? And where do tears finally drain?
A swelling on the lid (not the margin) = inflamed tarsal / meibomian gland = internal hordeolum / chalazion (a margin swelling = external hordeolum / stye of Zeis or Moll). Tears drain punctum → canaliculi → sac → nasolacrimal duct → inferior meatus of the nose.
4
Explain why levator palpebrae superioris is unique — its two lamellae, the smooth superior tarsal muscle, and Horner syndrome.
content: L19 · 37:00–47:00 · L20 · 00:15–05:00
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State the origin/action: LPS arises from the lesser wing of the sphenoid above the optic canal and elevates the upper eyelid (there is no elevator for the lower lid). (L19 37:57–38:14)
  • Split its two lamellae: the anterior (skeletal) lamella → anterior tarsal plate, CN III (GSE); the posterior lamella = the superior tarsal muscle, which is smooth muscle → superior margin of tarsal plate, sympathetic only. (L19 38:21–39:57)
  • Predict a sympathetic lesion: partial ptosis (not complete, because the CN III skeletal part is intact). (L19 40:22–41:08)
  • List the 5 signs of Horner syndrome (a board favorite): partial ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis, facial flushing (vasodilation), and enophthalmos — from sympathetic failure on one side (classically a Pancoast tumor of the lung apex). (L19 41:08–46:06)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“A part of this muscle is smooth muscle … controlled by a sympathetic nervous system … superior tarsal muscle is not functioning, but third nerve is intact, so there is not complete ptosis, because [the] skeletal muscle part is intact.”— The Orbit and Its Contents (L20 & L19) · ~L20 00:01:30 / L19 00:40:58
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M08 Eye & Orbit Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Why does a sympathetic lesion give only PARTIAL ptosis, and what are the five features of Horner syndrome?
Only the smooth superior tarsal muscle (sympathetic) fails; the larger skeletal LPS (CN III) still works, so ptosis is partial. Horner = partial ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis, facial flushing, enophthalmos (classic cause: Pancoast tumor compressing the cervical sympathetic chain).
5
Lay out the three coats of the eyeball and the orbital/bulbar fascia — what each coat contains and its clinical hook.
content: L20 · 12:42–32:00
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Name the three coats: outer fibrous (tunica fibrosa), middle vascular (tunica vasculosa), inner nervous (tunica nervosa). (L20 18:47–21:59)
  • Fibrous: sclera (posterior 5/6, white; turns yellow first in jaundice when bilirubin > ~2 mg%) + cornea (anterior 1/6, transparent, avascular, main refractor; a foreign body → pain + photophobia). The perforated lamina cribrosa is where the optic nerve pierces — a classic “which layer?” trap. (L20 22:17–27:16)
  • Vascular: choroid (posterior, feeds the avascular outer coat) → ciliary body (processes secrete aqueous humor; contains the ciliary muscle) → iris (eye color; central aperture = pupil). (L20 27:55–32:00)
  • Add the fascia: periorbita (periosteal lining of the orbital bones) and the bulbar fascia = Tenon’s capsule (separated from the sclera by the episcleral space; left behind to hold an artificial eye after enucleation), plus the medial & lateral check ligaments. (L20 12:42–18:00)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Eyeball is composed of 3 layers … outer, middle, and innermost … the outermost coat is fibrous, tunica fibrosa … composed of sclera … and cornea … cornea is transparent, it forms [the] anterior transparent window for the light rays to enter your eyeball.”— The Orbit and Its Contents contd (L20) · ~00:18:47–00:19:52
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M08 Eye & Orbit Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which coat does the lamina cribrosa belong to, and which three structures make up the vascular coat?
The lamina cribrosa is part of the fibrous coat (perforated sclera where the optic nerve exits) — Maini’s “don’t exclude it” trap. The vascular coat = choroid + ciliary body + iris.
6
Describe the retina & photoreceptors — its cell layers, rods vs cones, and the landmarks (optic disc, macula, fovea, ora serrata).
content: L20 · 35:00–46:00
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Split the retina into an outer pigmented layer (melanin absorbs stray light; deficient in albinism) and an inner neural layer. (L20 36:31–37:35)
  • Chain the three neural layers: rods & cones → bipolar cells → ganglion cells, and it is the axons of the ganglion cells that form the optic nerve (CN II) — not the rods/cones. (L20 39:49–41:38)
  • Contrast the photoreceptors: rods = dim/night vision, no color; cones = bright light, color vision. (L20 38:41–39:44)
  • Place the landmarks: optic disc = where the nerve exits, no photoreceptors = blind spot; the macula lutea (yellow) with its center the fovea centralis, saturated with cones = point of acute vision; the serrated ora serrata is the anterior margin of the retina. (L20 41:48–45:56)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Fovea centralis is the place where cones are maximally located. It is saturated with cones. That means this is the point of acute vision … [the optic disc] lacks photoreceptors … this is called [the] blind spot.”— The Orbit and Its Contents contd (L20) · ~00:42:00–00:43:49
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M08 Eye & Orbit Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which cells’ axons form the optic nerve, and what makes the fovea centralis the point of sharpest vision?
The ganglion cell axons form the optic nerve (rods/cones → bipolar → ganglion). The fovea centralis is saturated with cones, so it gives the sharpest, most detailed (acute) color vision. The optic disc lacks photoreceptors = blind spot.
7
Follow aqueous humor through the chambers — where it is made, its circulation, its drainage, and how glaucoma happens.
content: L20 · 47:00–54:00
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Separate the chambers: the vitreous chamber (behind the lens, holds the gel vitreous body that keeps the eyeball’s shape) vs the anterior and posterior chambers in front of the lens, which communicate through the pupil. (L20 47:07–49:21)
  • Route the aqueous humor: secreted by the ciliary processes into the posterior chamber → through the pupil → anterior chamber. (L20 48:32–49:36)
  • Drain it: absorbed at the iridocorneal angle into the sinus venosus sclerae (canal of Schlemm) → venous blood. Function: nourish the avascular cornea and maintain its curvature. (L20 49:37–53:15)
  • Explain glaucoma: blocked canal of Schlemm or a narrowed iridocorneal angle → aqueous backs up → raised intraocular pressure → retinal degeneration/blindness (open-angle = debris blocks the canal; closed-angle = a chronically acute angle). (L20 50:48–54:09)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Aqueous humor … is formed by the ciliary processes … exits the anterior chamber by draining into sinus venosus sclerae … which is called [the] canal of Schlem[m] … in case this canal is blocked … that will increase intraocular tension, leading to … glaucoma.”— The Orbit and Its Contents contd (L20) · ~00:50:11–00:51:03
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M08 Eye & Orbit Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Trace aqueous humor from where it is made to where it is drained — and what raises pressure in glaucoma?
Ciliary processes → posterior chamber → pupil → anterior chamber → absorbed at the iridocorneal angle into the canal of Schlemm (sinus venosus sclerae) → veins. Glaucoma = blocked canal or narrowed angle → aqueous accumulates → raised intraocular pressure.
8
List the four refractive media and match each refractive error to its corrective lens.
content: L20 · 54:00–63:00
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Name the four refractive media in order: cornea (largely responsible) → aqueous humor → lens → vitreous humor — all must be transparent to focus light on one retinal spot. (L20 54:38–56:14)
  • Myopia (short-sightedness, bigger eye, focus in front of the retina) → concave glasses; common in the young. (L20 60:49–61:15)
  • Hyperopia (farsightedness, smaller eye, focus behind the retina) → convex glasses. (L20 60:35–61:04)
  • Astigmatism (irregular corneal curvature) → cylindrical glasses; plus presbyopia (near-focus loss after ~40), cataract (lens opacity), and asthenopia (eye strain). (L20 61:46–63:13)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Hyperopia … the size of the eyeball is shorter than normal, and light rays are focused behind the retina … you have to wear convex glasses … myopia … light rays are focused in front … to correct myopia, you have to wear concave glasses.”— The Orbit and Its Contents contd (L20) · ~01:00:55–01:01:27
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M08 Eye & Orbit Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
In myopia, where does light focus and which lens corrects it? Name the four refractive media.
Myopia: light focuses in front of the retina → corrected with a concave lens (hyperopia focuses behind → convex). Four media: cornea, aqueous humor, lens, vitreous humor (cornea does most of the refraction).
9
Give the six extraocular muscles — origin, action, innervation (SO4/LR6, rest III), and the “floor of the orbit” board trap.
content: L20 · 01:17–01:32
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Separate them from the intrinsics: 6 extrinsic skeletal muscles (4 recti + 2 obliques, from occipital myotomes, GSE) vs the 3 smooth intrinsic muscles (sphincter/dilator pupillae, ciliary). Recti share a common tendinous ring around the optic canal. (L20 01:17:44–01:20:51)
  • Give the actions: SR up & in, IR down & in, MR adducts, LR abducts, SO down & out (via the trochlea pulley), IO up & out. (L20 01:23:57–01:29:29)
  • Nail the innervation with Maini’s mnemonic: SO4 (superior oblique = CN IV), LR6 (lateral rectus = CN VI), everything else CN III (plus LPS). (L20 01:21:23–01:22:56)
  • Flag the traps: the inferior oblique is the only muscle from the floor of the orbit (Maini: seen on board exams), and her mnemonic “inferior people extort money” (IR + IO = extortion; both superiors = intorsion). (L20 01:27:45–01:30:10)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“It goes SO4, LR6 … all others by third [nerve] … this is the only muscle that originates from the floor of the orbit … I’ve seen this question appearing … in your board exams … inferior people extort money … both inferior rectus and inferior oblique cause extortion.”— The Orbit and Its Contents contd (L20) · ~01:21:23–01:30:05
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M08 Eye & Orbit Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which extraocular muscle originates from the floor of the orbit, and which two muscles are NOT innervated by CN III?
The inferior oblique is the only one from the floor of the orbit (the other five arise near the sphenoid). By SO4 / LR6: the superior oblique (CN IV) and lateral rectus (CN VI) are the exceptions; all the rest (SR, IR, MR, IO + LPS) are CN III.
10
Perform the clinical (H-pattern) testing of the eye muscles — and know why the test position is “opposite” to the action.
content: L21 · 00:00–10:00
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State the principle: to isolate a muscle you line the optic axis up with the muscle’s axis, which is why the test position is opposite to the action — only the abduction/adduction part flips; elevation/depression stays the same. (L21 09:16–09:41)
  • Test the vertical recti by abducting first: eye out, then up = superior rectus, down = inferior rectus. (L21 02:31–05:14)
  • Test the obliques by adducting first: eye in, then up & in = inferior oblique, down & in = superior oblique. (L21 03:25–06:34)
  • Draw two H’s (one per eye, labeled right/left): MR/LR are the easy horizontal tests; recti go on the outside, obliques on the inside — and Maini warns to label the eye so you don’t reverse the direction. (L21 05:47–09:05)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“It is opposite to the actions of the muscles — just the abduction and [adduction] part. [The] elevation-depression part is the same … to test the recti you ask the patient to abduct; to test the obliques you ask the patient to adduct.”— Testing of Eye Muscles … (L21) · ~00:09:16–00:09:41
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M08 Eye & Orbit Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
To isolate the superior rectus, which way do you ask the patient to look first — and why is that “opposite” to its action?
Ask the patient to abduct (look laterally), then up — abducting lines the optic axis up with the SR’s pull so it acts as a pure elevator. Testing flips the abduction/adduction part of the action; the elevation/depression part stays the same. Recti = abduct to test; obliques = adduct to test.
11
Map the cranial nerves of the orbit — CN II fiber type, CN III/IV/VI targets, and the three branches of V1 (NFL).
content: L21 · 13:00–20:00
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Classify the optic nerve (CN II): it carries SSA fibers (special somatic afferent — vision, like hearing, from ectoderm); the central retinal artery runs in its substance. (L21 13:16–14:31)
  • Assign the motor nerves: CN III (oculomotor) enters via the SOF and supplies SR, IR, MR, IO + LPS; CN IV (trochlear) → superior oblique; CN VI (abducent) → lateral rectus. (L21 14:33–16:04, 15:47–16:04)
  • Break down V1 (ophthalmic) with Maini’s mnemonic NFL: Nasociliary, Frontal (→ supraorbital + supratrochlear), Lacrimal (→ lacrimal gland) — all GSA, cell bodies in the trigeminal ganglion. (L21 16:05–16:57)
  • List the nasociliary branches: sensory root to the ciliary ganglion, long ciliary nerves, infratrochlear, and anterior + posterior ethmoidal nerves. (L21 20:00–22:43)

“Frontal gives supraorbital and supratrochlear … lacrimal nerve for the lacrimal gland, nasociliary nerve. So, NFL — National Football League. These are the three branches of V1.”— Testing of Eye Muscles … (L21) · ~00:16:36–00:16:57
🎯 Cement it

🧪 Quick self-check
What fiber type is the optic nerve, and what are the three branches of V1?
The optic nerve carries SSA fibers (special somatic afferent, vision). V1 branches = NFL: Nasociliary, Frontal, Lacrimal (frontal splits into supraorbital + supratrochlear). Their GSA cell bodies sit in the trigeminal ganglion.
12
Trace the arteries & veins of the orbit — ophthalmic artery & its key branches, and where the ophthalmic veins drain.
content: L21 · 10:00–13:00
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Source the supply: the ophthalmic artery is a branch of the internal carotid artery (ICA gives off ophthalmic, then divides into middle & anterior cerebral). (L21 10:03–10:29)
  • Star branch = the central retinal artery, which runs in the substance of the optic nerve and enters at the center of the optic disc (an end artery). (L21 10:29–11:04)
  • List other branches: anterior ciliary (at the corneoscleral junction), short & long posterior ciliary, lacrimal, supraorbital, supratrochlear, anterior & posterior ethmoidal, medial palpebral, dorsal nasal. (L21 11:04–12:35)
  • Drain the orbit: superior ophthalmic vein → directly to the cavernous sinus; inferior ophthalmic vein → pterygoid venous plexus via the IOF; both connect anteriorly with the facial vein (a route for facial infection to reach the cavernous sinus). (L21 12:35–13:09)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Ophthalmic artery is a branch of internal carotid artery … central retinal artery … runs in the substance of [the] optic nerve to enter the eye at the center of [the] optic disc … superior ophthalmic is directly draining into cavernous sinus.”— Testing of Eye Muscles … (L21) · ~00:10:15–00:12:45
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M08 Eye & Orbit Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
The ophthalmic artery is a branch of which vessel, and where does the superior ophthalmic vein drain?
The ophthalmic artery branches from the internal carotid artery; its central retinal artery runs inside the optic nerve. The superior ophthalmic vein drains directly into the cavernous sinus (inferior → pterygoid venous plexus; both link to the facial vein).
13
Wire up the ciliary ganglion & eye autonomics — its three roots, which fibers synapse vs pass through, and the two “2C” muscles.
content: L21 · 24:00–34:00
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Classify it: the ciliary ganglion is a parasympathetic ganglion (lateral to the optic nerve) holding the cell bodies of postganglionic parasympathetic neurons. (L21 25:09–25:43)
  • Parasympathetic root: preganglionic fibers from CN III (oculomotor) SYNAPSE here → postganglionic leave via short ciliary nerves → the 2C muscles: constrictor (sphincter) pupillae (miosis) + ciliary muscle (accommodation). (L21 25:43–27:11)
  • Sympathetic root: postganglionic fibers from the superior cervical ganglion (via the ICA plexus) PASS THROUGH uninterrupted (they already synapsed) → short ciliary nerves (and also long ciliary nerves) → dilator pupillae. (L21 28:24–29:00)
  • Sensory root: GSA fibers from the nasociliary nerve also pass through. So the short ciliary nerves carry all three — GSA, sympathetic, and postganglionic parasympathetic. (L21 29:00–30:07)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“It’s a parasympathetic ganglion … cell bodies of postganglionic parasympathetic … parasympathetic root carries preganglionic fibers from oculomotor nerve … sympathetic root carries postganglionic sympathetic fibers … they pass through the ganglion uninterruptedly.”— Testing of Eye Muscles … (L21) · ~00:25:30–00:30:03
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M08 Eye & Orbit Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which fibers actually SYNAPSE in the ciliary ganglion, and which two muscles do its postganglionic fibers supply?
Only the parasympathetic fibers (preganglionic, from CN III) synapse; the sympathetic and sensory roots just pass through. Its postganglionic fibers (via short ciliary nerves) supply the 2C muscles: constrictor pupillae (miosis) and ciliary muscle (accommodation).
14
Run the eye reflexes — the pupillary light (direct/consensual), accommodation (3 C’s), and corneal reflexes, plus Argyll Robertson.
content: L20 · 01:03–01:17
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Wire the pupillary light reflex: light → retina → optic nerve → pretectal nucleus → Edinger-Westphal nucleus → CN III → ciliary ganglion → sphincter pupillae → constriction. Crossing at the pretectal level gives both a direct (same eye) and a consensual (opposite eye) response. (L20 01:03:26–01:08:30)
  • Use it to localize: an optic nerve (sensory) lesion vs a CN III (motor) lesion predicts exactly which direct/consensual responses are lost. (L20 01:06:31–01:07:55)
  • List the 3 C’s of accommodation (a parasympathetic reflex): Convergence (medial recti), pupillary Constriction, and increased Convexity of the lens (ciliary muscle contracts → suspensory ligament relaxes → lens thickens). (L20 01:10:05–01:13:31)
  • Do the corneal reflex (sensory V1, motor VII → orbicularis oculi blink) and recognize the Argyll Robertson pupil (light reflex lost, accommodation preserved — classically syphilis). (L20 01:03:26–01:16:47, 01:15:50)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“There are three C … convergence … pupillary constriction, and increased convexity of the lens … [the corneal reflex] sensory division is 5th nerve, motor division is 7th nerve … Argyll Robertson pupil — light reflex is lost, but accommodation reflex is preserved … seen in syphilis.”— The Orbit and Its Contents contd (L20) · ~01:10:30–01:16:47
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M08 Eye & Orbit Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
What are the three C’s of the accommodation reflex, and which nerves are sensory vs motor in the corneal reflex?
Accommodation = Convergence, Constriction, Convexity (all parasympathetic, CN III). Corneal reflex: sensory = V1 (ophthalmic), motor = CN VII (orbicularis oculi blink). Argyll Robertson pupil = light reflex lost but accommodation preserved (syphilis).

⚠️ Don’t mix up sympathetic and parasympathetic in the eye. Parasympathetic (CN III, ciliary ganglion, short ciliary nerves) = miosis (sphincter pupillae) + accommodation (ciliary muscle). Sympathetic (superior cervical ganglion, ICA plexus, long ciliary nerves) = mydriasis (dilator pupillae) + the superior tarsal muscle — whose failure gives Horner syndrome. Maini’s memory hook: mydriasis is the longer word (dilation), myosis the shorter (constriction).
