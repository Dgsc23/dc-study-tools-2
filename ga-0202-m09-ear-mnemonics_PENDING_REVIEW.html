Topic 1 — Three ears, one job

External and middle ear just carry the sound; the internal ear is where hearing actually happens.
Where hearing happens
Dr. Maini’s own hook
🎧 RECEPTORS LIVE DEEP INSIDE
The auditory receptors that turn sound-wave vibrations into action potentials sit deep and protected in the internal ear — NOT on the eardrum, ossicles, or in the canal. From there the signal runs to the temporal lobe.
🎙️ Maini: “Internal ear is the place where your receptors are located… those vibrations reach there and are converted into action potentials.”

Job of the outer two
Dr. Maini’s own hook
🚇 OUTER + MIDDLE = THE CONDUIT
The external and middle ear are just a conduit passage: one structure hands the vibration to the next, to the next, until the receptors fire. They hold no receptors of their own.
🎙️ Maini: “External and middle ear is kind of a conduit passage for the vibrations.”

The auricle is one piece of elastic cartilage — bend it and it springs back. The other elastic-cartilage example Maini names is the epiglottis.

Topic 2 — The auricle: parts, nerves & arteries

Maini said she has seen board questions on the named parts — and the nerve/artery lists are classic traps.
Auricle landmark
Dr. Maini’s own hook
🛶 SCAPHA = A BOAT
The outer curved ridge is the helix; running parallel inside it is the antihelix. The boat-shaped groove between helix and antihelix is the scapha.
🎙️ Maini: “Scapha means a boat. It’s like a boat.”
💡 Same root as scaphoid (“boat-shaped”). The long thin trough between the two ridges is your little canoe.

Tragus & the notch
Dr. Maini’s own hook
🎧 TRAGUS OUT FRONT · NOTCH HOLDS AIRPODS
The tragus is the flap immediately anterior to the ear canal; opposite it is the antitragus. The intertragic notch sits between tragus and antitragus.
🎙️ Maini: “What is the function of intertragic notch? To hold your AirPods… nature knew that one day will come when you’ll have to put your AirPods in there.”

Earlobe
Dr. Maini’s own hook
💎 LOBULE = FOR YOUR DIAMONDS
The lobule (earlobe) is the one auricle part with no cartilage — just skin and soft tissue, and it’s very vascular.
🎙️ Maini: “What is the function of lobule? To wear your diamonds.”
💡 “No cartilage” is why an earring pierces the lobule painlessly — and why it bleeds well (very vascular).

Sensory nerves of the auricle
My study hook (illustrative)
🔌 “GALV” SENSORY · FACIAL = MOTOR
Four sensory suppliers: Great auricular (cervical plexus), Auriculotemporal (V3), Lesser occipital (cervical plexus), and the auricular branch of the Vagus (X, deep concha / canal). Separately, the facial nerve (VII) gives MOTOR to the rudimentary auricular muscles.
💡 Get a jolt of “GALV” sensation (Great auricular, Auriculotemporal, Lesser occipital, Vagus). The odd one out — the Facial — doesn’t feel the ear, it moves it.

Arteries of the auricle
My study hook (illustrative)
🩸 3 BRANCHES, ALL OFF THE ECA
The auricle’s three arteries — Superficial temporal, Posterior auricular, and Occipital — are all branches of the external carotid artery (ECA).
💡 “S.P.O. off the ECA.” If a stem lists all three feeding the ear, the single common parent is always the external carotid — not the internal.

Topic 3 — Ear canal & eardrum

The S-shaped tube, the wax trap, and the clock-face landmarks of the tympanic membrane.
External acoustic meatus
Dr. Maini’s own hook
〰️ SINUOUS = S-SHAPED (2.5 cm)
The ear canal is a sinuous (S-shaped) tube about 2.5 cm long. Support: lateral one-third = elastic cartilage, medial two-thirds = temporal bone.
🎙️ Maini: “Sinuous means S-shaped.”
💡 Cartilage on the outside (continuous with the auricle you can wiggle), bone on the inside (the hard part near the drum). Cartilage-out, bone-in.

Cerumen (wax)
Dr. Maini’s own hook
🕸️ WAX + HAIR = A STICKY TRAP-MAT
Ceruminous glands secrete cerumen (wax); with the canal hairs it forms a mat that traps dust, soot, and small insects before they reach the drum.
🎙️ Maini: “Along with the hair, it creates a mat to trap dust particles, soot particles, sometimes tiny insects.”

Ear-canal vagus reflex
Dr. Maini’s own hook
🪥 CLEAN YOUR EAR → TICKLE IN THE THROAT
The auricular branch of the vagus (X) carries GSA from the ear canal. Stimulating it while cleaning the canal fires the vagus and you feel it in your throat (a cough/irritation).
🎙️ Maini: “Sometimes you’re cleaning your… tube, you get this irritating sensation in your throat… this is because of the vagus nerve.”

Tympanic membrane
Dr. Maini’s own hook
🥁 EARDRUM = PEARLY-GRAY, 3 LAYERS
The eardrum is a thin, semi-transparent, oval, pearly-gray membrane with 3 layers: outer skin, middle fibrous core, inner mucous membrane. Sensory: outer surface = auriculotemporal (V3) + vagus; inner surface = glossopharyngeal (IX).
🎙️ Maini: “Semi-transparent, oval, fibrous, pearly-gray membrane… covered externally by thin skin, and internally by mucous membrane, with a core of dense fibrous tissue.”
💡 Skin faces the outside world, mucosa faces the middle-ear cavity, dense fibrous filling in between makes it a drumhead. Different skin vs mucosa = different nerves.

Dr. Maini’s own hook
☂️ UMBO = UMBRELLA (central dimple)
The eardrum’s central depression, where the malleus handle tents it inward, is the umbo.
🎙️ Maini: “It has a central depression called [the] umbo… [it] means umbrella, so it’s not straight, and it has a slight depression in the middle.”

Pars flaccida vs pars tensa
Dr. Maini’s own hook
🎈 TENSA = TENSED (lower) · FLACCIDA = FLACCID (upper)
The name tells you the feel: the inferior, taut majority is pars TENSA; the small superior, lax part is pars FLACCIDA.
🎙️ Maini: “The inferior part is called pars tensa… because the inferior part is tensed… the superior… is kind of flaccid. That’s where it’s called pars flaccida.”

Which quadrant to cut
My study hook (illustrative)
⚠️ POSTERO-SUPERIOR = DANGER · POSTERO-INFERIOR = DRAIN
Behind the posterosuperior quadrant lurks the chorda tympani — don’t cut there. The posteroinferior quadrant is the safe myringotomy site (nothing important behind it).
💡 “High-and-back = harm (chorda); low-and-back = let it out (drain the pus).” Both are posterior; the up/down is what flips danger vs safe.

Topic 4 — The middle-ear box: six walls

Maini turns the tympanic cavity into a cube so you can name all six walls and what each holds.
The six walls
My study hook (illustrative)
📦 ROOF-TILE · JUG-FLOOR · TUBE-FRONT · MASTOID-BACK · MAZE-MEDIAL · DRUM-SIDE
Six walls of the tympanic cavity (in the petrous temporal bone):
• Roof = tegmen tympani (Roof-TILE)
• Floor = roof of the jugular fossa (JUG)
• Anterior = auditory TUBE + tensor tympani openings
• Posterior = MASTOID wall (aditus, pyramid, facial canal)
• Medial = labyrinth / MAZE wall (promontory, oval + round windows)
• Lateral = the eardruM (DRUM)
💡 Read the box like a room: tiled roof up top, jug on the floor, the tube at the front door, the big mastoid out the back, the bony maze on the inside wall, the drum on the outside.

Promontory (medial wall)
Dr. Maini’s own hook
🔨 PROMONTORY = A BUMP PUSHED INTO THE WALL
The promontory is the rounded bulge on the medial wall, raised by the basal turn of the cochlea pressing into it.
🎙️ Maini: “If you just push very hard on a wall, and you create a bump on the wall, it’s like that. That is promontory.”

Dr. Maini’s own hook
🕳️ ANTRUM = A CAVITY (air cells, not solid)
Posteriorly the middle ear opens through the aditus into the mastoid antrum. “Antrum” = cavity: the mastoid is full of air cells, so infection can spread there → mastoiditis.
🎙️ Maini: “Antrum means cavity of the mastoid bone… it has air cells.”

Medial-wall windows
My study hook (illustrative)
🪟 OVAL = STAPES IN · ROUND = SECONDARY DRUM
The medial wall carries two windows: the oval window (fenestra vestibuli) plugged by the stapes footplate, and the round window (fenestra cochlea) closed by the secondary tympanic membrane.
💡 “Oval takes the stapes’ foot; round is the give valve.” The stapes pushes IN at the oval, the round window bulges OUT to relieve the pressure wave.

Topic 5 — Ossicles & the two tiny muscles

The smallest bones in the body, their shapes, their joints, and the two muscles that guard against loud noise.
Ossicle order + shapes
Dr. Maini’s own hook
🔗 M-I-S: MALLEUS · INCUS · STAPES
Three ossicles (tiniest bones in the body), from eardrum inward: Malleus (hammer) → Incus (anvil) → Stapes (stirrup). The malleus is the largest and its handle grips the eardrum.
🎙️ Maini: “M-I-S, MES, or SIM, whatever you want to say… in that order.” (malleus, incus, stapes)
💡 A little workshop: swing the hammer, hit the anvil, kick the stirrup. That’s the order the vibration travels.

Stapes = stirrup
Dr. Maini’s own hook
🐴 STAPES = STIRRUP; ITS FOOT SITS IN THE OVAL WINDOW
The stapes has a head, neck, two crura, and a footplate that sits in the oval window. Its shape is a horse-rider’s stirrup. The muscle attached to its neck is named after it: stapedius.
🎙️ Maini: “When you’re going for horse riding, that is how you stabilize your feet inside the stirrup… that’s why its name is stapedius.”

Ossicle joints
My study hook (illustrative)
🪑 M-I = SADDLE · I-S = BALL-&-SOCKET
The incudomalleolar joint (malleus–incus) is a saddle joint; the incudostapedial joint (incus–stapes) is a ball-and-socket joint.
🎙️ Maini (states the facts): “Incus-malleolar joint is a saddle joint, and [incudo]stapedial joint is a ball-and-socket joint.”
💡 The hook is mine: move outward along the chain and the joint gets freer — saddle first, then the roomier ball-and-socket at the stapes.

The two middle-ear muscles
My study hook (illustrative)
💪 TENSOR TYMPANI = V3 tenses the drum · STAPEDIUS = 7 stops the stapes
Tensor tympani: from the auditory-tube cartilage, nerve V3 (1st arch); it draws the eardrum medially to tense it. Stapedius: from the pyramid, nerve VII; it dampens the stapes by pulling its head posteriorly.
💡 Two number tricks: Tensor Tympani → T… think trigeminal V3 (jaw muscles’ nerve). Stapedius → Seven (S = 7th nerve). Each muscle guards the ossicle chain from its own end.

Protective reflex
Dr. Maini’s own hook
🎚️ LOUD (DJ ROOM) → MUSCLES CLAMP (auditory reflex)
In loud surroundings both muscles reflexively contract — the auditory (acoustic) reflex — stiffening the chain so violent vibrations don’t reach the delicate receptors. Lose stapedius (facial-nerve lesion) and you get hyperacusis.
🎙️ Maini: “You are in a DJ room… your stapedius muscle contracts, and prevents that transmission of those noises.”

Topic 6 — The auditory (pharyngotympanic) tube

One tube, three names, and one job that has nothing to do with hearing.
Three names, one tube
My study hook (illustrative)
🏷️ AUDITORY = PHARYNGOTYMPANIC = EUSTACHIAN
One tube, three names: auditory = pharyngotympanic = eustachian. It runs from the middle ear to the nasopharynx, and its job is pressure equalization across the eardrum.
💡 Say all three names in your head every time you meet it (Maini’s advice): A-P-E — Auditory, Pharyngotympanic, Eustachian.
bank Q57, Q58, Q59 · Maini L22 38:47
Why your ears pop
Dr. Maini’s own hook
✈️ PLANE POPS YOUR EARS → YAWN TO EQUALIZE
When cabin pressure changes on takeoff/landing, the two sides of the eardrum go out of balance. Yawning or a Valsalva forces air through the tube to equalize the pressure, and hearing clears.
🎙️ Maini: “What happens when your plane is taking off?… you do the Valsalva maneuver, or you just yawn… you equalize the pressure on the two sides of tympanic membrane.”
Maini L22 32:46 · bank Q108
Tube support & guard
My study hook (illustrative)
🦴 1/3 BONE, 2/3 CARTILAGE · TUBAL TONSIL AT THE DOOR
The tube is 3.5–4 cm: posterior one-third bony, anterior two-thirds cartilaginous. Lymphoid tubal tonsil guards its nasopharyngeal opening. Block it (long flu) → a nasal twang.
💡 Mirror-image of the ear canal: the canal is cartilage-out/bone-in, the tube is bone-back/cartilage-front. Don’t mix them up.
bank Q60, Q61, Q109
Classic confusion
Dr. Maini’s own hook
🚫 AUDITORY TUBE ≠ EAR CANAL
The name is misleading: the auditory tube is for pressure, not sound. Don’t confuse it with the external acoustic meatus (the canal that actually conducts sound).
🎙️ Maini: “The name can be misguiding. Auditory tube has got nothing to do with hearing… it’s for pressure equalization.”

Topic 7 — Internal ear: labyrinth, cochlea, vestibule & canals

This is the internal-ear detail from the audited bank. Hooks here are my illustrative devices unless a green quote box is shown.
Labyrinth & its two fluids
My study hook (illustrative)
💧 PERI = OUTSIDE (periphery) · ENDO = INSIDE
The internal ear (labyrinth) = a bony labyrinth housing a membranous labyrinth. Perilymph lies between bone and membrane (outside the membranous sac); endolymph fills the membranous labyrinth (inside).
💡 “Peri-meter = around the outside; Endo = inside.” The membranous tube floats inside its bony shell like a balloon (endolymph) in a jar of water (perilymph).
bank Q62, Q63, Q64
Bony labyrinth parts
My study hook (illustrative)
🧩 “C-V-C”: COCHLEA · VESTIBULE · CANALS
The bony labyrinth has three parts: Cochlea (hearing), Vestibule (holds utricle & saccule), and the three semicircular Canals (rotation). Vestibule sits in the middle, cochlea in front, canals behind.
💡 “CVC” — front-to-back: Cochlea, then the central Vestibule, then the Canals. One for pitch, one for still-position, one for spin.
bank Q65, Q66, Q83
Cochlea shape
Dr. Maini’s own hook
🐌 COCHLEA = A SNAIL (core = modiolus)
The cochlea coils ~2.5 turns like a snail shell around a central bony core, the modiolus, which houses the spiral ganglion (first-order hearing neurons).
🎙️ Maini: “Cochlea is something like a snail. It looks like a snail.”
💡 my add-on Snail shell = cochlea, the pillar it winds around = modiolus (“little hub”) holding the ganglion.

Cochlear duct: 3 scalae
My study hook (illustrative)
🥪 TOP + BOTTOM = PERILYMPH · MIDDLE = ENDOLYMPH
Two membranes split the cochlea into three: scala vestibuli (top, perilymph), cochlear duct = scala media (middle, endolymph), scala tympani (bottom, perilymph). Vestibular membrane = roof, basilar membrane = floor of the duct.
💡 A sandwich: two perilymph “bread” layers (vestibuli & tympani) around the endolymph “filling” (scala media). Only the middle carries endolymph.

Organ of Corti
My study hook (illustrative)
👂 HAIR CELLS ON THE BASILAR FLOOR · CILIA IN THE TECTORIAL GEL
The organ of Corti (hearing receptor) sits on the basilar membrane; its hair cells poke cilia into the gelatinous tectorial membrane. As the basilar membrane vibrates, cilia bend against the tectorial roof → action potential.
💡 “Basilar = Base you stand on; Tectorial = Top tent the hairs brush.” Bending the hairs is the trigger.
bank Q72, Q73, Q74, Q82
The 3 receptor organs & their gels
My study hook (illustrative)
🎯 CORTI→TECTORIAL · MACULA→OTOLITHIC · CRISTA→CUPULA
Three inner-ear sense organs, each capped by its own gelatinous membrane: Organ of Corti → tectorial membrane (hearing); Macula → otolithic membrane with otoliths (static/linear); Crista ampullaris → cupula (rotation).
💡 Pair them off: Corti–Tectorial, Macula–Otolithic, Crista–Cupula. Only the macula’s gel carries stones (otoliths).

Vestibule: utricle vs saccule
My study hook (illustrative)
🚗 UtRiCle = BIGGER / horizontal · SaCCule = smaller / vertical
The vestibule holds two sacs for static equilibrium + linear acceleration: the utricle (larger, horizontal macula → horizontal motion, e.g. a car) and the saccule (smaller, vertical macula → vertical motion, e.g. an elevator).
💡 “Utricle = U drive a caR (horizontal); Saccule = Soaring elevator (vertical).” Bigger sac = utricle.

Semicircular canals
My study hook (illustrative)
🔄 3 CANALS AT 90° · SPIN = CRISTA IN THE AMPULLA
Three semicircular canals per ear sit at right angles to each other and sense rotational (dynamic) equilibrium. Each duct swells into an ampulla holding the crista ampullaris, whose hair cells sit in the cupula.
💡 Three canals = the three planes of a spinning head (like x-y-z). The sensor lives in the bulge (ampulla), gel-capped by the cupula.

Canal orientation (board flag)
My study hook (illustrative)
🧭 vs POSTERIOR PETROUS: ANT = 90°, POST = along, LAT = horizontal
Named relative to the posterior surface of the petrous temporal bone: Anterior canal = right angle to it; Posterior canal = along (in line with) it; Lateral canal = horizontal. Maini flagged this as board-style.
💡 “Anterior is Across (90°), Posterior is Parallel, Lateral Lies flat.” Three canals, three orientations.

Static vs dynamic
My study hook (illustrative)
⚖️ maCUla = still & straight · CRIsta = spin
Sort the balance organs: the macula (utricle/saccule) handles static equilibrium + linear motion; the crista ampullaris (canals) handles dynamic / rotational motion.
💡 “Macula for the map (where’s down, am I sliding straight?); Crista for the carousel (am I turning?).”

Topic 8 — Nerves, vessels & clinical

The glossopharyngeal detour, what rides through the internal acoustic meatus, and the exam-ready clinical calls.
Tympanic plexus pathway (IX)
My study hook (illustrative)
🔀 IX’s DETOUR: TYMPANIC br → PLEXUS → LESSER PETROSAL → OTIC → PAROTID
The tympanic branch of glossopharyngeal (IX) enters the middle ear, forms the tympanic plexus over the promontory, then leaves as the lesser petrosal nerve to the otic ganglion — ultimately (via auriculotemporal) to the parotid gland.
💡 IX “had no business” in the ear (Maini) — the middle ear was just on its way to the parotid. Chain: tympanic → plexus → lesser petrosal → otic → parotid.

Internal acoustic meatus
My study hook (illustrative)
🚪 IAM = 7 & 8 + LABYRINTHINE VESSELS
The internal acoustic meatus (on the posteromedial petrous surface) transmits CN VII (facial), CN VIII (vestibulocochlear), and the labyrinthine artery & vein. The facial nerve then runs on in the facial canal.
💡 “7 & 8 go on a date through the IAM” — plus their little blood supply (labyrinthine vessels). VIII is hearing + balance; VII motors the face.

Which test, which function
Dr. Maini’s own hook
🌡️ CALORIC (warm/cold water) = BALANCE · WEBER/RINNE = HEARING
The caloric test (irrigating the ear with warm and cold water) checks vestibular function; Weber’s and Rinne’s are hearing tests.
🎙️ Maini: “In [the] caloric test, you irrigate your ear with warm and cold water… [it’s] the test of vestibular function.”
bank Q104, Q105
Myringotomy vs spontaneous rupture
My study hook (illustrative)
🩹 PLANNED CUT HEALS CLEAN · RAGGED TEAR HEALS BAD
A myringotomy drains a middle-ear collection through a clean cut in the posteroinferior quadrant and heals without scar. A spontaneous rupture has serrated (ragged) margins and may not heal properly — a ruptured/thickened/fibrosed drum causes hearing impairment.
💡 “Straight edge = good, torn edge = bad.” That’s why you drain early with a neat cut instead of waiting for the pressure to blow a jagged hole.
