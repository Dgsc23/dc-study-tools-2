Topic 1 — Dr. Maini’s OWN mnemonics (quoted from lecture)

These are hers, in her words. She literally hands each one to the class — learn them exactly as she says them.
🎓 Maini’s words
Extraocular muscle innervation
SO4 · LR6 · ALL OTHERS = 3
Superior Oblique → CN IV (trochlear). Lateral Rectus → CN VI (abducens). Every other extraocular muscle — superior rectus, inferior rectus, medial rectus, inferior oblique (and levator palpebrae superioris) — is CN III (oculomotor).
Maini: “I’ll give you a mnemonic to remember the innervation of the muscles … it goes like SO4, LR6 … all others by third nerve.”
💡 Filter out just SO and LR first; whatever is left over all belongs to CN III.
Maini, Lecture 20 (~1:21:16). Bank: “Using the mnemonic SO4, LR6, AO3…”
🎓 Maini’s words
Intorsion vs extorsion
INFERIOR PEOPLE EXTORT MONEY
Both INFERIOR muscles — inferior rectus + inferior oblique — produce EXTORSION (top of the eye rolls outward). Both SUPERIOR muscles — superior rectus + superior oblique — produce INTORSION.
Maini: “Just remember this mnemonic — inferior people extort money. That means both inferior rectus and inferior obliques, they cause extortion.”
💡 “Inferior” muscles extort; so by elimination the two “superior” muscles must do the opposite (intorsion).
Maini, Lecture 20 (~1:29:56). Bank: “Extortion … ('inferior people extort')”
🎓 Maini’s words
Superior oblique action
O FOR OBLIQUE, O FOR OUT
The superior oblique rotates the eyeball DOWN and OUT (down + laterally). Its tendon first threads through the trochlea (a fibrocartilaginous pulley), which is why the pull ends up oblique.
Maini: “It rotates the eyeball down and laterally, so you can remember this as down and out. O for oblique. O for out.”
💡 Pairs with the inferior oblique = up and out: both obliques take the eye OUT; the superior one also takes it down.
Maini, Lecture 20 (~1:26:47). Bank: “What is the action of the superior oblique?”
🎓 Maini’s words
Mydriasis vs miosis
mydriasis has a “D” = DILATION
Mydriasis (the longer word, and it contains a D) = pupillary DILATION = sympathetic (dilator pupillae). Miosis (the shorter word) = pupillary CONSTRICTION = parasympathetic (sphincter pupillae).
Maini: “There is a D in mydriasis, it’s pupillary dilation … mydriasis is a larger word, dilation; miosis is a smaller word, constriction.”
💡 Big word, big pupil (dilation); small word, small pupil (constriction).
Maini, Lecture 20 (~0:36:04). Bank: “the pupillary finding is miosis … what does 'miosis' mean?”
🎓 Maini’s words
Branches of the ophthalmic nerve (V1)
NFL = NATIONAL FOOTBALL LEAGUE
The three branches of the ophthalmic nerve (V1) are Nasociliary, Frontal, Lacrimal. Frontal then splits into supraorbital + supratrochlear; nasociliary is the one that also sends a sensory root to the ciliary ganglion.
Maini: “Frontal, lacrimal, nasociliary nerve — so, NFL. National Football League. These are the three branches. We won.”
💡 NFL “says we won” — Nasociliary, Frontal, Lacrimal are the only three branches of V1 inside the orbit.
Maini, Lecture 21 (~0:16:36). Bank: “Using the mnemonic 'NFL', the three branches of the ophthalmic nerve (V1) are…”
🎓 Maini’s words
Accommodation reflex
3 C’s: CONVERGE · CONSTRICT · CONVEX
Shifting your gaze to a NEAR object triggers three changes: Convergence (eyes adduct via the medial recti), pupillary Constriction, and increased Convexity of the lens (ciliary muscle contracts → suspensory ligaments slacken → lens fattens). All parasympathetic, via CN III.
Maini: “There are three C that happen in an accommodation reflex — convergence … pupillary constriction, and increased convexity of the lens.”
💡 All three start with C, and all three are the parasympathetic “look up close” package.
Maini, Lecture 20 (~1:10:38). Bank: “the three changes ('3 C's') of the accommodation reflex…”
🎓 Maini’s words
Parasympathetic eye muscles
2 C’s: CONSTRICTOR PUPILLAE + CILIARY
The two intraocular muscles driven by parasympathetic fibers (CN III → ciliary ganglion → short ciliary nerves) both start with C: the Constrictor (sphincter) pupillae and the Ciliary muscle.
Maini: “What are the two muscles innervated by parasympathetic fibers? 2C … constrictor pupillae … ciliary muscle.”
💡 The 2 C’s of “close vision”: constrict the pupil, and fatten the lens (ciliary).
Maini, Lecture 21 (~0:26:51). Bank: “Postganglionic parasympathetic fibers … supply which two muscles?”
🎓 Maini’s words
Clinical testing of the eye muscles
DRAW THE H (testing is OPPOSITE the action)
Draw an H per eye (label right vs left). Recti are tested in ABDUCTION (patient looks OUT); obliques are tested in ADDUCTION (looks IN). Rule: testing is OPPOSITE the muscle’s action for the abduct/adduct part; the up/down part stays the same.
Maini: “There is an H figure … just remember one thing, it is opposite to the actions of the muscles — just the abduction and adduction part.”
💡 Superior rectus acts up-and-in, but you test it up-and-OUT (in abduction) so the muscle’s axis lines up with the gaze.
Maini, Lecture 21 (~0:02:43, ~0:09:16). Bank: “To isolate and test the superior rectus during the H-test…”

Topic 2 — Orbit osteology & the 3 doorways

The hardest lists in the module: which bone builds which wall, and what passes through each opening.
💡 my hook
Bones of the 4 walls
ROOF-frontal · FLOOR-maxilla · MEDIAL-ethmoid · LATERAL-zygoma+sphenoid
Roof = orbital plate of frontal + lesser wing of sphenoid. Floor = mostly maxilla (tiny zygomatic + palatine). Medial wall = ethmoid (prominent, air cells) + lacrimal (the papery “papyrus” bone) + frontal + body of sphenoid + maxilla. Lateral wall = frontal process of zygomatic + greater wing of sphenoid.
💡 Bank one prominent bone per wall — Frontal roof, Maxilla floor, Ethmoid medial, Zygoma+Sphenoid lateral. Sphenoid touches every wall except the bulk of the floor.
Facts: Maini, Lecture 19 (~0:02:33–0:06:37). The one-bone-per-wall pairing is my aid.
💡 my hook
Margin (base) of the orbit
F-Z-M ring: Frontal, Zygomatic, Maxilla
The rim (base) of the orbit — the part you can feel around your own eye socket — is made of three bones: frontal (above), zygomatic (lateral), and maxilla (below and medial).
💡 Trace your finger around the socket top → side → bottom: Frontal, Zygomatic, Maxilla.
Maini, Lecture 19 (~0:01:59). Bank: “The base (margin) of the orbit is made up of three bones…”
💡 my hook
The 3 doorways into the orbit
3 GATES: OPTIC CANAL · SUP. FISSURE · INF. FISSURE
(1) Optic canal (lesser wing of sphenoid) → optic nerve (CN II) + ophthalmic artery; to the middle cranial fossa. (2) Superior orbital fissure (between greater & lesser wings of sphenoid) → CN III, IV, VI + the V1 branches + superior ophthalmic vein; to the middle cranial fossa. (3) Inferior orbital fissure (between maxilla & greater wing) → maxillary nerve (V2), inferior ophthalmic vein, infraorbital artery; to the pterygopalatine / infratemporal fossa (NOT the cranial cavity).
💡 Two fissures are slits between sphenoid parts; the canal is a round tube. Only the inferior fissure opens away from the skull cavity.
Maini, Lecture 19 (~0:10:33–0:14:20). Bank: “How many 'doorways' allow structures to enter or leave the orbit…”
💡 my hook
Superior orbital fissure contents
3-4-6 + V1 (NFL) + superior ophthalmic vein
The superior orbital fissure transmits CN III, IV, VI, all three V1 branches (nasociliary, frontal, lacrimal = NFL), and the superior ophthalmic vein. It lies between the greater and lesser wings of the sphenoid and communicates with the middle cranial fossa.
💡 Every nerve that moves the eye (3, 4, 6) plus the sensory NFL, all pouring through one slit.
Maini, Lecture 19 (~0:11:42–0:12:45). Ties to Maini’s own NFL mnemonic above.

Topic 3 — Eyelid & lacrimal apparatus

Two ordered lists (lid layers, tear pathway) plus the gland-and-lump vocabulary and Horner’s.
💡 my hook
Layers of the eyelid (outer → inner)
Skin → Muscle → LPS tendon → Tarsal plate → Conjunctiva
Surface to deep: (1) skin, (2) superficial fascia with orbicularis oculi, (3) tendon of levator palpebrae superioris, (4) tarsal plate (holds the tarsal / Meibomian glands), (5) palpebral conjunctiva.
💡 Peel the lid: cover (skin) → closer-muscle (orbicularis) → opener-tendon (LPS) → stiff cardboard (tarsal plate) → wet lining (conjunctiva).
Maini, Lecture 19 (~0:31:33–0:34:49). Bank: “Dissecting the eyelid from superficial to deep…”
💡 my hook
Three eyelid glands (and their lumps)
ZEIS + MOLL at the margin · MEIBOMIAN in the plate
Margin glands: glands of Zeis (sebaceous, on the lashes) and glands of Moll (sweat). Deep in the tarsal plate: the tarsal / Meibomian glands (sebaceous; keep closed lids airtight). Inflamed margin gland = external hordeolum (stye). Inflamed Meibomian gland = internal hordeolum / chalazion (lump on the body of the lid, often needs scooping).
💡 Margin lumps (Zeis/Moll) show on the edge; a chalazion sits back on the lid because the Meibomian gland is buried in the plate.
Maini, Lecture 19 (~0:21:16–0:24:50); Lecture 20 (~0:13:44).
💡 my hook
Tear (lacrimal) drainage pathway
GLAND → LAKE → PUNCTUM → CANALICULI → SAC → DUCT → NOSE
Lacrimal gland → ducts spread tears over the eye → tears pool in the lacrimal lake at the medial angle → lacrimal punctum (on each papilla) → lacrimal canaliculi (superior + inferior) → lacrimal sac → nasolacrimal duct → inferior meatus of the nasal cavity. That is why crying makes your nose run.
💡 Tears drain like a sink: the punctum is the drain hole in the medial corner, the nasolacrimal duct is the pipe to the nose.
Maini, Lecture 19 (~0:28:02–0:30:08); Lecture 20 (~0:06:04). Bank: “Which sequence correctly traces tear drainage…”
💡 my hook
Horner syndrome (sympathetic failure)
5 SIGNS: Ptosis · Miosis · Anhidrosis · Flushing · Enophthalmos
One-sided sympathetic failure (classically a Pancoast tumor at the lung apex compressing the cervical sympathetic chain) gives: partial Ptosis (superior tarsal muscle fails), Miosis (unopposed constriction), Anhidrosis (no sweating), Flushing (vasodilation), and Enophthalmos (sunken eye). Ptosis is only partial because CN III still runs the skeletal part of LPS.
💡 Sympathetic = “fight or flight”; lose it on one side and that side goes droopy-lidded, small-pupiled, dry, flushed, and sunken.
Maini, Lecture 19 (~0:40:08–0:46:04); Lecture 20 (~0:02:00). Bank: “Which finding is NOT part of Horner syndrome…”

Topic 4 — Eyeball coats & the retina

Three nested coats, the parts of the uvea, and the retina’s signal chain.
💡 my hook
Three coats of the eyeball
FIBROUS → VASCULAR → NERVOUS (outer → inner)
Outer fibrous (tunica fibrosa) = sclera (posterior 5/6) + cornea (anterior 1/6). Middle vascular (tunica vasculosa / uvea) = choroid + ciliary body + iris. Inner nervous (tunica nervosa) = retina.
💡 Three nested balls: tough white shell (fibrous), blood-rich curtain (vascular), the camera film (nervous / retina).
Maini, Lecture 20 (~0:18:47–0:21:20). Bank: “The outer fibrous coat … consists of which two parts?”
💡 my hook
Middle (vascular) coat = 3 parts
Choroid → Ciliary body → Iris
Back to front, the uvea is choroid (largest, vascular; feeds the avascular outer layer), ciliary body (ciliary processes secrete aqueous humor; ciliary muscle for accommodation), and iris (colored, central pupil; sphincter + dilator pupillae).
💡 One continuous drape: dark lining (choroid) → fluid-making fringe (ciliary body) → adjustable diaphragm (iris).
Maini, Lecture 20 (~0:20:00–0:32:00). Bank: “The middle vascular coat … is composed of which three structures?”
💡 my hook
Retina signal chain
RODS/CONES → BIPOLAR → GANGLION (axons = CN II)
Light hits the photoreceptors (rods + cones) → they synapse on bipolar cells → bipolar cells synapse on ganglion cells → the axons of the ganglion cells form the optic nerve (CN II). The signal runs “backward” through the retina toward the front-facing photoreceptors.
💡 A three-relay chain, and only the LAST relay (ganglion cells) sends the wire (optic nerve) out of the eye.
Maini, Lecture 20 (~0:37:47–0:41:38). Bank: “The optic nerve (CN II) is formed by the axons of which retinal cells?”
💡 my hook
Rods vs cones, disc vs macula
RODS = night / no color · CONES = bright / color
Rods = dim-light / night vision, no color. Cones = bright light + color, packed at the fovea centralis (point of sharpest vision) inside the macula lutea. The optic disc is where CN II exits — no photoreceptors — so it is the blind spot.
💡 coNES = coloR at the fovea; the disc is a hole in the film, so it is blind.
Maini, Lecture 20 (~0:38:05–0:44:13). Bank: “Which photoreceptors … night vision … color vision…”

Topic 5 — Aqueous humor & optics

The fluid loop that fails in glaucoma, the refractive media, and the error-of-refraction pairs.
💡 my hook
Aqueous humor circulation
CILIARY PROCESSES → POST. CHAMBER → pupil → ANT. CHAMBER → CANAL OF SCHLEMM
Ciliary processes secrete aqueous humor into the posterior chamber → it flows through the pupil into the anterior chamber → drains at the iridocorneal angle into the sinus venosus sclerae (canal of Schlemm) → venous blood. Block the canal → pressure rises → glaucoma.
💡 A fountain: made at the back fringe, flows through the pupil doorway, drains at the corner gutter (Schlemm). Clog the gutter and the eye floods (glaucoma).
Maini, Lecture 20 (~0:47:02–0:51:03). Bank: “Which sequence correctly describes normal aqueous humor circulation?”
💡 my hook
Refractive media (order of light)
CORNEA → AQUEOUS → LENS → VITREOUS
Light bends through four clear media in order: cornea (does most of the refracting), aqueous humor, lens, vitreous body. All four must stay transparent to see.
💡 Four windows in a row — and the cornea (not the “lens”) is the main refractor.
Maini, Lecture 20 (~0:54:52–0:56:08). Bank: “In the correct order, through which refractive media does light pass…”
💡 my hook
Errors of refraction
MYOPIA-long / concave · HYPEROPIA-short / convex
Myopia (near-sighted): eyeball too LONG, image focuses in front of the retina, fix with a CONCAVE lens (common in the young). Hyperopia (far-sighted): eyeball too SHORT, image focuses behind the retina, fix with a CONVEX lens. Astigmatism: irregular corneal curvature, cylindrical lens. Presbyopia: age >40, near focus lost, reading glasses.
💡 “myOpia = eye tOO lOng, needs a scOOped-out (concave) lens.” Hyperopia is the opposite on every count.
Maini, Lecture 20 (~1:00:15–1:02:40). Bank: “In myopia, where is the image focused and what lens corrects it?”

Topic 6 — Extraocular muscles (actions & origins)

The six-muscle action grid, the common origin, and the floor-of-the-orbit board trap.
💡 my hook
Actions of the 6 extraocular muscles
SR up-in · IR down-in · MR in · LR out · SO down-out · IO up-out
Superior rectus = up + in. Inferior rectus = down + in. Medial rectus = adduct (in). Lateral rectus = abduct (out). Superior oblique = down + out. Inferior oblique = up + out.
💡 Recti are the “in” family, obliques the “out” family; “superior” means up for the rectus but down for the oblique — a deliberate flip. (See Maini’s “O for oblique, O for out.”)
Maini, Lecture 20 (~1:23:39–1:29:29). Bank: “What is the action of the superior rectus?” etc.
💡 my hook
Common origin of the recti
4 RECTI = one ring around the optic canal
All four recti share a common tendinous ring (annulus) around the optic canal (sphenoid), then diverge forward to insert on the sclera. The superior oblique starts from the body of the sphenoid; the inferior oblique from the orbital floor.
💡 Four straps radiating forward from one back ring, like tent guy-lines from a single peg.
Maini, Lecture 20 (~1:20:19–1:21:05). Bank: “The four recti of the eye share a common origin. From where…”
💡 my hook
Board trap: floor of the orbit
INFERIOR OBLIQUE = the ONLY muscle from the FLOOR
Five of the six extraocular muscles (the 4 recti via the common ring + the superior oblique) arise from the sphenoid near the optic canal. The inferior oblique is the only one that originates from the floor of the orbit (medial part). Maini flagged this as a repeated board question.
💡 The odd one out is the low one: inferior oblique starts on the floor, everyone else starts at the back (sphenoid).
Maini, Lecture 20 (~1:27:38–1:28:01). Bank: “Which extraocular muscle is the ONLY one that originates from the floor of the orbit…”

Topic 7 — Nerves, vessels & the ciliary ganglion

The vessel source, the venous exit, and the three-root logic that trips everyone up.
💡 my hook
Ophthalmic artery & central retinal artery
ICA → OPHTHALMIC A. → central retinal a. (end artery)
The ophthalmic artery is a branch of the internal carotid artery. Its central retinal artery runs inside the optic nerve and enters at the center of the optic disc — an end artery, so a blockage blinds. Other branches: anterior / posterior ciliary, lacrimal, supraorbital, supratrochlear, ethmoidal, palpebral, dorsal nasal.
💡 The central retinal artery hitches a ride inside the optic nerve; being an end artery, there is no backup if it clogs.
Maini, Lecture 21 (~0:10:15–0:12:35). Bank: “The ophthalmic artery is a branch of which vessel?”
💡 my hook
Where the orbit drains
SUP. + INF. OPHTHALMIC VEINS → CAVERNOUS SINUS
Two ophthalmic veins drain the orbit. The superior ophthalmic vein drains directly to the cavernous sinus; the inferior drains via the inferior orbital fissure to the pterygoid venous plexus. Anteriorly both link to the facial vein — a route for facial infection to reach the cavernous sinus.
💡 The orbit’s back door opens onto the cavernous sinus, its front door onto the facial vein — the “danger triangle” connection.
Maini, Lecture 21 (~0:12:35–0:13:09). Bank: “The two ophthalmic veins ultimately drain principally into…”
💡 my hook
Ciliary ganglion: 3 roots
PARASYMP synapses · SYMP & SENSORY pass through
Parasympathetic (motor) root: preganglionic from CN III → SYNAPSES here → postganglionic via short ciliary nerves to the 2 C’s (constrictor pupillae + ciliary muscle). Sympathetic root (from the superior cervical ganglion via the ICA plexus) and sensory root (from nasociliary / V1) both PASS THROUGH without synapsing. Short ciliary nerves therefore carry all three fiber types.
💡 Only the parasympathetic fibers “get off the bus” (synapse) at this stop; sympathetic and sensory fibers just ride through.
Maini, Lecture 21 (~0:25:01–0:30:09). Bank: “How do sympathetic fibers behave at the ciliary ganglion?”
💡 my hook
Pupil control (two muscles)
SPHINCTER = para = MIOSIS · DILATOR = symp = MYDRIASIS
Sphincter pupillae (circular iris fibers) = parasympathetic (CN III) = miosis (constriction). Dilator pupillae (radial fibers) = sympathetic = mydriasis (dilation). (Recall Maini’s tag: mydriasis has a “D.”)
💡 The circular muscle cinches the pupil shut like a drawstring; the radial muscle pulls it open like spokes.
Maini, Lecture 20 (~0:33:10–0:36:04). Bank: “The circular fibers of the iris form which muscle…”
💡 my hook
Two reflexes to keep straight
LIGHT: II in, III out · CORNEAL: V1 in, VII out
Pupillary light reflex — afferent CN II → pretectal nucleus → Edinger-Westphal → CN III → ciliary ganglion → sphincter pupillae (constriction). Shining one eye also constricts the other = consensual reflex (crossover at the pretectal nucleus). Corneal (blink) reflex — afferent CN V1 (ophthalmic), efferent CN VII (orbicularis oculi).
💡 Light reflex “sees” with II and “acts” with III; corneal reflex “feels” with V and “blinks” with VII.
Maini, Lecture 20 (~1:03:26–1:08:03, ~1:16:22). Bank: “In the corneal reflex, which nerves form the sensory … and motor limbs?”
💡 my hook
Argyll Robertson pupil
ACCOMMODATES but does NOT REACT (syphilis)
Argyll Robertson pupil: the light reflex is LOST but the accommodation reflex is PRESERVED. Classically seen in syphilis. Both are CN III functions, so the dissociation is the exam point.
💡 “Accommodates but does not react” — the pupil still shrinks for near focus, but ignores a shining light.
Maini, Lecture 20 (~1:15:03–1:15:45). Bank: “Argyll Robertson pupil … is classically associated with which disease?”
