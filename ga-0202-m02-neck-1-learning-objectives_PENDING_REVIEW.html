1
Lay out the posterior triangle of the neck — its boundaries, its two subdivisions, and its marquee contents.
content: Lecture 3 · Posterior triangle (+ L05 landmarks)
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Give the boundaries: sternocleidomastoid anteriorly, trapezius posteriorly, the clavicle as the base. (L03 · boundaries)
  • Split it: the inferior belly of omohyoid divides it into the occipital triangle and the supraclavicular (subclavian) triangle. (L03 · subdivisions)
  • Place the brachial plexus: its trunks emerge between the anterior and middle scalene and lie POSTERIOR to the anterior scalene; roots = ventral rami of C5–T1. (L03 · brachial plexus)
  • Name a marquee content: the spinal accessory nerve (CN XI) runs through the posterior triangle. (L05 · contents)

“In your quiz I’m going to ask which structure is anterior or posterior to anterior scalene. Brachial plexus is posterior.”— Dr. Maini · Lecture 3 (Cervical Triangles & Blood Vessels)

🧪 Quick self-check
Which two muscles and which bone bound the posterior triangle, and does the brachial plexus lie anterior or posterior to the anterior scalene?
SCM (anterior), trapezius (posterior), clavicle (base). The brachial plexus lies POSTERIOR to the anterior scalene (its trunks emerge between the anterior & middle scalene). The inferior belly of omohyoid splits the triangle into occipital + supraclavicular.
2
Map the cervical plexus — its roots, the four cutaneous branches, and its muscular branches.
content: Lecture 3 · Cervical plexus
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State the roots: ventral rami of C1–C4; the plexus relates to the internal jugular vein within the carotid sheath. (L03 · formation)
  • Name the four cutaneous branches — lesser occipital, greater auricular, transverse cervical, supraclavicular — all emerging from the nerve point of the neck (Erb’s point) at the mid-posterior border of SCM (greater auricular heads up toward the ear). (L03 · cutaneous branches)
  • Break down supraclavicular into its 3 groups: medial, intermediate, lateral. (L03 · supraclavicular)
  • Give the muscular branches: the phrenic nerve (C3, C4, C5 — “keep you alive”), and C2–C4 fibers that hitch onto CN XI to reach SCM & trapezius. (L03 · muscular branches)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Phrenic nerve … root value? 3, 4, 5, to keep you alive. It’s a branch of cervical plexus.”— Dr. Maini · Lecture 3
🎯 Cement it
📄 GA 0202 · Neck Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Name the four cutaneous branches of the cervical plexus and the single point they all emerge from; give the phrenic root value.
Lesser occipital, greater auricular, transverse cervical, supraclavicular — all from the nerve point of the neck (Erb’s point) at the mid-posterior border of SCM. Phrenic = C3, C4, C5.
3
Explain the ansa cervicalis — its two roots and exactly what it innervates (with the one exception).
content: Lecture 3 · Ansa cervicalis
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Trace the superior root: C1 fibers that travel with the hypoglossal nerve (CN XII), then separate from it. (L03 · superior root)
  • Trace the inferior root: mostly C2 and C3 (a few C4). (L03 · inferior root)
  • State what the loop does: it supplies most of the infrahyoid (strap) muscles. (L03 · function)
  • Lock in the exception: the thyrohyoid does NOT come from the ansa — it gets C1 fibers carried by CN XII directly (the hypoglossal itself exits via the hypoglossal canal). (L03 · exception)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Superior root is a branch from C1 that joins the hypoglossal nerve and then separates from it.”— Dr. Maini · Lecture 3
🎯 Cement it
📄 GA 0202 · Neck Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
What fibers form the superior vs inferior root of the ansa cervicalis, and which infrahyoid muscle is the exception to ansa innervation?
Superior root = C1 (carried by CN XII); inferior root = mostly C2–C3. The ansa supplies most infrahyoids; the thyrohyoid is the exception (C1 via CN XII).
4
Draw the anterior triangle and its four subdivisions — boundaries and key contents of each.
content: Lecture 3 · Anterior triangle
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Give the boundaries: midline of the neck, anterior border of SCM, and lower margin of the mandible; the bellies of digastric + omohyoid carve it into four. (L03 · boundaries)
  • Submental: midline / anterior belly of digastric / body of the hyoid — contains submental lymph nodes and the start of the anterior jugular vein. (L03 · submental)
  • Digastric (submandibular): anterior belly / posterior belly of digastric / lower mandible — contains the submandibular (salivary) gland. (L03 · digastric)
  • Carotid: posterior belly of digastric / SCM / superior belly of omohyoid — contains the common carotid bifurcation and CN IX, X, XII. Muscular: midline / superior belly of omohyoid / anterior SCM — contains the strap muscles + thyroid, larynx, trachea, esophagus. (L03 · carotid + muscular)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“You can actually draw an anterior triangle and divide it into submental, digastric, carotid, and muscular.”— Dr. Maini · Lecture 3
🎯 Cement it
📄 GA 0202 · Neck Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Name the four subdivisions of the anterior triangle and the gland found in the digastric triangle.
Submental, digastric (submandibular), carotid, muscular. The digastric triangle contains the submandibular salivary gland.
5
Answer the “common boundary” questions — which single muscle is shared between adjacent triangles.
content: Lecture 3 · Common boundaries (Maini quiz items)
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Submental & digastric share the anterior belly of digastric. (L03)
  • Carotid & digastric share the posterior belly of digastric. (L03)
  • Muscular & carotid share the superior belly of omohyoid. (L03)
  • Know why she drills these: she tells you to practice drawing the anterior triangle so you can answer “which muscle forms the common boundary between X and Y?” on the quiz. (L03 · study advice)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Which muscle forms a common boundary between carotid triangle and digastric triangle? Posterior belly of digastric.”— Dr. Maini · Lecture 3
🎯 Cement it
📄 GA 0202 · Neck Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which muscle forms the common boundary between the submental and digastric triangles, and which between the muscular and carotid triangles?
Submental/digastric = anterior belly of digastric; muscular/carotid = superior belly of omohyoid. (Carotid/digastric = posterior belly of digastric.)
6
Detail the four suprahyoid muscles — their attachments and (the graded part) their nerve supply.
content: Lecture 3 · Suprahyoid muscles
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State the shared action: elevate the hyoid (key for swallowing). All develop from pharyngeal arches, so most carry SVE fibers. (L03 · action / fibers)
  • Digastric: posterior belly from the mastoid notch (temporal bone), anterior belly to the digastric fossa of the mandible — a “composite” muscle: posterior belly = facial nerve (CN VII), anterior belly = nerve to mylohyoid (inferior alveolar, from V3). (L03 · digastric)
  • Stylohyoid: from the styloid process, pierced by the digastric’s intermediate tendon, supplied by CN VII (the “F for facial” pair with the posterior belly of digastric). (L03 · stylohyoid)
  • Mylohyoid: from the mylohyoid line, forms the floor of the mouth, anterior fibers meet at a fibrous raphe (symphysis menti to hyoid), nerve to mylohyoid. Geniohyoid: from the genial tubercle, sits on top of mylohyoid, supplied by C1 via CN XII. (L03 · mylohyoid + geniohyoid)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Posterior belly by facial nerve, the 7th nerve … anterior belly by nerve to mylohyoid.”— Dr. Maini · Lecture 3
🎯 Cement it
📄 GA 0202 · Neck Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which nerve supplies the posterior belly of the digastric, and which supplies the anterior belly?
Posterior belly = facial nerve (CN VII); anterior belly = nerve to mylohyoid (branch of inferior alveolar, from V3). Stylohyoid is also CN VII (“F for facial”); geniohyoid = C1 via CN XII.
7
Detail the four infrahyoid (strap) muscles — their action, the omohyoid quirk, and the thyrohyoid nerve exception.
content: Lecture 3 · Infrahyoid muscles
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State the shared action: depress the hyoid; most are supplied by the ansa cervicalis. Sternohyoid & sternothyroid sit in the muscular triangle. (L03 · action / location)
  • Omohyoid: the only strap with a scapular attachment (superior border of scapula); its intermediate tendon is slung to the clavicle and first rib. (L03 · omohyoid)
  • Sternothyroid: inserts on the oblique line of the thyroid cartilage, so it depresses the larynx (never reaches the hyoid); the thyrohyoid continues that line up to the body of the hyoid. (L03 · sternothyroid / thyrohyoid)
  • Lock in the exception: the thyrohyoid’s nerve is C1 carried by CN XII, NOT the ansa cervicalis. (L03 · exception)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“This is the only muscle in the infrahyoid group that originates from scapula.”— Dr. Maini · Lecture 3 (omohyoid)
🎯 Cement it
📄 GA 0202 · Neck Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which infrahyoid muscle attaches to the scapula, and which one is NOT supplied by the ansa cervicalis?
Omohyoid attaches to the scapula (inferior belly). Thyrohyoid is the exception — supplied by C1 via the hypoglossal nerve (CN XII), not the ansa.
8
Work through the carotid system — common & internal carotid, the carotid sheath, and the sinus vs body.
content: Lecture 3 · Carotid arteries, sheath, sinus & body
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Give the origins: the right common carotid is a branch of the brachiocephalic trunk; the left arises directly from the aortic arch. It bifurcates at the superior border of the thyroid cartilage. (L03 · origins / bifurcation)
  • Tell ICA from ECA: only the external carotid gives branches in the neck — the internal carotid gives none, and enters the skull through the carotid canal (petrous temporal bone). (L03 · ICA vs ECA)
  • List the carotid sheath “three musketeers”: common carotid, internal jugular vein, and vagus nerve; after the bifurcation the external carotid leaves the sheath. (L03/L05 · sheath)
  • Split sinus from body: the carotid sinus (start of the ICA) holds baroreceptors (pressure); the carotid body holds chemoreceptors (↑CO2, ↑H+, ↓O2) — both sensed via CN IX (glossopharyngeal). (L03 · sinus / body)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Common carotid artery, internal jugular vein, and vagus … wrapped in a common sheath, the three musketeers.”— Dr. Maini · Lecture 3
🎯 Cement it
📄 GA 0202 · Neck Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
How do you distinguish the internal from the external carotid in the neck, and what three structures ride in the carotid sheath?
The ECA gives branches in the neck; the ICA gives none (it enters via the carotid canal). The sheath’s three musketeers = common carotid artery, internal jugular vein, and vagus nerve.
9
List the eight branches of the external carotid artery — grouped 3 anterior, 3 posterior, 2 terminal — plus the traps.
content: Lecture 3 · External carotid branches
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Give the count: 8 — three anterior, three posterior, two terminal. (L03 · overview)
  • Anterior 3: superior thyroid (to the upper pole of the thyroid), lingual (arises at the tip of the greater horn of the hyoid), facial. (L03 · anterior branches)
  • Posterior 3: ascending pharyngeal, occipital, posterior auricular. (L03 · posterior branches)
  • Terminal 2: superficial temporal (palpable in front of the tragus, with the auriculotemporal nerve) and maxillary (deep to the neck of the mandible into the infratemporal fossa). Trap: the transverse facial artery is a branch of the superficial temporal, NOT the facial. (L03 · terminal + trap)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“3 anterior, 3 posterior, and 2 terminal branches — 8 branches in total.”— Dr. Maini · Lecture 3
🎯 Cement it
📄 GA 0202 · Neck Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
List the three anterior branches and the two terminal branches of the external carotid; which artery does the transverse facial branch off?
Anterior = superior thyroid, lingual, facial. Terminal = superficial temporal, maxillary. The transverse facial artery branches off the superficial temporal (a classic trap — not the facial).
10
Break down the subclavian artery — its three parts, the branches by part, and its relation to the anterior scalene.
content: Lecture 3 · Subclavian artery (+ L05 scalene relation)
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Give the origins & parts: right subclavian from the brachiocephalic trunk, left directly from the aortic arch. The anterior scalene splits it into 3 parts (part 1 medial, part 2 deep/posterior, part 3 lateral) — the artery itself runs POSTERIOR to the anterior scalene. (L03/L05 · parts / scalene)
  • Part 1 gives three: vertebral (up the transverse foramina, then through the foramen magnum), internal thoracic, and thyrocervical trunk (= inferior thyroid + transverse cervical + suprascapular). (L03 · part 1)
  • Part 2 gives the costocervical trunk (= deep cervical + superior intercostal); part 3 usually gives no branch (occasionally a dorsal scapular). (L03 · parts 2 & 3)
  • Nail the relations trap: while the artery is posterior to the anterior scalene, the transverse cervical and suprascapular arteries lie ANTERIOR to it; in thoracic outlet (scalene) syndrome the subclavian artery + brachial plexus get compressed together. (L03 · relations / syndrome)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Part 1 gives off vertebral, internal thoracic, and thyrocervical trunk.”— Dr. Maini · Lecture 3
🎯 Cement it
📄 GA 0202 · Neck Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Name the three branches of the first part of the subclavian artery, and state the artery’s relationship to the anterior scalene.
Part 1 = vertebral, internal thoracic, thyrocervical trunk. The subclavian artery lies POSTERIOR to the anterior scalene (its vein, plus the transverse cervical and suprascapular arteries, lie anterior).
11
Trace the venous drainage of the neck — the subclavian, internal & external jugular veins, and the thyroid-vein trap.
content: Lecture 5 · Neck veins & drainage
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Subclavian vein: passes ANTERIOR to the anterior scalene; it is the continuation of the axillary vein (at the outer border of the first rib) and unites with the IJV to form the brachiocephalic vein. (L05 · subclavian vein)
  • Internal jugular vein: begins at the jugular foramen as the continuation of the sigmoid sinus; runs in the carotid sheath with the common carotid and vagus; drains the brain, face, and neck. (L05 · IJV)
  • External jugular vein: superficial, with essentially one tributary — the anterior jugular vein. (L05 · EJV)
  • Nail the thyroid-vein trap: superior and middle thyroid veins drain into the IJV, but the inferior thyroid vein drains into the brachiocephalic vein. (L05 · thyroid drainage)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Subclavian and internal jugular vein join together to form brachiocephalic.”— Dr. Maini · Lecture 5 (Blood Vessels & Cervical Viscera)
🎯 Cement it
📄 GA 0202 · Neck Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Does the subclavian vein run anterior or posterior to the anterior scalene, and where do the superior, middle, and inferior thyroid veins drain?
The subclavian VEIN runs ANTERIOR to the anterior scalene (the artery is posterior). Superior & middle thyroid veins → IJV; inferior thyroid vein → brachiocephalic vein.
12
Cover the lymphatics and surface landmarks of the neck — the jugular trunks, the worrisome node, and the two supraclavicular fossae.
content: Lecture 5 · Lymphatics & landmarks
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Trace the lymph: head & neck lymph collects into the right and left jugular trunks, which drain into the right lymphatic duct (right side), the thoracic duct (left side), or directly into the brachiocephalic veins. (L05 · jugular trunks)
  • Flag the worrisome node on palpation: painless, firm-to-hard, fixed, matted (think cancer or lymphoma). (L05 · node palpation)
  • Lesser supraclavicular fossa = the depression between the two heads (sternal & clavicular) of the SCM. (L05 · landmarks)
  • Greater supraclavicular fossa = corresponds to the supraclavicular (subclavian) triangle, where the subclavian vessels are found. (L05 · landmarks)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Painless lymph nodes, firm to solid, fixed, sometimes matted … more worrisome, might be cancer or lymphoma.”— Dr. Maini · Lecture 5
🎯 Cement it
📄 GA 0202 · Neck Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which lymph-node qualities on palpation are the worrisome ones, and what lies in the greater supraclavicular fossa?
Painless, firm-to-hard, fixed, and matted nodes are worrisome (cancer/lymphoma). The greater supraclavicular fossa corresponds to the supraclavicular (subclavian) triangle, containing the subclavian vessels.

🔗 How to use these 12 to study

1. Read each objective out loud, then try the self-check before revealing the answer. If you nailed it, tick Got it.

2. For any objective you miss, jump back to the GA 0202 Neck Hub and drill exactly that topic.

3. Dr. Maini flags her quiz items out loud (“in your quiz I’m going to ask…”). The triangle boundaries & common boundaries (Objectives 4–5) and the scalene relations (Objectives 1, 10–11) are where those flagged questions live — spend extra reps there and practice drawing the triangles.

⚠️ Don’t mix up anterior vs posterior to the anterior scalene. Posterior = brachial plexus + subclavian ARTERY. Anterior = subclavian VEIN, plus the transverse cervical & suprascapular arteries. And watch the odd-ones-out: the thyrohyoid is the strap muscle NOT supplied by the ansa (C1 via CN XII); the transverse facial artery branches off the superficial temporal, not the facial; and the inferior thyroid vein drains to the brachiocephalic while the superior & middle thyroid veins drain to the IJV.
