1
Organize the brain into its four major parts — what each does, and the order of the brainstem.
content: L16 · 01:33–04:48
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Cerebrum — the largest, executive part; seat of the higher functions (personality, judgment, memory, motor, sensory). It’s so large it masks all underlying structures except the brainstem. (L16 01:33–02:28)
  • Diencephalon — the connecting part, mostly ascending & descending pathways; contains thalamus, hypothalamus, optic chiasma, pineal body. (L16 02:40–03:32)
  • Cerebellum — the “small brain,” the seat of coordination of movement. (L16 03:32–04:11)
  • Brainstem, superior → inferior = midbrain → pons → medulla oblongata, and the medulla is continuous with the spinal cord. (L16 04:27–04:48)

2
Explain the cortex’s architecture — gray vs white matter, sulcus vs gyrus, and how the two hemispheres are joined.
content: L16 · 05:01–08:37
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • The cerebral cortex is gray matter (neuronal cell bodies at the periphery); the interior is white matter (myelinated fibers — that’s what makes it look white). (L16 07:36–08:37)
  • Sulcus / fissure = a groove; gyrus = a ridge. A groove deeper than ~1 inch gets called a fissure, but the two words are used interchangeably. (L16 06:23–07:02)
  • The longitudinal fissure divides the left from the right hemisphere, and the falx cerebri sits within it. (L16 05:42–06:12)
  • The two hemispheres exchange information through the corpus callosum, a band of fibers running between them. (L16 05:21–05:42; L17 Menti 01:31:18)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“The gray matter … is located within the cerebral cortex … and then on the inside, you have white matter … cell bodies … towards the periphery, and then white matter is what? The fibers.”— The Brain · ~00:07:41–00:08:17
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · Brain Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Where are neuronal cell bodies vs fibers in the cerebrum, and what fissure (and structure inside it) separates the hemispheres?
Cell bodies = gray matter in the peripheral cortex; myelinated fibers = white matter inside. The longitudinal fissure separates the hemispheres and holds the falx cerebri; the corpus callosum connects them. Sulcus/fissure = groove, gyrus = ridge.
3
Divide each hemisphere into its five lobes — the boundaries between them, and the primary cortex each lobe carries.
content: L16 · 10:41–35:04 · L17 Menti
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • List the five lobes: frontal, parietal, temporal, occipital on the surface, and the insula as the deep 5th lobe (found by retracting the lateral fissure). (L16 10:41–10:59, 34:30)
  • Give the boundaries: central sulcus = frontal/parietal border; lateral sulcus = temporal below vs frontal/parietal above; the occipital lobe is set off by a line from the preoccipital notch to the superior end of the parieto-occipital sulcus. (L16 11:21–15:26)
  • Match lobe to primary cortex: frontal = primary motor, parietal = primary sensory, occipital = primary visual, temporal = primary auditory (and olfactory), insula = primary gustatory (taste, plus emotion & visceral sensation). (L16 15:43–18:10, 34:43; L17 Menti)
  • Add the clinical note: the prefrontal cortex (reasoning/judgment) isn’t fully developed until ~25 — why teenagers lack that judgment. (L16 15:57–16:28)

4
Map the motor and sensory cortex — precentral vs postcentral gyrus, the homunculus, and the 2- vs 4-neuron pathways.
content: L16 · 18:45–26:55 · L17 · 09:39
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • The precentral gyrus (just anterior to the central sulcus) = primary motor cortex; the postcentral gyrus (just posterior) = primary sensory / somesthetic cortex (general sensations only). (L16 18:45–24:36)
  • Read the homunculus: areas needing fine control (face, hands) or with dense receptors get a larger representation than big, coarse muscles like the back. (L16 22:42–26:10)
  • Motor is a two-neuron pathway: upper motor neuron in the primary motor cortex → corticospinal tract → lower (alpha) motor neuron in the anterior/ventral horn. (L16 20:01–20:54)
  • Sensory is a four-neuron pathway: ganglion → medulla → thalamus → postcentral gyrus. Just anterior to the motor cortex is the frontal eye field for eye movements. (L16 26:55; L17 09:39–10:24)

“Motor pathway is just two neuron[s] … But sensory pathway is [a] four neuron pathway … second neuron is located in the medulla, third in the thalamus, and fourth in the postcentral gyrus.”— The Brain (contd.) · ~00:09:39–00:10:24
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · Brain Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which gyrus is primary motor vs sensory, and how many neurons are in the motor vs sensory pathway (name the sensory relays)?
Precentral gyrus = motor, postcentral gyrus = sensory. Motor = 2 neurons (cortex → anterior-horn lower motor neuron via the corticospinal tract). Sensory = 4 neurons: ganglion → medulla → thalamus → postcentral gyrus.

6
Distinguish Broca’s from Wernicke’s area — location, function, and the aphasia each lesion produces.
content: L16 · 36:49–41:04
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Broca’s area = frontal lobe, the motor / expressive speech area (finds the words, spoken or written). (L16 36:49–38:22)
  • Wernicke’s area = posterior temporal lobe, the sensory speech area for comprehension (understanding what’s said or read). (L16 37:09–38:31)
  • Broca’s aphasia (motor): patient is short of words but comprehension is intact. (L16 40:00–41:04)
  • Wernicke’s aphasia (sensory): patient speaks fluently but it’s “word salad” with no comprehension — nothing makes sense. (L16 39:19–39:55)

7
Identify the basal nuclei and the three capsules — and why the internal capsule matters clinically.
content: L16 · 08:50–10:23, 01:19:48–01:27:12 · L17 Menti
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Define them: basal nuclei = islands of gray matter within the white matter (“basal nuclei,” not “ganglia” — a ganglion is cell bodies outside the CNS). The three: caudate, putamen, globus pallidus; putamen + globus pallidus = lentiform; all three = corpus striatum. (L16 08:50–10:23)
  • Note the trap: the thalamus is NOT a basal nucleus (it’s diencephalon); the claustrum is the thin strip of gray between the external & extreme capsules. (L16 01:20:07; L17 Menti 01:36:34)
  • Place the three capsules: internal capsule = the L-shaped white matter between caudate/globus pallidus and thalamus; external capsule = between putamen/lentiform and claustrum; extreme capsule = between claustrum and insular gray. (L16 01:23:37–01:27:12; L17 Menti 01:35:40)
  • Explain why it matters: the corticospinal tract runs through the internal capsule, so a lesion/ischemia there causes contralateral paralysis (a very common stroke). A resting tremor points to basal-nuclear dysfunction (e.g. Parkinsonism). (L16 01:24:04, 01:34:46)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Internal capsule is very important … corticospinal pathway, your motor tract … passes through internal capsule … if there is damage on the right side, there’ll be paralysis on the left side of the body.”— The Brain · ~01:24:04–01:25:32
⚠️ Maini’s own quiz (Menti): “Internal capsule is located between…” → caudate nucleus and globus pallidus; and “which is not a basal nucleus?” → thalamus. (Putamen + claustrum = external; claustrum + insular gray = extreme.) (L17 01:35:40–01:36:34)
🎯 Cement it

🧪 Quick self-check
Name the three basal nuclei, say which structure is NOT one, and explain why an internal-capsule stroke paralyzes the opposite side.
Basal nuclei = caudate, putamen, globus pallidus (putamen+GP = lentiform; all three = corpus striatum). The thalamus is NOT one. The corticospinal tract passes through the internal capsule and its fibers cross in the medulla, so a lesion on one side paralyzes the contralateral body.

9
Describe the diencephalon — thalamus as the sensory relay, the hypothalamus, pineal, and the optic chiasma–pituitary relationship.
content: L16 · 50:03–57:36 · L17 · 06:15–13:08
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Thalamus = the relay center for sensory pathways heading to the cortex — the third neuron of the sensory pathway sits here. Only smell (olfaction) bypasses the thalamus. (L16 50:03–51:28)
  • The two thalami are joined by the interthalamic adhesion = massa intermedia = intermediate mass. (L16 51:46–52:10)
  • Hypothalamus = the master of the autonomic & endocrine system — the body’s thermostat, plus thirst, hunger/satiety, sleep-wake, and pituitary control. Pineal gland secretes melatonin (sleep-wake cycle). (L16 55:34–57:36; L17 11:00–12:00)
  • Note the optic chiasma (where the optic nerves cross) sits right by the pituitary — a pituitary tumor compresses the chiasma, giving visual field loss. (Corpora arenacea = normal pineal calcification after ~40.) (L16 54:37–55:21; L17 13:08)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Your thalamus is your London Heathrow. This is where there is a halt, you leave one flight, and start next flight … [the] next neuron in the sensory pathway is located in the thalamus.”— The Brain · ~00:50:11–00:50:39
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · Brain Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which sensation bypasses the thalamus, and why does a pituitary tumor cause visual loss?
The thalamus relays every sensation except smell (olfaction). The optic chiasma sits right above the pituitary, so an enlarging pituitary tumor compresses the chiasma and causes visual field loss. Hypothalamus = autonomic/endocrine thermostat; pineal = melatonin.

Work through the internal brainstem & midbrain — colliculi, the aqueduct/red nucleus/substantia nigra, the pyramids and their decussation, olive, and reticular formation.

12
Explain the cerebellum — arbor vitae, vermis, folia, its plan-vs-performed function, and the signs of a cerebellar lesion.
content: L16 · 59:42–01:00:02, 01:28:34–01:34:41
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Name the structure: the branching gray/white pattern = arbor vitae (“tree of life”); the vermis connects the two cerebellar hemispheres; its folds are folia (“leaves”). (L16 59:42, 01:28:49–01:30:52)
  • State the function: the cerebellum coordinates movement by comparing the planned movement with the performed movement (via spinocerebellar feedback) and refining it — it decides when to stop. (L16 01:32:03–01:33:28)
  • List the lesion signs: intention tremor (the hallmark), finger-nose overshoot, dysarthria, and hypotonia. (L16 01:33:32–01:34:41)
  • Contrast the tremors: intention tremor = cerebellar; resting tremor = basal-nuclear dysfunction. (L16 01:34:36–01:34:51)

13
Trace the ventricular system and the flow of CSF — from choroid plexus to the superior sagittal sinus.
content: L17 · 42:29–56:39
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Name the ventricles: two lateral ventricles (anterior horn in the frontal lobe, posterior horn in the occipital, inferior horn in the temporal); a slit-like third ventricle between the thalami; a diamond-shaped fourth ventricle (pons/medulla in front, cerebellum behind). (L17 43:07–44:01)
  • Link them: interventricular foramen (lateral → 3rd), cerebral aqueduct (3rd → 4th, through the midbrain), then out of the 4th via two foramina of Luschka (lateral) + one foramen of Magendie (median). (L17 44:10–46:08)
  • Say the source & sink: choroid plexus secretes CSF in each ventricle; arachnoid granulations absorb it into the superior sagittal sinus. (L17 02:48, 50:00)
  • Recite the full path: lateral → IVF → 3rd → aqueduct → 4th → subarachnoid space → arachnoid granulations → superior sagittal sinus; a blockage (e.g. meningitis) raises intracranial pressure / causes hydrocephalus. (L17 55:37, 51:57)

14
Map the brain’s blood supply and the Circle of Willis — the two systems, the ICA/vertebral branches, the cortical territories, and why the circle exists.
content: L17 · 57:42–01:14:02
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State the dual supply: internal carotid arteries (anterior) + vertebral arteries (posterior). The brain is ~2% of body weight but takes ~15% of cardiac output. (L17 57:42–58:29)
  • ICA has 4 parts (cervical, petrous, cavernous, cerebral), gives the ophthalmic artery, and ends as the anterior cerebral (ACA) + middle cerebral (MCA) arteries. ACA = medial surface of frontal & parietal lobes; MCA = lateral cortex + insula (the commonest stroke artery). (L17 58:46–01:04:39)
  • Follow the posterior system: the two vertebrals unite into the basilar artery, which ends as two posterior cerebral arteries (PCA); the vertebral is a branch of the subclavian. So the occipital lobe’s blood ultimately comes from the vertebral arteries. (L17 01:06:46–01:11:39)
  • Build the Circle of Willis: PCA + posterior communicating + ICA + ACA + anterior communicating, ringing the optic chiasma / infundibulum / mammillary bodies — it provides an alternate (collateral) supply if one artery blocks. (Dr. Bowie calls it the “Alien Man.”) (L17 01:12:05–01:14:02)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Two vertebral arteries uniting together to form basilar, and then basilar artery dividing again to form posterior cerebral arteries.”— The Brain (contd.) · ~01:08:07

15
Apply the clinical considerations — the three cranial hemorrhages, venous drainage, and the valveless cerebral veins.
content: L17 · 01:15:08–01:20:31
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Epidural hematoma = arterial, from the middle meningeal artery (blow to the pterion / temporal region); develops fast. (L17 01:16:49–01:18:01)
  • Subdural hematoma = venous, from torn bridging veins (common in the elderly with brain atrophy); develops slowly (days to weeks to months). (L17 01:18:07–01:19:03)
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage = mostly arterial, often from a ruptured aneurysm; can block arachnoid granulations and raise ICP. (L17 01:19:40–01:19:57)
  • Trace venous drainage: cerebral veins are valveless (low / negative pressure); everything funnels to the dural venous sinuses, and the sigmoid sinus becomes the internal jugular vein at the jugular foramen. A cavernous-sinus ICA rupture gives pulsatile exophthalmos. (L17 01:15:08, 01:16:00, 01:20:07)

🧪 Quick self-check
The neural tube comes from which germ layer, and what do the metencephalon and myelencephalon become?
The neural tube forms from ectoderm. Metencephalon → pons + cerebellum; myelencephalon → medulla (+ spinal cord). Forebrain: telencephalon → hemispheres, diencephalon → thalamus/hypothalamus/pineal, etc.
