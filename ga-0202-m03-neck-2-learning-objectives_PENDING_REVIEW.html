1
Trace the venous drainage of the neck — the internal jugular vein’s origin, course, companions, and tributaries.
content: L05 · 02:29–07:22
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State that the subclavian vein is the continuation of the axillary vein at the outer border of the first rib, passes anterior to the anterior scalene, and unites with the IJV to form the brachiocephalic vein. (L05 02:34–03:04)
  • Trace the internal jugular vein: the main vein of the head/neck, it begins at the jugular foramen as the continuation of the sigmoid sinus, descends in the carotid sheath with the common carotid artery + vagus nerve (“three musketeers”), and ends by joining the subclavian → brachiocephalic → SVC. (L05 04:05–05:53)
  • Nail the exam item: “Which sinus continues as the IJV?” = sigmoid sinus — every other dural sinus drains into the sigmoid first. (L05 05:01–05:25)
  • List the IJV tributaries: inferior petrosal sinus, occipital, pharyngeal, lingual, facial (common facial), and the superior + middle thyroid veins. The external jugular is superficial and drains far less. (L05 06:03–07:22)

Which dural venous sinus continues as the internal jugular vein, and what two structures share the carotid sheath with it?
The sigmoid sinus continues as the IJV at the jugular foramen. In the carotid sheath it runs with the common carotid artery and the vagus nerve (the “three musketeers”), then joins the subclavian to form the brachiocephalic vein.

Map the neck lymphatics and surface landmarks — the node groups, their drainage, the supraclavicular fossae, and the painful-vs-painless node rule.
content: L05 · 07:29–11:22
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Name the regional node groups (submental, submandibular, preauricular, superior/inferior deep cervical, pre-laryngeal, paratracheal, pre-tracheal); their vessels form the right + left jugular trunks → right lymphatic duct (right side), thoracic duct (left side), or directly into the brachiocephalic veins. (L05 07:29–08:40)
  • Apply Maini’s clinical node rule: a tender/painful node is reassuring (infection/inflammation); a painless node that is firm-to-hard, fixed, and matted is more worrisome — think cancer or lymphoma. (L05 08:51–09:45)
  • Locate the fossae: the lesser supraclavicular fossa sits between the two heads (sternal + clavicular) of the SCM; the greater supraclavicular fossa lies between the trapezius and the posterior border of the SCM. (L05 10:21–10:54)
  • Link surface to deep: the greater supraclavicular fossa overlies the supraclavicular (subclavian) triangle — where the subclavian vessels lie; the spinal accessory nerve runs in the posterior triangle. (L05 10:01–11:22)

🧪 Quick self-check
A painless, firm, fixed, matted cervical node — reassuring or worrisome, and what does a painful node suggest instead?
Worrisome — painless + firm + fixed + matted points to cancer or lymphoma. A painful/tender node is more reassuring: it usually means benign infection or inflammation. Neck lymph ultimately drains via the jugular trunks into the right lymphatic duct / thoracic duct.

Describe the thyroid gland’s gross anatomy and relations — lobes, isthmus, the tracheal rings it crosses, and why posterior enlargement causes hoarseness.
content: L05 · 13:05–21:11
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Lay out the shape: two lobes joined by a midline isthmus, with an inconstant pyramidal lobe hanging off the isthmus (a remnant of the thyroglossal duct). Upper pole reaches the oblique line of the thyroid cartilage (attachment for sternothyroid + thyrohyoid); lower pole to the 4th/5th tracheal ring. (L05 13:44–15:25)
  • Bank the board-exam number: the isthmus crosses the 2nd, 3rd, and 4th tracheal rings — Maini flags the trap (“they’ll ask 1,2,3 / 2,3,4 / 4,5,6 … it’s 2,3,4”). (L05 20:44–21:11)
  • Give the relations: anterolaterally the infrahyoid (strap) muscles (sternothyroid, sternohyoid, superior omohyoid) + anterior SCM; posterolaterally the carotid sheath (common carotid, IJV, vagus); medially the trachea/larynx and esophagus. (L05 17:28–20:07)
  • Explain the red flag: the recurrent laryngeal nerve runs in the tracheoesophageal groove against the posterior thyroid, so a posteriorly-enlarging gland can present as hoarseness before any visible swelling. (L05 16:55–17:13)

🧪 Quick self-check
Which tracheal rings does the thyroid isthmus cross, and why does a posteriorly-enlarging thyroid cause hoarseness?
The isthmus crosses tracheal rings 2, 3, and 4. Posterior enlargement compresses the recurrent laryngeal nerve in the tracheoesophageal groove, so the patient can be hoarse with no visible swelling. The pyramidal lobe is a thyroglossal-duct remnant.

State the thyroid gland’s dual arterial supply and its split venous drainage — and the one vein that drains differently.
content: L05 · 21:20–28:06
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Give the dual arterial supply: the superior thyroid artery (first branch of the external carotid artery) + the inferior thyroid artery (a branch of the thyrocervical trunk, itself off the subclavian artery). (L05 21:20–23:27)
  • Know the “don’t-test” item: an inconstant thyroid ima artery (~10% of people) exists — Maini explicitly says she will not test it. (L05 23:27–24:13)
  • Split the venous drainage: superior + middle thyroid veins → internal jugular vein; the inferior thyroid vein → brachiocephalic vein. (L05 24:13–25:08)
  • Flag it as high-yield: Maini calls this a “very good question” precisely because the inferior vein drains differently from the other two. (L05 27:52–28:06)

Where does each of the three thyroid veins drain, and what are the parent vessels of the two thyroid arteries?
Superior + middle thyroid veins → internal jugular vein; inferior thyroid vein → brachiocephalic vein. Arteries: superior thyroid = first branch of the external carotid; inferior thyroid = branch of the thyrocervical trunk (from the subclavian).
5
Explain thyroid regulation and contrast hypo- vs hyper-thyroidism — the intolerances, cretinism, Graves’ disease, and the role of calcitonin.
content: L05 · 25:23–36:35
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State the control loop: the thyroid needs no nerve stimulation — hypothalamus (TRH) → anterior pituitary (TSH) → thyroid (T3/T4), with negative feedback from T3/T4 switching the hypothalamus/pituitary off. Sympathetic fibers are vasomotor only. (L05 25:23–27:41)
  • Hypothyroidism = slow metabolism: weight gain despite low appetite, bradycardia, cold intolerance, lethargy (“sluggish like a sloth”); in children = cretinism (short stature, intellectual impairment, large tongue). Severe form = myxedema. (L05 28:08–30:32)
  • Hyperthyroidism = fast metabolism: weight loss despite increased appetite, tachycardia, heat intolerance, sweating, tremor, anxiety; exophthalmos only in Graves’ disease (autoimmune — the TSI antibody stimulates the gland → high T3/T4, low TSH). (L05 31:07–34:16)
  • Place calcitonin: secreted by parafollicular C cells (between the colloid-filled follicles); it lowers blood calcium but is minor in humans — PTH is the real calcium regulator. (L05 34:34–36:35)

“They will be very slow, sluggish, like a sloth. Some of the books even say, sluggish like a sloth … [exophthalmos] is seen only in Graves’ disease.”— L05 (Blood Vessels of Neck, Cervical Viscera) · ~00:28:44–00:32:42
🎯 Cement it

🧪 Quick self-check
Cold vs heat intolerance — which is hypo- and which is hyper-thyroid, and which finding is unique to Graves’ disease?
Cold intolerance = hypothyroid (slow, sloth-like, weight gain, bradycardia); heat intolerance = hyperthyroid (weight loss, tachycardia, sweating, tremor). Exophthalmos is unique to Graves’, driven by the TSI antibody (high T3/T4, low TSH). Calcitonin comes from parafollicular C cells.
6
Describe the parathyroid glands and calcium homeostasis — PTH’s actions and the signs of hypocalcemia.
content: L05 · 36:45–44:48
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State the anatomy: four small, pea-sized glands (2 superior, 2 inferior) embedded in the posterior surface of the thyroid, supplied by the superior + inferior thyroid arteries. (L05 36:45–38:43)
  • Give PTH’s job: raises blood calcium by leaching calcium from bone, increasing renal reabsorption, and activating vitamin D (→ more gut/kidney absorption). It is regulated directly by the calcium level, not by nerves. (L05 37:27–39:02)
  • Connect the pathology: hypoparathyroidism (e.g., after total thyroidectomy for carcinoma — the glands can’t be left behind) → severe hypocalcemia → hypocalcemic tetany + carpopedal spasm. (L05 39:26–40:19)
  • Know the two bedside signs: Trousseau’s sign (BP cuff above systolic → carpopedal spasm) and Chvostek’s sign (tap over the facial nerve → facial twitch). Normal Ca ~9–12 mg%; tetany when it drops below ~7. (L05 40:11–44:48)

“The patient will experience hypocalcemic tetany, carpo[pedal] spasm, and these are two signs … you will test for [Trousseau’s] sign and [Chvostek’s] sign.”— L05 (Blood Vessels of Neck, Cervical Viscera) · ~00:40:11–00:40:30
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M03 Neck 2 Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Name the two signs of hypocalcemia and what each one tests — and PTH’s three ways of raising calcium.
Trousseau’s (BP cuff above systolic → carpopedal spasm) and Chvostek’s (tap the facial nerve → facial twitch). PTH raises calcium by pulling it from bone, increasing renal reabsorption, and activating vitamin D. Four glands sit on the posterior thyroid.
7
Explain the structure and relations of the trachea — why the cartilage rings are incomplete, and its vertebral levels.
content: L05 · 45:11–47:36
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Describe it: an air tube supported by C-shaped (incomplete) cartilage rings, with the open posterior ends bridged by the trachealis muscle. (L05 45:11–45:45)
  • Answer Maini’s favorite “why”: the rings are incomplete posteriorly so the esophagus (directly behind) can expand/bulge forward during swallowing — while the partial rings keep the airway patent. (L05 45:45–46:18)
  • Fix the levels: begins at the lower border of C6, ends at the upper border of T4/T5 (sternal angle of Louis) by dividing — it lies only in the superior mediastinum. (L05 46:21–46:44)
  • Give the relations: the thyroid isthmus crosses rings 2–4 (enlargement → dyspnea); recurrent laryngeal nerves ascend in the tracheoesophageal grooves; esophagus posterior; thyroid lobes + common carotids lateral. (L05 46:57–47:36)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“If they were complete [rings], then esophagus will not be able to open up during swallowing … you [have] partial rings to keep it open, patent.”— L05 (Blood Vessels of Neck, Cervical Viscera) · ~00:46:01–00:46:18
🎯 Cement it

🧪 Quick self-check
Why are the tracheal rings incomplete posteriorly, and between which vertebral levels does the trachea run?
Incomplete (C-shaped) so the esophagus behind can expand during swallowing — the trachealis muscle bridges the gap and the partial rings keep the airway patent. It runs from the lower border of C6 to the sternal angle (T4/5), in the superior mediastinum only.
8
Walk through thyroid gland development and migration — where it starts, its path, its remnants, and the foramen cecum.
content: L06 · 48:55–55:34
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State the origin: the thyroid is the first gland to develop; it begins as an endodermal thickening in the FLOOR of the primordial pharynx — Maini flags the exact true/false wording. (L06 48:55–49:41)
  • Trace the descent: it stays attached by the thyroglossal duct (“glossal” = tongue), migrating close to the hyoid bone and then anterior to the trachea, reaching its definitive shape/site by week 7 — with no relation to the pharyngeal arches. (L06 50:33–52:44)
  • Say why the path matters: remnant thyroid tissue can persist along the tract → accessory/cervical thyroid, a pyramidal lobe off the isthmus (inconstant), or a thyroglossal duct cyst. (L06 52:44–53:43)
  • Anchor the landmark: the foramen cecum — a blind pit at the midpoint of the sulcus terminalis (which separates the anterior 2/3 from posterior 1/3) — marks where the thyroid started and is the remnant of the thyroglossal duct. (L06 54:05–55:34)

🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M03 Neck 2 Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Where does the thyroid begin, what is its migration path, and what tongue landmark marks its origin?
It begins as an endodermal thickening in the floor of the primordial pharynx, descends via the thyroglossal duct (near the hyoid → anterior to the trachea) by week 7. Its origin is marked by the foramen cecum at the midpoint of the sulcus terminalis. Remnants: accessory/cervical thyroid, pyramidal lobe, thyroglossal duct cyst.
9
Break down tongue development — which arches build the anterior 2/3 vs posterior 1/3, the swellings involved, and where the muscles come from.
content: L06 · 55:34–01:07:39, 01:08:18
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Anterior 2/3 comes from the 1st pharyngeal arch: a midline tuberculum impar plus the paired lateral lingual swellings that overgrow it; where the two lateral swellings fuse = the median sulcus. (L06 59:29–01:00:36)
  • Posterior 1/3 comes mainly from the 3rd arch (with 2nd + 4th): the copula (midline of 2nd/3rd arches) is overgrown by the hypobranchial eminence (midline of 3rd/4th arches); the posterior-most part is a 3rd median swelling of the 4th arch. (L06 01:01:00–01:02:14)
  • Place the divider: the sulcus terminalis (a V-shaped groove) separates anterior 2/3 from posterior 1/3, with the foramen cecum at its apex. (L06 54:15–54:33, 01:01:50)
  • Source the muscles: tongue muscles come from occipital somites/myotomes (paraxial mesoderm) that migrate in — which is why their motor nerve (CN XII) carries general somatic efferent (GSE), not branchial (SVE) fibers. (L06 01:02:35–01:07:28)

🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M03 Neck 2 Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which arch builds the anterior 2/3 vs the posterior 1/3 of the tongue, and where do the tongue muscles come from?
Anterior 2/3 = 1st arch (tuberculum impar + lateral lingual swellings → median sulcus); posterior 1/3 = mainly 3rd arch (with 2nd/4th; copula overgrown by hypobranchial eminence). Muscles = occipital somites → innervated by CN XII (GSE).
10
Reproduce the tongue innervation grid cold — general sensation, taste, and motor, region by region, with the palatoglossus exception.
content: L06 · 01:03:39–01:07:39
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • General sensation (GSA / touch): anterior 2/3 = lingual branch of V3 (mandibular, 1st arch); posterior 1/3 = CN IX (glossopharyngeal); root / posterior-most = CN X (vagus, via the internal laryngeal nerve). (L06 01:04:06–01:04:36)
  • Taste (SVA): anterior 2/3 = CN VII via the chorda tympani (2nd-arch cells dispersed over the anterior tongue to make taste buds, piggybacking on the lingual nerve); posterior 1/3 = CN IX (which carries BOTH GSA + taste there). (L06 01:05:06–01:06:52)
  • Motor: ALL tongue muscles = CN XII (hypoglossal), carrying GSE fibers — EXCEPT the palatoglossus, which is CN X (vagus). (L06 01:02:51, 01:07:01)
  • Spot the trap: on the anterior 2/3, touch and taste ride different nerves — lose taste but keep touch = a chorda tympani (VII) problem with V3 intact. (L06 clinical · audited MC bank)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Taste sensation from anterior [two-thirds] is carried by 7th nerve, called a [chorda tympani] branch of 7th nerve … both GSA and SVA fibers from posterior one-third … are carried by 9th nerve.”— L06 (Cervical Viscera, The Pharyngeal Apparatus) · ~01:06:21–01:06:52
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M03 Neck 2 Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
For the anterior 2/3, which nerve carries touch and which carries taste — and what is the one muscle exception to CN XII?
Anterior 2/3: touch = V3 (lingual), taste = VII (chorda tympani). Posterior 1/3: GSA + taste = IX; root = X. Motor = XII for every tongue muscle except the palatoglossus, which is CN X.

12
Explain palate development — primary vs secondary palate, how the palatine shelves go from vertical to horizontal, and the timing/critical period.
content: L06 · 01:14:31–01:16:23
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Place the primary palate: it grows from the back of the intermaxillary segment. (L06 01:15:11)
  • Build the secondary (definitive) palate: the maxillary processes send down palatine shelves that grow VERTICALLY beside the tongue; as the mandible grows the tongue descends, letting the shelves ELEVATE to horizontal, then grow medially and FUSE with each other and with the primary palate. (L06 01:15:23–01:15:44)
  • Finish the roof: the nasal septum grows down in the midline and fuses with the secondary palate; the primitive choanae form when the oronasal membrane disintegrates by week 8. (L06 01:14:43–01:16:01)
  • Lock the timing: the secondary palate is usually complete by week 12; the critical period for face + palate development is week 6–12 (month 2–3). (L06 01:16:06–01:16:23)

How do the palatine shelves go from vertical to horizontal, and by when is the secondary palate complete?

13
Match the congenital anomalies of the head & neck to their causes — the tongue anomalies, cleft lip/palate, the bifid uvula, and the newborn clinical picture.
content: L06 · 01:08:47–01:19:52
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Match the tongue anomalies: fissuring + hypertrophy of lingual papillae = Down syndrome; macroglossia (large tongue) = hypothyroidism / cretinism; ankyloglossia (tongue-tie) = a too-short lingual frenulum → delayed speech, fixed by cutting the frenulum, otherwise harmless. (L06 01:08:47–01:09:52)
  • Trace cleft lip/palate to incomplete fusion of the facial/palatine processes: unilateral, bilateral, or median cleft lip; median cleft lower lip; oblique facial cleft; cleft palate (one or both sides). (L06 01:17:06–01:19:11)
  • Call the bifid (cleft) uvula what it is: a normal variant with NO abnormal consequences. (L06 01:17:57–01:18:11)
  • Give the newborn picture: cleft palate → feeding difficulties + upper-respiratory infections (special feeders used); reconstructive surgery at ~3–6 months, once the child is big enough. (L06 01:19:11–01:19:52)
