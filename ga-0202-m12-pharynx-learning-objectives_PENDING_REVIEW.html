1
Frame the pharynx overall — what it is, its shape and extent, the wall it lacks, and its three parts.
content: Pharynx L27 · 00:52–03:53
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Define it: the pharynx is the common chamber for the respiratory AND digestive tracts — both air and a swallowed bolus pass through it. It is funnel-shaped, about 12 cm long. (L27 00:52–02:37)
  • Give the extent: from the base of the skull down to the lower border of the cricoid cartilage = C6, where it becomes continuous with the esophagus. The larynx and laryngopharynx share that same C6 inferior limit. (L27 02:41–03:16)
  • State the shape trap: it is C-shaped, not a complete tube — it has essentially no anterior wall because it opens forward into the three cavities. (L27 02:00–03:43)
  • Name the three parts top-to-bottom: nasopharynx, oropharynx, laryngopharynx — Maini’s own picture is “three rooms and one common corridor” (nasal cavity, oral cavity, larynx are the rooms). (L27 01:33, 03:53)

🧪 Quick self-check
The pharynx runs from the base of the skull to which cartilage / vertebral level, and which wall does it essentially lack?
Base of skull to the lower border of the cricoid cartilage = C6, then continuous with the esophagus. It is C-shaped with no anterior wall — it opens forward into the nasal cavity, oral cavity, and larynx (Maini’s “three rooms, one corridor”). Funnel-shaped, ~12 cm.
2
Characterize the nasopharynx — why it can’t collapse, its boundaries, and its roof, floor, and posterior wall supports.
content: Pharynx L27 · 05:04–09:12
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State its nature: the nasopharynx is entirely respiratory, and its walls are rigid — they cannot be collapsed or obliterated because they are supported by bone almost all around. (L27 05:13–05:34)
  • Give the extent: from the base of the skull to the level of the soft palate. (L27 05:40–05:48)
  • Support the roof (body of the sphenoid + basilar part of the occipital bone) and the floor (the sloping upper surface of the soft palate). (L27 06:00–06:40)
  • Name the pharyngeal isthmus (the opening between the soft palate and the posterior pharyngeal wall) and the posterior wall support = the anterior arch of the atlas (C1). (L27 06:45–07:38)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Nasopharynx is entirely respiratory … its walls are relatively rigid and cannot be obliterated … supported by the bony structures almost all around, so it’s rigid, it cannot be collapsed.”— The Pharynx (Lecture 27) · ~00:05:13–00:05:34
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M12 Pharynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which two bones support the roof of the nasopharynx, and what supports its posterior wall?
Roof = body of the sphenoid + basilar part of the occipital bone. Posterior wall = anterior arch of the atlas (C1). Floor = sloping surface of the soft palate; the opening there is the pharyngeal isthmus. Because it is boxed in by bone, the nasopharynx cannot collapse.
3
Map the lateral wall of the nasopharynx — the auditory tube opening, torus tubarius, tubal tonsil, pharyngeal recess, and salpingopharyngeal fold.
content: Pharynx L27 · 07:50–17:22
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Identify the pharyngeal opening of the auditory (pharyngotympanic) tube, which connects the nasopharynx to the middle ear (tympanic cavity), opening into its anterior wall. (L27 07:50–09:56)
  • Name the ridge the tube’s cartilage raises — the torus tubarius (tubal elevation) — and the tubal tonsils that surround the tube opening. (L27 10:11–10:44)
  • Locate the pharyngeal recess, a pocket behind / above the torus tubarius. (L27 10:54–11:10)
  • Trace the salpingopharyngeal fold running down from the torus, overlying the salpingopharyngeus muscle — and know “salpingo” (salpinx) means “tube” (as in the fallopian tube / salpingitis). (L27 16:18–17:06)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Lateral wall has pharyngeal opening of auditory tube, tubal elevation, salpingopharyngeal fold, and pharyngeal recess.”— The Pharynx (Lecture 27) · ~00:07:50 (restated ~00:17:22)
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M12 Pharynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
What is the ridge in the lateral nasopharyngeal wall called, what raises it, and what does “salpingo-” mean?
The ridge is the torus tubarius, raised by the cartilage of the auditory (pharyngotympanic) tube, which opens the nasopharynx to the middle ear. The pharyngeal recess sits behind it, and the salpingopharyngeal fold runs down from it. Salpingo- = tube.
4
Explain the pharyngeal tonsil and adenoids — where it sits, what “adenoids” means, and the mouth-breather / adenoid-facies story.
content: Pharynx L27 · 11:15–16:04
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Place it: a single pharyngeal tonsil sits in the roof of the nasopharynx. (L27 11:15–11:26)
  • Define adenoids = the inflamed / enlarged pharyngeal tonsil (the clinical name). (L27 11:47–12:10)
  • Trace the sequence: an enlarged pharyngeal tonsil blocks the posterior nasal passage → nasal obstruction → the child becomes a mouth-breather. (L27 12:38–13:01)
  • Recognize adenoid facies (recessed/smaller mandible, flat face, habitually open mouth) and note that early adenoidectomy can normalize it because facial bones and paranasal sinuses keep developing until puberty. (L27 14:45–15:32)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“You have a single pharyngeal tonsil in the roof of nasopharynx. The other name for this tonsil … is called as adenoids … Adenoids is inflamed pharyngeal tonsil.”— The Pharynx (Lecture 27) · ~00:11:47–00:12:10
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M12 Pharynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
A chronic mouth-breathing child with a flat face and recessed jaw — what is the enlarged structure, and what is that facial pattern called?
Enlarged pharyngeal tonsil = adenoids in the roof of the nasopharynx, blocking the posterior nares. The pattern is adenoid facies (recessed/smaller mandible, flat face, open mouth). Caught early, adenoidectomy can let it normalize (bones develop until puberty).
5
Detail the oropharynx — the digestive entrance, its boundaries, its tongue floor, and the folds and tonsils of its walls.
content: Pharynx L27 · 17:30–22:14
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Frame it: the oropharynx is the digestive entrance of the chamber, extending from the soft palate to the upper (superior) border of the epiglottis. (L27 17:30–17:52)
  • Give the floor = the posterior one-third of the tongue, carrying the single lingual tonsil at the tongue root; the three glossoepiglottic folds (one median + two lateral) connect tongue to epiglottis, with the valleculae (right and left) as the spaces between them. (L27 18:46–20:24)
  • Read the lateral wall: the palatoglossal and palatopharyngeal folds, with the tonsillar fossa between them housing the palatine tonsil. (L27 21:08–21:46)
  • Support the posterior wall = body of C2, upper part of the body of C3, and the C2–C3 disc; the anterior wall opens to the mouth via the oropharyngeal isthmus. (L27 20:48–20:57)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Oropharynx represents the digestive entrance of the chamber. It extends from the soft palate to upper border of epiglottis … floor is formed by posterior one-third of the tongue.”— The Pharynx (Lecture 27) · ~00:17:44–00:18:46
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M12 Pharynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
The palatine tonsil sits between which two folds, and what are the valleculae?
The palatine tonsil sits in the tonsillar fossa, between the palatoglossal and palatopharyngeal folds. The valleculae are the right and left spaces between the three glossoepiglottic folds (one median, two lateral) linking the tongue to the epiglottis.
6
Work through the laryngopharynx and piriform fossa — its boundaries, the fossa’s foreign-body trap, and the internal laryngeal nerve consequence.
content: Pharynx L27 · 22:21–27:37
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Place it: the laryngopharynx lies behind the laryngeal inlet and the posterior wall of the larynx, extending from the upper border of the epiglottis to the lower border of the cricoid cartilage (C6); it communicates above with the oropharynx and below with the esophagus. (L27 22:21–23:15)
  • Identify the piriform fossa: a pear-shaped, shallow depression on each side of the laryngeal inlet — a spot where sharp foreign bodies (e.g., a fishbone) can lodge. (L27 24:34–25:04)
  • Warn about the internal laryngeal nerve lying just deep to the piriform-fossa mucosa (sensory to the larynx above the vocal folds); a foreign body injuring it causes loss of the gag reflex → aspiration → aspiration pneumonitis. (L27 25:13–26:33)
  • Support the walls: posterior wall = bodies of C3–C6; lateral wall = thyroid cartilage + thyrohyoid membrane, which Maini likens to “armor.” (L27 27:16–27:37)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Pear-shaped fossae on each side of the laryngeal inlet … which is called as piriform fossa … lying right under the mucous membrane of the piriform fossa, we have internal laryngeal nerve.”— The Pharynx (Lecture 27) · ~00:24:34–00:25:13
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M12 Pharynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
A fishbone lodges in the piriform fossa and injures the nerve beneath its mucosa — which nerve, and what is the clinical result?
The internal laryngeal nerve (sensory to the larynx above the vocal folds). Injuring it causes loss of the gag reflex → aspiration → aspiration pneumonitis. The piriform fossa is a shallow, pear-shaped recess on each side of the laryngeal inlet.
7
Collect the four pharyngeal tonsils — name each, know where it sits, and which are single vs paired.
content: Pharynx L27 · 10:44–21:46
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Name the four tonsils guarding the pharyngeal openings: pharyngeal, tubal, palatine, and lingual. (L27 10:44–21:46)
  • Place each: pharyngeal tonsil in the roof of the nasopharynx; tubal tonsils around the auditory-tube opening; palatine tonsil in the tonsillar fossa (between palatoglossal and palatopharyngeal folds); lingual tonsil on the root of the tongue. (L27 11:15, 10:44, 21:46, 19:11)
  • Flag which are single (unpaired): the pharyngeal tonsil and the lingual tonsil. (L27 16:04, 19:11)
  • Remember the tubal and palatine tonsils come as a pair (right and left), matching their paired tube openings and tonsillar fossae. (L27 10:44, 21:46)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“How many pharyngeal tonsils do we have? Just one. Single … [and] we have a single lingual tonsil also.”— The Pharynx (Lecture 27) · ~00:16:04 & ~00:19:11
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M12 Pharynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Name the four tonsils of the pharynx and say which two are single.
Pharyngeal (roof of nasopharynx), tubal (around the auditory-tube opening), palatine (tonsillar fossa), and lingual (root of tongue). The pharyngeal and lingual tonsils are single; the tubal and palatine are paired.
8
Build the pharyngeal wall in layers — the epithelium (and why), the two fasciae, the muscle layers, and the retropharyngeal space.
content: Pharynx L27 · 27:51–34:13
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State the lining: stratified squamous, non-keratinized epithelium — stratified to withstand abrasion from the food bolus, non-keratinized because keratin is found only in skin (mucous membranes lack it). (L27 29:08–29:54)
  • Order the layers internal → external: mucosa → pharyngobasilar fascia (submucosa) → muscular layer → buccopharyngeal fascia. (L27 30:20–32:12)
  • Note the pharyngobasilar fascia is a strong fibrous sheet that blends with the periosteum of the base-of-skull bones; the muscular layer has an outer circular (constrictor) and inner longitudinal arrangement. (L27 30:30–31:34)
  • Explain the retropharyngeal space (space of Gillette/“styles”): loose areolar connective tissue that lets the pharynx glide up and down during swallowing — its obliteration (fibrosis, tumor, post-abscess) fixes the pharynx to the spine and causes dysphagia. (L27 32:26–34:07)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Stratified squamous epithelial tissue … lines the pharynx … [it’s] non-keratinized … keratin is found only in the skin … keratin makes the tissue tough, and you need that only in the skin.”— The Pharynx (Lecture 27) · ~00:29:27–00:29:54
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M12 Pharynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
List the pharyngeal wall layers from inside out, and say what fills the retropharyngeal space and why it matters.
Mucosa → pharyngobasilar fascia → muscular layer (circular + longitudinal) → buccopharyngeal fascia. The epithelium is stratified squamous non-keratinized. The retropharyngeal space holds loose areolar tissue that lets the pharynx glide when swallowing; obliterating it causes dysphagia.
9
Master the three constrictor muscles — their layer, origins and insertions (pharyngeal tubercle and raphe), and Maini’s oblique-line exam trap.
content: Pharynx L27 · 34:16–42:42
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State that the three constrictors (superior, middle, inferior) form the external, circular layer and contract sequentially to propel the bolus during swallowing. (L27 34:37–34:50, 37:05)
  • Superior constrictor: origin from the pterygoid hamulus, pterygomandibular raphe, mylohyoid line, and side of the tongue → inserts on the pharyngeal tubercle (center of the basilar occipital, just anterior to the foramen magnum). Buccinator lies anterior to it on the pterygomandibular raphe. (L27 34:50–36:29)
  • Middle constrictor: origin from the stylohyoid ligament + greater and lesser horns of the hyoid → inserts on the midline pharyngeal raphe. (L27 37:14–37:47)
  • Nail Maini’s trap: the inferior constrictor, sternothyroid, and thyrohyoid all attach to the oblique line of the thyroid cartilage — so “which is NOT attached?” the answer is sternohyoid. (L27 41:55–42:42)
🎤 Dr. Maini said (her own exam flag)
“So this makes a good question for me: which one of the following muscle is not attached to [the] oblique line of thyroid cartilage? Inferior constrictor is attached … sternothyroid and thyrohyoid.”— The Pharynx (Lecture 27) · ~00:42:26–00:42:42
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M12 Pharynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Where does the superior constrictor insert, and which muscle is NOT attached to the oblique line of the thyroid cartilage?
The superior constrictor inserts on the pharyngeal tubercle (basilar occipital). Attached to the oblique line of the thyroid cartilage: inferior constrictor, sternothyroid, thyrohyoid — so the one NOT attached (Maini’s trap) is sternohyoid.
10
Explain the inferior constrictor’s two parts and the cricopharyngeus as the upper esophageal sphincter — plus the aerophagia consequence.
content: Pharynx L27 · 37:58–41:32
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Split the inferior constrictor into its two named parts: thyropharyngeus (upper) and cricopharyngeus (lower). (L27 38:32–38:54)
  • Apply the raphe rule: thyropharyngeus forms a raphe, but cricopharyngeus does NOT — it encircles the pharyngoesophageal junction (Maini compares it to the puborectalis sling). (L27 38:58–41:13)
  • Name its function: the cricopharyngeus is the upper esophageal sphincter (UES) — it keeps the esophagus closed except during swallowing. (L27 39:31–40:15)
  • Give the clinical sequel: in old age the UES loses tone → aerophagia (swallowed air enters the stomach), producing constant belching / eructation. (L27 40:23–40:48)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“This is your upper esophageal sphincter … this muscle, when it contracts, it closes the upper end of esophagus … it keeps esophagus closed all the time, except for when you’re swallowing.”— The Pharynx (Lecture 27) · ~00:39:31–00:40:15
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M12 Pharynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which part of the inferior constrictor is the upper esophageal sphincter, and what does its loss of tone in old age cause?
The cricopharyngeus (lower part of the inferior constrictor) is the upper esophageal sphincter — it keeps the esophagus closed except when swallowing and does not form a raphe. Loss of tone in old age causes aerophagia (swallowing air), i.e., constant belching.
11
Name the three longitudinal (inner) muscles — their origins, shared insertion, and elevating function.
content: Pharynx L27 · 42:42–43:43
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Name the three longitudinal (inner-layer) muscles: palatopharyngeus, salpingopharyngeus, and stylopharyngeus. (L27 42:42–43:27)
  • Give their origins: palatopharyngeus from the hard palate + palatine aponeurosis (it forms the palatopharyngeal arch); salpingopharyngeus from the cartilage of the auditory tube; stylopharyngeus from the styloid process. (L27 42:51–43:27)
  • Note the shared insertion: palatopharyngeus and stylopharyngeus insert onto the thyroid cartilage, and all three blend together. (L27 43:27–43:43)
  • State the function (vs the constrictors): the longitudinal muscles run lengthwise, so they elevate / shorten the pharynx and larynx to produce movement (they pull the structure up). (L27 31:26–31:34)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“There is palatopharyngeus muscle … salpingopharyngeus … [and] stylopharyngeus muscle … originates from styloid process … [they are] inserted onto the thyroid cartilage.”— The Pharynx (Lecture 27) · ~00:42:42–00:43:43
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M12 Pharynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Name the three longitudinal pharyngeal muscles and their overall function.
Palatopharyngeus (hard palate/palatine aponeurosis), salpingopharyngeus (auditory-tube cartilage), and stylopharyngeus (styloid process). Running lengthwise, they elevate / shorten the pharynx and larynx; palatopharyngeus and stylopharyngeus insert on the thyroid cartilage.
12
Nail the pharyngeal innervation — the arch-to-vagus rule and the single stylopharyngeus exception.
content: Pharynx L27 · 34:16–44:49
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State the rule: most pharyngeal muscles develop from the 4th and 6th pharyngeal arches, so they are supplied by the vagus nerve (CN X), via its pharyngeal branch. (L27 34:16–34:30)
  • Give the exception: stylopharyngeus is the one pharyngeal muscle not supplied by the vagus — it is innervated by the glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX) because it derives from the 3rd arch. (L27 43:45–44:26)
  • Use Maini’s memory hook: “stylopharyngeus is so stylish, it has its own nerve” (CN IX). (L27 44:34–44:43)
  • Separate sensation from motor: the internal laryngeal nerve (a vagus branch) carries sensory supply to the mucosa around the piriform fossa and the upper larynx. (L27 25:37–26:15)
🎤 Dr. Maini said (her memory hook)
“All the pharyngeal muscles are innervated by [the] 10th nerve, except for stylopharyngeus muscle, which is innervated by glossopharyngeal nerve … stylopharyngeus muscle is so stylish, it has its own nerve.”— The Pharynx (Lecture 27) · ~00:43:45–00:44:43
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M12 Pharynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which nerve supplies most pharyngeal muscles, and which single muscle is the exception (and by which nerve)?
Most pharyngeal muscles (from arches 4 & 6) are supplied by the vagus (CN X) pharyngeal branch. The exception is stylopharyngeus (3rd arch), supplied by the glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX) — “so stylish it has its own nerve.”

⚠️ Don’t mix up the boundaries and vertebral levels. The whole pharynx runs base of skull → C6 (lower cricoid); the nasopharynx ends at the soft palate, the oropharynx at the upper border of the epiglottis. Posterior-wall supports climb the spine: nasopharynx = C1 (anterior arch of atlas), oropharynx = C2 + upper C3, laryngopharynx = C3–C6. And keep the clinical quartet straight: adenoids → mouth-breather / adenoid facies, internal laryngeal nerve → lost gag reflex / aspiration, retropharyngeal space obliterated → dysphagia, UES tone lost → aerophagia.
