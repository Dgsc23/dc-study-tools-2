🧠 What this page is

1
Frame the larynx overall — its functions, its location, and its skeleton of nine cartilages (which are unpaired, which are paired, and what they’re made of).
content: Larynx L25 · 41:33–50:03 · L26 · 00:31
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • List the functions: the voice box (organ of phonation), an air passage, the link between the respiratory and digestive pathways, and a sphincter that guards the airway. (L25 41:52–42:41)
  • Place it: it sits between the root of the tongue and the trachea, spanning about C3 to the lower border of C6; it opens above into the laryngopharynx (via the laryngeal inlet) and below into the trachea. (L25 47:00–48:24)
  • State the count: nine cartilages total — 3 large unpaired (thyroid, cricoid, epiglottis) and 3 paired (arytenoid, corniculate, cuneiform). (L25 49:18–50:03)
  • Assign the cartilage types: thyroid & cricoid = hyaline; epiglottis = elastic; corniculate & cuneiform = yellow elastic. (L25 53:47, 1:11:26, 1:16:06)

“There are 9 cartilages … there are 3 pairs … and 3 unpaired — [thyroid], cricoid and epiglottis … these three are large … and then arytenoids, corniculates and cuneiforms … so in total, 3 plus 6, 9 cartilages.”— The Larynx (L25) · ~00:49:18–00:50:03

🧪 Quick self-check
How many cartilages make up the larynx, which three are the large unpaired ones, and what cartilage type is the epiglottis?
Nine total: 3 large unpaired (thyroid, cricoid, epiglottis) + 3 paired (arytenoid, corniculate, cuneiform). Thyroid & cricoid are hyaline; the epiglottis is elastic cartilage.
2
Describe the thyroid cartilage — its two laminae and laryngeal prominence, its horns, the joint it makes, and what attaches to it.
content: Larynx L25 · 52:00–58:19
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Call it the largest laryngeal cartilage (unpaired, hyaline): two laminae fuse in the midline at the laryngeal prominence / thyroid notch (the Adam’s apple, sharper in males), and it is posteriorly deficient — open behind like a necklace with no back. (L25 52:00–54:59)
  • Name the projections: each posterior border extends into a superior horn and an inferior horn (cornu). (L25 54:15–54:30)
  • Give the joint: the inferior horn articulates with the cricoid at the cricothyroid joint. (L25 56:41–57:15)
  • Load its landmarks: the oblique line takes sternothyroid & thyrohyoid; the cartilage links to the hyoid by the thyrohyoid membrane, to the epiglottis by the thyroepiglottic ligament, and (via the vocal ligament) to the vocal process of the arytenoid. (L25 55:51–58:19)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“[Thyroid] cartilage is posteriorly deficient. It’s incomplete … like a necklace which doesn’t have a posterior limb … this notch, it has a prominence … laryngeal prominence, or Adam’s apple in males, because it’s very prominent in males.”— The Larynx (L25) · ~00:52:40–00:55:07
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M11 Larynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which part of the thyroid cartilage forms the Adam’s apple, and which of its horns makes the cricothyroid joint?
The midline fusion of the two laminae forms the laryngeal prominence (Adam’s apple / thyroid notch). The inferior horn articulates with the cricoid at the cricothyroid joint. Remember it is posteriorly deficient (open behind).
3
Describe the cricoid cartilage — the only complete ring, its signet-ring shape, its two joints, and the trachea below.
content: Larynx L25 · 1:02:36–1:05:56
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Nail Dr. Maini’s “good exam question”: the cricoid is the ONLY laryngeal cartilage that completely encircles the airway as a full ring (hyaline). (L25 1:03:43–1:03:57)
  • Use her shape cue: it’s a signet ring — narrow anterior arch, broad posterior lamina. (L25 1:02:47–1:04:22)
  • Give its two joints: cricothyroid joint with the thyroid’s inferior horn, and cricoarytenoid joint with the arytenoid. (L25 1:04:22–1:05:05)
  • Fix its neighbours: attached to the thyroid by the cricothyroid ligament (intrinsic) and to the trachea below by the cricotracheal ligament (extrinsic) — the cricoid’s inferior border is where the larynx ends and the trachea begins. (L25 1:05:20–1:05:56)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“This is, by the way, the only cartilage which completely encircles the larynx, which makes a good question … shaped like a signet ring … it consists of an anterior arch and a posterior quadrangular lamina.”— The Larynx (L25) · ~01:03:43–01:04:22
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M11 Larynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
What makes the cricoid unique among the laryngeal cartilages, and which two joints does it form?
It is the only cartilage that completely encircles the airway (a full signet ring: narrow arch in front, broad lamina behind). It forms the cricothyroid joint (with the thyroid) and the cricoarytenoid joint (with the arytenoid). Below it: the trachea.
4
Map the paired small cartilages — the arytenoid (its processes), plus corniculate, cuneiform, and the triticeal “trap.”
content: Larynx L25 · 1:06:00–1:18:11
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Describe the arytenoid: a three-sided pyramid sitting on the lateral superior border of the cricoid lamina, with a base, an apex, a vocal process (anchors the vocal ligament) and a muscular process (anchors intrinsic muscles). (L25 1:06:00–1:07:45)
  • Place the corniculate: small nodules on the apex of each arytenoid that prolong it backward and medially (in the aryepiglottic fold). (L25 1:10:53–1:11:11)
  • Place the cuneiform: yellow-elastic rods sitting within the aryepiglottic fold (superior border of the quadrangular membrane), anterior to the corniculate. (L25 1:16:06–1:16:23)
  • Dodge the trap: the triticeal (triticeous) cartilages are NOT part of the larynx — they lie in the free border of the thyrohyoid membrane and exist “to confuse you” as a distractor. (L25 1:17:29–1:18:11)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“[Arytenoids] are pyramidal-shaped … three-sided pyramid … with a base, an apex, and two processes. There is a vocal process … and there is a muscular process, for the attachment of muscles.”— The Larynx (L25) · ~01:06:24–01:07:45
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M11 Larynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
What attaches to the arytenoid’s vocal process vs its muscular process, and why is the triticeal cartilage a classic distractor?
The vocal process anchors the vocal ligament (vocal cord); the muscular process anchors the intrinsic muscles. The triticeal cartilage is a trap because it is NOT part of the larynx — it sits in the free border of the thyrohyoid membrane.
5
Explain the epiglottis — its shape and cartilage type, its (limited) role in swallowing, and its folds and valleculae.
content: Larynx L25 · 58:42–1:15:00 · L26 · 26:16
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Describe it: a leaf-shaped / spoon-shaped elastic cartilage that projects up behind the tongue and hyoid. (L25 58:42, 1:11:26)
  • State its swallowing role: during deglutition it bends back over the laryngeal inlet — but in humans it is somewhat degenerate and not essential to swallowing (the vestibular folds back it up). (L25 1:11:53–1:12:06)
  • Give the naming logic: “epiglottis” = the lid to the glottis. (L26 26:16)
  • Name its connections: to the tongue by the glossoepiglottic folds (one median + two lateral), with the shallow valleculae between them (where food can lodge); to the hyoid by the hyoepiglottic ligament (extrinsic); to the thyroid by the thyroepiglottic ligament (intrinsic); to the arytenoid by the quadrangular membrane. (L25 1:13:00–1:15:00)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“In humans, it is somewhat degenerate in function and is not essential to swallowing … your epiglottis was named after glottis. Epiglottis is the lid to glottis.”— The Larynx (L25 ~01:12:00 · L26 ~00:26:16)
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M11 Larynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
What cartilage type is the epiglottis, and what are the depressions between its glossoepiglottic folds called?
The epiglottis is elastic cartilage (leaf/spoon-shaped). The spaces between the median and lateral glossoepiglottic folds are the valleculae, where food can lodge. In humans it is somewhat degenerate and not essential to swallowing.
6
Sort the membranes and ligaments — extrinsic vs intrinsic, the quadrangular membrane’s two borders, and the conus elasticus.
content: Larynx L25 · 50:18–51:07 · L26 · 03:00–15:51
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State the rule: extrinsic connects the larynx to a non-laryngeal structure; intrinsic connects laryngeal structures to each other. The three extrinsic ones are the thyrohyoid membrane, cricotracheal ligament, and hyoepiglottic ligament. (L25 50:18–51:07 · L26 03:00)
  • Map the quadrangular membrane (intrinsic): it spans epiglottis to arytenoid; its superior border = aryepiglottic ligament/fold, its inferior border = vestibular ligament (the false vocal cord). (L26 04:14–06:20)
  • Define the vocal ligament (= thyroarytenoid ligament): runs from the arytenoid’s vocal process forward to the thyroid; covered by mucosa it becomes the vocal fold. (L26 08:08–08:33)
  • Assemble the conus elasticus: the cone-shaped complex of the median cricothyroid ligament + the vocal ligaments + the lateral cricothyroid ligaments (which actually attach up to the vocal cords, not the thyroid). (L26 11:42–15:17)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Anything that connects larynx with a structure outside … that is extrinsic. Intrinsic is structures that are connected within the larynx … these three structures together make a cone-like arrangement, which is called conus elasticus.”— The Larynx (L25 ~00:51:29 · L26 ~00:15:06)
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M11 Larynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
What do the superior and inferior borders of the quadrangular membrane become, and which three ligaments make the conus elasticus?
Quadrangular membrane: superior border = aryepiglottic ligament/fold, inferior border = vestibular ligament (false cord). The conus elasticus = median cricothyroid ligament + vocal ligaments + lateral cricothyroid ligaments. Extrinsic trio: thyrohyoid membrane, cricotracheal, hyoepiglottic.
7
Contrast the vocal folds and the vestibular (false) folds — what each is, which one moves, and what lives inside the vocal fold.
content: Larynx L26 · 05:49–10:19, 39:00–40:37
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Define the vestibular fold = the mucosa-covered vestibular ligament (inferior border of the quadrangular membrane), a.k.a. the false vocal cord; the vocal fold = the mucosa-covered vocal (thyroarytenoid) ligament = the true cord. (L26 06:03–08:33)
  • Give the key difference: vocal folds are mobile (muscles abduct/adduct them), while vestibular folds have no muscle and are not normally mobile — the vestibular folds lie superior, the vocal folds deep/inferior to them. (L26 24:30–25:00)
  • Use her guitar-string image: moving the arytenoid changes the tension in the cord, and that is how we speak. (L26 10:01–10:19)
  • Name what sits inside the vocal fold: thyroarytenoid laterally and vocalis medially. (L26 39:44–40:37)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Vestibular folds otherwise are called false vocal cords … vocal cords are moved because muscles act on them, they abduct or adduct them … vestibular folds, they stay there.”— The Larynx (L26) · ~00:06:53–00:24:54
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M11 Larynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which folds are the “false” cords, which folds actually move, and which two muscles lie within the vocal fold?
The vestibular folds = the false vocal cords (no muscle, lie superior, not normally mobile). The vocal folds are the mobile true cords (muscles abduct/adduct them). Inside the vocal fold: thyroarytenoid (lateral) + vocalis (medial).
8
Divide the laryngeal cavity — vestibule, ventricle (with saccule), and infraglottic space — and name the rima vestibuli vs rima glottidis.
content: Larynx L26 · 18:54–30:07
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Stack the three parts: vestibule (inlet → vestibular folds), ventricle (the narrow space between vestibular & vocal folds), and infraglottic space (vocal folds → inferior border of cricoid). (L26 18:54–21:24)
  • Add the saccule: an anterior pocket off the ventricle whose mucous glands lubricate the vocal cords. (L26 21:49–22:06)
  • Name the two openings: rima vestibuli = the gap between the vestibular folds; rima glottidis = the gap between the vocal folds (“your actual glottis”). (L26 24:06–25:42)
  • Bound the laryngeal inlet: epiglottis anteriorly, arytenoid cartilages posteriorly, aryepiglottic folds laterally; and read the rima glottidis by state — partially open in quiet breathing, approximated to a slit in phonation. (L26 16:43–18:00, 44:22–44:52)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“The space between the right and left vestibular fold is called [rima] vestibuli … the space between the two vocal folds … is called [rima] glottidis. That is your actual glottis.”— The Larynx (L26) · ~00:24:06–00:25:42
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M11 Larynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Name the three parts of the laryngeal cavity in order, and say which rima sits between the vocal folds.
Top to bottom: vestibule → ventricle → infraglottic space. The rima glottidis (the “actual glottis”) sits between the vocal folds; the rima vestibuli sits between the vestibular folds. The ventricle’s saccule lubricates the cords.
9
Assign the intrinsic muscles by action — the tensor, the sole abductor, the adductors, and the relaxers.
content: Larynx L26 · 34:07–41:28
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Tensor: cricothyroid (arch of cricoid → inferior horn/lower border of thyroid) tilts the thyroid down, lengthening and stretching the cords — the chief tensor that changes pitch. (L26 34:28–35:44, 39:48)
  • Sole abductor: posterior cricoarytenoid (posterior cricoid lamina → muscular process) — the only muscle that opens the rima glottidis. (L26 36:24–37:33)
  • Adductors: lateral cricoarytenoid + the transverse and oblique arytenoids close the rima glottidis. (L26 37:47–38:30)
  • Relaxers: thyroarytenoid and vocalis (both lie within the fold) slacken/shorten the cords. (L26 40:37–41:28)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“[Posterior cricoarytenoid] is the only, the sole, abductor of vocal cords … the chief tensor is cricothyroid … vocalis and thyroarytenoid, they slacken or relax the vocal cords.”— The Larynx (L26) · ~00:37:20–00:41:28
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M11 Larynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which single muscle is the sole abductor of the vocal cords, which is the chief tensor, and which two relax the cords?
Posterior cricoarytenoid = the sole abductor (opens the airway). Cricothyroid = the chief tensor. Thyroarytenoid + vocalis = the relaxers. Adductors (close the cords): lateral cricoarytenoid + transverse/oblique arytenoids.
10
Trace the innervation — the vagus, the superior laryngeal’s two branches, the recurrent laryngeal, and the two exception rules.
content: Larynx L26 · 41:32–53:03
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Give the developmental logic: the laryngeal muscles come from the 4th & 6th pharyngeal arches, so they’re all vagus (CN X), carrying SVE motor fibers. (L26 41:37–42:18)
  • Split the superior laryngeal nerve: internal laryngeal = purely sensory (GSA), pierces the thyrohyoid membrane, supplies mucosa down to the vocal folds; external laryngeal = motor to one muscle only, the cricothyroid. (L26 45:26–46:12, 50:52–51:20)
  • Give the recurrent laryngeal nerve: motor to ALL intrinsic muscles except cricothyroid and sensory below the vocal folds; its terminal part is the inferior laryngeal nerve, running in the tracheoesophageal groove. (L26 46:37–53:03)
  • State the two exception rules: (1) cricothyroid is the odd muscle out — external laryngeal, not recurrent; (2) sensory supply splits at the vocal folds — internal laryngeal above, recurrent laryngeal below. (L26 42:25–43:16, 51:04–52:02)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Cricothyroid is innervated by external laryngeal nerve … all the other muscles … recurrent laryngeal … above the level of vocal folds is innervated by internal laryngeal … the mucosa below the vocal cords … recurrent laryngeal.”— The Larynx (L26) · ~00:42:25–00:52:02
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M11 Larynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which muscle is the innervation exception, and where does laryngeal sensation switch from internal laryngeal to recurrent laryngeal?
Cricothyroid is the exception (motor from the external laryngeal nerve); every other intrinsic muscle is recurrent laryngeal. Sensation switches at the vocal folds: internal laryngeal above, recurrent laryngeal below. All are branches of the vagus (CN X).
11
Trace the blood supply — the superior and inferior laryngeal arteries back to their parent vessels, and the venous return.
content: Larynx L26 · 53:03–54:30
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Upper half: the superior laryngeal artery — a branch of the superior thyroid artery, itself a branch of the external carotid — pierces the thyrohyoid membrane with the internal laryngeal nerve. (L26 53:10–53:29)
  • Lower half: the inferior laryngeal artery — a branch of the inferior thyroid artery, which arises from the thyrocervical trunk (off the subclavian). (L26 53:17–54:04)
  • Anchor the “why”: the internal carotid gives no branches in the neck, so a thyroid/laryngeal artery must come off the external carotid (or the subclavian’s thyrocervical trunk). (L26 53:39–54:04)
  • Give venous return: the superior laryngeal vein → superior thyroid vein → internal jugular; the inferior laryngeal vein → inferior thyroid → brachiocephalic vein. (L26 54:13–54:30)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Superior laryngeal artery, a branch of superior thyroid artery, supplies the upper half. Inferior laryngeal artery … branch of inferior thyroid supplies the lower half … inferior thyroid is a branch of [thyrocervical] trunk … subclavian.”— The Larynx (L26) · ~00:53:10–00:54:04
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M11 Larynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Trace the superior and inferior laryngeal arteries back to their ultimate parent vessels.
Superior laryngeal a. ← superior thyroid a. ← external carotid. Inferior laryngeal a. ← inferior thyroid a. ← thyrocervical trunk ← subclavian. (The internal carotid gives no branches in the neck.)
12
Work the clinical picture — the two nerve-lesion voice/airway pictures, the surgical nerve risks, laryngeal elevation in swallowing, and dysphonia.
content: Larynx L25 · 01:52 · L26 · 31:32–52:38
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Read the voice lesions: a monotonous voice (can speak, can’t vary pitch) = cricothyroid / external laryngeal palsy; bilateral posterior cricoarytenoid paralysis leaves the cords adducted, the glottis can’t open, and the patient asphyxiates. (L26 35:53, 37:12–37:20, 46:12)
  • Read the sensory lesion: internal laryngeal damage removes the gag/cough reflex above the cords → risk of aspiration. (L26 52:12–52:38)
  • Flag the surgical risks in thyroidectomy: the external laryngeal nerve runs with the superior thyroid artery (ligate close to origin), and the recurrent laryngeal nerve runs with the inferior thyroid artery. (L26 49:55–50:33)
  • Explain the mechanics: during swallowing the larynx is elevated (suprahyoids) to meet the epiglottis and close the inlet (infrahyoids depress it back); and define dysphonia = a change in voice quality from laryngeal pathology. (L26 31:32–32:57 · L25 01:52)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“If this muscle [cricothyroid] is paralyzed, the patient will speak in monotonous … you cannot breathe if both vocal cords stay adducted … the glottis will not open up, and patient will die … if internal laryngeal nerve is damaged, there’ll be loss of gag reflex and increased chances of aspiration.”— The Larynx (L26) · ~00:35:53–00:52:38
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M11 Larynx Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which lesion gives a monotonous voice, which gives asphyxiation, and which artery puts the recurrent laryngeal nerve at risk in surgery?
Cricothyroid / external laryngeal palsy = monotonous voice. Bilateral posterior cricoarytenoid paralysis = cords stuck adducted → asphyxiation. In surgery the recurrent laryngeal nerve is at risk near the inferior thyroid artery (external laryngeal near the superior thyroid artery).

🔗 How to use these 12 to study

1. Read each objective out loud, then try the self-check before revealing the answer. If you nailed it, tick Got it.

2. For any objective you miss, jump back to the GA 0202 M11 Larynx Hub and drill exactly that topic.

3. The muscles + innervation (Objectives 9–10) are where Dr. Maini’s “exception-rule” questions live — spend extra reps there.

⚠️ Don’t mix up the look-alike pairs. Two openings: the rima vestibuli is between the false (vestibular) folds, the rima glottidis is between the true (vocal) folds. Two muscle traps: the posterior cricoarytenoid is the sole abductor (opens the airway), while the lateral cricoarytenoid is an adductor. Two nerve exceptions: cricothyroid is the one muscle on the external laryngeal nerve, and sensation switches at the cords — internal laryngeal above, recurrent laryngeal below. And the triticeal cartilage is NOT a laryngeal cartilage.
