1 — Dr. Maini’s OWN memory hooks (her words)

Straight from the recordings. These are hers — quoted, not paraphrased.
Maini’s own hook (with her caveat)
🪞 P ↔ 9 = PAROTID ↔ GLOSSOPHARYNGEAL
The parasympathetic (secretomotor) supply to the parotid gland is the glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX). Full pathway: IX → tympanic branch → tympanic plexus → lesser petrosal nerve → otic ganglion (synapse) → auriculotemporal nerve → parotid.
“P is parotid, 9 is almost a mirror image of P… but maybe that can be risky. No, no, don’t think that.” She offered the mirror-image hook, then hedged it. Use it to jog the answer, but actually know the pathway.
Maini · L25 Oral Cavity 00:34
Maini’s own hook
⬆️ HIGHER NERVE = SUPERIOR NUCLEUS
CN VII exits the brainstem higher, so its parasympathetics (for submandibular + sublingual) sit in the SUPERIOR salivatory nucleus (pons). CN IX exits lower, so the parotid preganglionics sit in the INFERIOR salivatory nucleus (medulla).
“7th nerve is higher, so superior salivatory nucleus. 9th nerve is lower, inferior salivatory nucleus. That makes sense, right?”
Maini · L25 Oral Cavity 00:40
Maini’s own nickname
🚧 HYOGLOSSUS = THE “FENCE MUSCLE”
Hyoglossus is the landmark that separates nerves from arteries in the tongue. Behind it (deep): the nerves — hypoglossal + lingual. In front of it: the arteries — lingual artery + branches.
“Fence muscle because it separates nerves from arteries… It acts as a curtain.”
Maini · L25 Oral Cavity 00:27
Maini’s own hook
🔤 S·L·F = FIRST 3 ANTERIOR ECA BRANCHES
The lingual artery (supply to the tongue) is the 2nd anterior branch of the external carotid. Anterior branches in order: Superior thyroid, Lingual, Facial. Her count: 3 anterior, 3 posterior, 2 terminal.
“S, L, and F, anterior branches, 3 posterior, 2 terminal… superior thyroid, and next was lingual, and next was facial.”
Maini · L25 Oral Cavity 00:28
Maini’s “exception rule”
🏷️ “PALATO-” IN THE NAME = CN X
Every tongue muscle (all extrinsics AND all intrinsics) is hypoglossal (CN XII) — EXCEPT palatoglossus, which is vagus (CN X), because it is really a palatine muscle. The “-palato-” prefix is the tell.
“Only exception is palatoglossus, because there is ‘Palato’ in its name. It belongs to palate. 10th nerve.”
Maini · L25 Oral Cavity 00:15, 00:19
Maini’s word-root hook
🕳️ CECUM = “BLIND” PASSAGE
The foramen cecum sits at the apex of the terminal sulcus and is the remnant of the thyroglossal duct. “Cecum” means blind — it is a blind pit that ends there and does not lead into another cavity.
“What does the word cecum mean?… it’s just a blind passage. It ends there, does not lead to another cavity.”
Maini · L25 Oral Cavity 00:20
Maini’s flagged trap
🔥 AIR IS WARMED, NOT COOLED
As air crosses the nasal cavity it is warmed, humidified, and filtered. The classic exam trap swaps “warmed” for “cooled.”
“It’s warmed, not cooled… very silly question, but people miss that.”
Maini · L23 Nasal Cavity 00:48
Maini’s flagged trap
🎣 LACRIMAL BONE = NOT SEPTUM
The lacrimal bone helps form the medial orbital wall and the lateral wall of the nasal cavity — never the septum. Maini deliberately “sneaks” it into septum questions as a distractor.
“We sneak lacrimal bone in there… can it be part of the septum? No, because it’s the orbit and the lateral wall.”
Maini · L23 Nasal Cavity 00:55

2 — Nasal walls, septum & conchae

My hooks (not Maini’s) — every fact is from the audited bank.
Nasal septum
🧱 2 BONES + 1 CARTILAGE: ETHMOID (top) · VOMER (back) · CARTILAGE (front)
The bony septum is mostly the perpendicular plate of the ethmoid (upper) + the vomer (posteroinferior). The anterior septum is septal cartilage — the part that bends in a “deviated septum” and is removed in a submucous resection (SMR) + septoplasty.
💡 Picture a capital plus-sign: ethmoid drops from the roof, vomer rises from the floor, they meet in the middle, and the cartilage caps the front where your finger reaches.
bank · Nasal septum (Maini L23)
Nasal roof
🏠 ROOF = “C·S·F·N”: CRIBRIFORM · SPHENOID · FRONTAL · NASAL
Four bones roof the nasal cavity: cribriform plate of the ethmoid (middle — transmits the olfactory nerves), body of the sphenoid (posterior), frontal bone, and nasal bone (anterior).
💡 “C-S-F-N” runs back-to-front; the only working part is the cribriform sieve in the middle that the smell nerves pass through.
bank · Nasal roof
Nasal floor
🦶 FLOOR = ROOF OF THE MOUTH
The nasal floor IS the hard palate: palatine process of the maxilla (front) + horizontal plate of the palatine bone (back). Same two bones serve two rooms.
💡 One slab, two ceilings: from below it roofs the mouth; from above it floors the nose. Learn it once, use it twice.
bank · Nasal floor
Conchae
🐚 SUP + MID = ETHMOID · INFERIOR = ITS OWN BONE
Superior and middle conchae are shelves of the ethmoid bone; the inferior concha is a separate bone in its own right. Conchae increase surface area to warm and humidify air.
💡 Two shelves grow off the ethmoid; the bottom shelf is a stand-alone. Maini’s own flag: “inferior concha is a separate bone, remember.”
bank · Conchae (Maini L23)
Meatuses
📥 MEATUS = POCKET UNDER A CONCHA
A meatus is the space UNDER each concha (superior, middle, inferior). ABOVE the superior concha is the sphenoethmoidal recess.
💡 Each shelf hides a cubbyhole beneath it (the meatus). One extra cubby sits ABOVE the top shelf: the sphenoethmoidal recess — and that’s where the sphenoid sinus empties.
bank · Meatuses

3 — Paranasal sinus drainage

Maini’s advice here was blunt: “draw it, try to know it.” Here is a map to hang it on (my hooks).
Sinus drainage map
🗺️ WHERE EACH SINUS EMPTIES
Sphenoid → sphenoethmoidal recess. Posterior ethmoid → superior meatus. Middle ethmoid → ethmoidal bulla (middle meatus). Frontal, anterior ethmoid, maxillary → hiatus semilunaris (middle meatus). Nasolacrimal duct (not a sinus) → inferior meatus.
💡 Read the wall top-to-bottom: highest recess drains the sphenoid; the superior meatus takes the posterior ethmoid; the busy middle meatus takes everything else; the bottom meatus carries only tears.
bank · Sinus drainage (Maini L23)
Hiatus semilunaris
🌙 HIATUS TOP→BOTTOM = “F·A·M”: FRONTAL · ANT-ETHMOID · MAXILLARY
Inside the crescent hiatus semilunaris: superior end = frontal sinus (via ethmoidal infundibulum / frontonasal duct); middle = anterior ethmoidal cells; inferior end = maxillary sinus.
💡 A crescent moon read top-to-bottom spells F-A-M: Frontal on top, Anterior-ethmoid in the belly, Maxillary at the tip.
bank · Hiatus semilunaris
Ethmoid sinus
🍯 ETHMOID = HONEYCOMB (MANY CELLS, MANY DOORS)
Unlike the frontal, sphenoid, and maxillary sinuses (one cavity, one opening each), the ethmoid is a cluster of air cells in 3 groups — anterior, middle, posterior — each with its own separate opening.
💡 The other three sinuses are single rooms with one door; the ethmoid is a honeycomb of tiny rooms, each needing its own exit — which is why its drainage splits across two meatuses.
bank · Ethmoidal air cells
Clinical
💊 SINUSITIS = SLOW (POOR BLOOD SUPPLY)
Sinuses are poorly vascularized, so antibiotics reach them slowly — Maini says plan on a course of at least ~3 weeks.
💡 Few blood vessels means few delivery trucks; the drug crawls in, so treatment is a marathon, not a sprint.
bank · Sinusitis (Maini L23)

4 — Tongue muscles

My hooks — the origin is hiding in the first half of each name.
Extrinsic muscles — actions
👅 GENIO-PUSHES OUT · HYO-PULLS DOWN · STYLO-PULLS UP & BACK
Genioglossus (fan-shaped bulk) = protrude + depress. Hyoglossus = depress + retract. Styloglossus = retract + elevate the back of the tongue. Palatoglossus = closes the oropharyngeal isthmus.
💡 The front anchor shoves the tongue out (genio), the low anchor drags it down/back (hyo), the high side anchor hauls it up/back (stylo). Origin height predicts the pull.
bank · Tongue muscles
Extrinsic muscles — origins
🏗️ THE PREFIX = THE ORIGIN
Each extrinsic name spells its origin: Genio- = genial tubercle / mental spine of mandible; Hyo- = hyoid (body + greater horn); Stylo- = styloid process; Palato- = palate (palatine aponeurosis).
💡 You never memorize the origin separately — it is baked into the first half of the name. Then just add “-glossus” = to the tongue.
bank · Tongue muscles
Intrinsic muscles
🔀 INTRINSICS = 2 LONGITUDINAL + TRANSVERSE + VERTICAL
Four intrinsic muscles change the tongue’s SHAPE (not its position): superior longitudinal, inferior longitudinal, transverse, vertical. All CN XII.
💡 Two run lengthwise (curl the tip up/down), one side-to-side, one top-to-bottom — together they let the tongue take any shape.
bank · Intrinsic muscles
Palatoglossus
🚪 PALATOGLOSSUS = THE CHEWING GATE
From the palatine aponeurosis, palatoglossus pulls tongue and soft palate together, closing the oropharyngeal isthmus so food is not swallowed before it is chewed. It raises the palatoglossal arch. Nerve: CN X (see Maini’s “-palato-” hook above).
💡 A drawstring between palate and tongue that cinches the doorway shut mid-chew.
bank · Palatoglossus

5 — Tongue sensation, taste & papillae

My hooks — follow each wire back to its ganglion.
Sensory map
🔟 ANT ⅔ = V3 + VII(taste) · POST ⅓ = IX (both)
Anterior two-thirds: general sensation via lingual branch of V3; taste via chorda tympani (CN VII). Posterior one-third: BOTH general + taste via glossopharyngeal (CN IX).
💡 The front needs two nerves (touch and taste on separate wires); the back gets both on one wire (IX).
bank · Tongue innervation
Cell-body ganglia
🧬 TRIGEMINAL · GENICULATE · GLOSSOPHARYNGEAL
Cell bodies: anterior ⅔ general (V3) → trigeminal ganglion; anterior ⅔ taste (VII / chorda tympani) → geniculate ganglion; posterior ⅓ (IX) → glossopharyngeal (superior/inferior) ganglia.
💡 Follow the wire back to its “battery pack”: touch-front to trigeminal, taste-front to geniculate, all-back to glossopharyngeal.
bank · Tongue GSA/SVA cell bodies
Papillae
🌿 4 PAPILLAE: FILIFORM · FUNGIFORM · FOLIATE · VALLATE
Filiform = scattered over the dorsum (most numerous, no taste buds). Fungiform = near the tip and edges. Foliate = lateral folds. Circumvallate (vallate) = a V-row just anterior to the terminal sulcus, taste buds in a surrounding “well.”
💡 Three F’s plus the big Vallate V: fungiform (“mushroom”) dot the tip; the vallate line up in the V of the terminal sulcus.
bank · Lingual papillae
Taste map
🍬 SWEET-TIP · SALTY-SIDES · UMAMI-MID · BITTER-BACK
Maini teaches taste by receptor location: sweet at the tip, salty at the sides of the tip, umami central, bitter along the posterior tongue. Each taste = a chemical class: sweet = sugars, sour = acids (H⁺), salty = Na⁺, bitter = alkaloids, umami = amino acids.
💡 Walk front-to-back: sweet tip, salty shoulders, umami core, bitter tail. Chemistry rides along — acids read sour, sodium reads salty, proteins read umami.
bank · Taste map (Maini L25)
Terminal sulcus
⛰️ TERMINAL SULCUS = ⅔/⅓ BORDER (1ST ARCH IN FRONT)
The V-shaped terminal sulcus splits the anterior ⅔ from the posterior ⅓; the foramen cecum sits at its apex. The anterior ⅔ develops from the first pharyngeal arch (matching V3 general sensation).
💡 The sulcus is the border fence; the blind pit (foramen cecum) marks the old thyroid escape hatch at the point of the V.
bank · Terminal sulcus / development

6 — Salivary glands & oral clinical

My hooks — two secret autonomic routes, plus the “dys-” word roots.
Salivary glands
💧 BIGGEST GLAND ≠ BIGGEST PRODUCER
Parotid = largest gland but only ~30% of saliva. Submandibular = ~60% (the most). Sublingual = ~10%. Size and output disagree: the submandibular wins on volume.
💡 The parotid is the big showy tank; the submandibular is the quiet workhorse that actually pumps the most.
bank · Salivary glands (Maini L25)
Parasympathetic split
🔌 PAROTID = IX / OTIC · SUBMAND+SUBLING = VII / SUBMAND GANGLION
Parotid: CN IX → otic ganglion → auriculotemporal nerve. Submandibular + sublingual: CN VII → chorda tympani → ride the lingual nerve → submandibular ganglion. Sympathetics for all three hitchhike on the external carotid (ECA) plexus.
💡 Two secret routes: the parotid’s runs through the OTIC ganglion (IX); the floor glands’ run through the SUBMANDIBULAR ganglion (VII). Sympathetics just piggyback on the arteries.
bank · Salivary autonomics
Von Ebner glands
🧪 VON EBNER = TASTE-BUD RINSE (LIPASE)
Von Ebner glands are small serous glands on the dorsal tongue (around the vallate papillae) that secrete lingual (salivary) lipase.
💡 Little rinse-taps at the base of the vallate “wells” that wash them out and start fat digestion.
bank · Von Ebner glands
Clinical terms
🗣️ DYSARTHRIA(say) · DYSPHAGIA(swallow) · DYSPHONIA(voice = larynx)
Dysarthria = disordered articulation (language intact; e.g., cerebellar or tongue lesion). Dysphagia = difficulty swallowing. Dysphonia = a voice problem from the larynx (NOT a tongue issue).
💡 Match the root: -arthria to articulate, -phagia to “phago” (eat/swallow), -phonia to phone/sound. Only dysphonia leaves the tongue and points at the larynx.
bank · Tongue clinical (Maini L25)
Tongue lesions
🩹 CHANCRE(syphilis) · CANKER(aphthous, vit C) · TOBACCO→CANCER
Chancre = hard sore of syphilis. Canker sore (aphthous ulcer) = appears when immunity is low; heals faster with vitamin C (collagen synthesis). Tongue/oral cancer is strongly linked to tobacco chewing.
💡 Chancre is the hard infection sore; canker is the stress / low-immunity ulcer that vitamin C helps heal; chewing tobacco is the classic cancer cause.
bank · Tongue clinical
Vascular / clinical
🩸 KIESSELBACH = ANTERIOR NOSEBLEED CORNER
Kiesselbach’s (Little’s) area in the anterior septum/vestibule is the most common epistaxis site — an anastomosis of the septal branch (superior labial, from the facial artery), sphenopalatine, and ethmoidal arteries. Overall nasal supply = maxillary + ophthalmic arteries.
💡 A four-way traffic circle of arteries right where a finger can reach; bump the scab and it gushes. Pinch it, or pack it.
bank · Kiesselbach’s area
