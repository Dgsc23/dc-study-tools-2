1
Master the olfactory nerve (CN I) — its fibre type, receptor cells, the pathway from nose to cortex, and its clinical conditions.
content: L28 · ~00:55–01:01
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Assign the fibre type: CN I carries SVA (special visceral afferent) — the sensation of smell. The receptors are olfactory neurons in the olfactory epithelium near the roof of the nasal cavity; they are chemoreceptors bearing multiple cilia, and the basal cells act as stem cells that replace worn-out receptors. (L28 ~00:55–00:57)
  • Trace the pathway: axons pass through the foramina of the cribriform plate of the ethmoid → synapse on mitral cells & association neurons in the olfactory bulb → the olfactory tract ends in the primary olfactory cortex of the temporal lobe. (L28 ~00:58–00:59)
  • State the structural quirk: there is no single olfactory nerve — it is multiple fascicles of axonal processes. (L28 ~00:59)
  • Know the clinical trio: anosmia = loss of smell (but the neurons can regenerate); phantosmia = smelling something that is not there (olfactory hallucination); olfactory adaptation = continuously stimulated receptors stop responding. (L28 ~00:59–01:01)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“SVA — they carry the sensation of smell … there is no definite single olfactory nerve, there are multiple fascicles … they penetrate cribriform plate of the ethmoid to enter the olfactory bulb, where they synapse … basal cells act as stem cells, they divide and replace the worn-out olfactory receptors.”— Lecture 28 · ~00:55:52–00:59:17
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M13 Cranial Nerves Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
What fibre type does the olfactory nerve carry, through which bony plate does it pass, and where does the olfactory tract end?
SVA (smell). The fascicles pass through the cribriform plate of the ethmoid, synapse in the olfactory bulb (mitral & association neurons), and the tract ends in the primary olfactory cortex of the temporal lobe. Basal cells are stem cells, so olfactory neurons can regenerate.
2
Master the optic nerve (CN II) — that it is ganglion-cell axons, its foramen, the chiasma rule, and the classic lesion patterns.
content: L28 · ~01:01–01:12
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Nail the board favourite: the optic nerve carries SSA (special somatic afferent — because the eye develops from ectoderm) and is composed of the axons of retinal ganglion cells, NOT the axons of rods and cones. Retinal circuit: rods/cones → bipolar cells → ganglion cells → optic nerve. (L28 ~01:01–01:04)
  • Contrast the receptors: cones = colour vision, concentrated centrally; rods = respond to small amounts of light, more peripheral, vitamin-A-derived photopigment. Dark adaptation ~20 min = time for rods to resynthesize photopigment. (L28 ~01:02–01:03)
  • State the foramen and chiasma rule: CN II leaves the orbit through the optic canal (sphenoid); at the optic chiasma only the NASAL retinal fibres cross (temporal fibres do not). The temporal field projects onto the nasal retina. (L28 ~01:05–01:08)
  • Map the lesions: one optic nerve = total blindness in that eye; at the chiasma = bitemporal hemianopia (commonest cause pituitary adenoma; patients often see an ophthalmologist first); one optic tract = loss of the opposite field in both eyes (homonymous). Beyond the midbrain the pathway continues as the optic radiation to the occipital lobe. (L28 ~01:08–01:12)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“It is not the axons of rods and cones that make optic nerves. It is the axons of ganglion cells … only the fibers coming from nasal part of the retina cross over, temporal retinal fibers do not cross … bitemporal hemianopia … the commonest condition causing this is pituitary adenoma.”— Lecture 28 · ~01:04:47–01:09:22
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M13 Cranial Nerves Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
The optic nerve is made of whose axons, which fibres cross at the chiasma, and what defect does a chiasmal lesion cause (with its commonest cause)?
The optic nerve = axons of retinal ganglion cells (not rods/cones). Only the nasal retinal fibres cross at the chiasma. A chiasmal lesion gives bitemporal hemianopia, most commonly from a pituitary adenoma. Foramen = optic canal (sphenoid).
3
Master the oculomotor nerve (CN III) — its two fibre components, the muscles it does (and does NOT) supply, and the full third-nerve-palsy picture.
content: L28 · ~01:12–01:19
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State the two components: motor to eye muscles plus preganglionic parasympathetic (GVE) fibres. CN III emerges from the anterior surface of the midbrain at the interpeduncular fossa, splits into superior & inferior divisions, and enters the orbit through the superior orbital fissure. (L28 ~01:12–01:15)
  • List the muscles it does supply: superior rectus, inferior rectus, medial rectus, inferior oblique, and levator palpebrae superioris (extrinsic); ciliary muscle and sphincter (constrictor) pupillae (intrinsic, parasympathetic). (L28 ~01:15–01:16)
  • List what it does NOT supply: lateral rectus (CN VI) and superior oblique (CN IV) — the two that stay working in a palsy. (L28 ~01:15)
  • Assemble the third-nerve palsy: eye down and out (intact LR + SO), ptosis (levator palpebrae out), mydriasis (dilated pupil), loss of accommodation (ciliary out), and no direct light reflex in that eye though the consensual reflex in the other eye is intact. Accommodation = convergence (medial rectus) + pupil constriction + ciliary contraction making the lens more convex. (L28 ~01:16–01:19)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Superior rectus, inferior rectus, inferior oblique … and levator palpebrae superioris. Do not forget this muscle … the eyeball will be deviated inferiorly and laterally … your lateral rectus is still intact, your superior oblique is still functioning.”— Lecture 28 · ~01:15:26–01:17:20
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M13 Cranial Nerves Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which two eye muscles does CN III NOT supply, and why is the eye deviated “down and out” in a third-nerve palsy?
CN III does not supply the lateral rectus (CN VI) or the superior oblique (CN IV). In a palsy those two stay intact and pull the eye down and out. Add ptosis (levator out), mydriasis, lost accommodation, and lost direct light reflex (consensual intact in the other eye).
4
Separate Horner syndrome from a third-nerve palsy — the pupil is the tell, and know WHY there is no squint in Horner (the “no-sympathetic” trap).
content: L28 · ~01:19–01:21
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Use the pupil as the decider (Maini’s rule): miosis = Horner syndrome; mydriasis = third-nerve palsy. (L28 ~01:21)
  • Name the one shared sign: ptosis (mild in Horner, from loss of the sympathetic tarsal muscle; frank in CN III palsy). (L28 ~01:20)
  • Explain the Horner miosis: loss of sympathetic supply to the dilator pupillae. Horner also brings anhidrosis and facial flushing. (L28 ~01:20)
  • Lock in the trap: there is NO squint (strabismus) in Horner syndrome, because sympathetic fibres do not innervate any extraocular muscle — whereas the CN III palsy has squint (and the down-and-out eye). (L28 ~01:20–01:21)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“If it is miosis, Horner syndrome. If it is mydriasis, third nerve palsy … squint is not found in Horner syndrome … because sympathetic fibers do not innervate any of the extraocular muscles … pupil is the best thing to look at.”— Lecture 28 · ~01:20:54–01:21:57
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M13 Cranial Nerves Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
A patient has ptosis and a constricted pupil but no squint. Which diagnosis, and what single fact rules out the other one?
Horner syndrome (miosis). The absence of squint rules out a third-nerve palsy, because sympathetic fibres do not supply any extraocular muscle. Pupil rule: miosis = Horner, mydriasis = CN III; ptosis is the one sign they share.
5
Recall the pharynx quick-review anchors — the three parts, the piriform fossa danger, Waldeyer’s ring, the upper esophageal sphincter, and the deglutition centre.
content: L28 · ~00:00–00:24
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Name the three parts: nasopharynx, oropharynx, laryngopharynx. The key laryngopharynx feature is the piriform fossa — clinically important because the internal laryngeal nerve lies superficially there and a sharp swallowed foreign body can injure it. (L28 ~00:00–00:02)
  • Place the internal laryngeal nerve: a branch of the vagus (via the superior laryngeal), sensory to the larynx above the vocal cords. (L28 ~00:02)
  • List Waldeyer’s ring: 1 pharyngeal tonsil (clinically the adenoid), 2 tubal, 2 palatine, 1 lingual — all secondary lymphatic tissue (primary = bone marrow & thymus). (L28 ~00:06–00:09)
  • Know the muscle/swallowing anchors: cricopharyngeus (part of the inferior constrictor) forms the upper esophageal sphincter; stylopharyngeus arises from the 3rd pharyngeal arch; the deglutition centre sits in the pons and medulla, and the oral stage is voluntary. (L28 ~00:04–00:24)

“Nasopharynx, oropharynx, laryngopharynx. Three parts … internal laryngeal nerve is very superficial, right under the mucous membrane in this part … pharyngeal tonsil (one), tubal tonsils (two), two palatine tonsils, and one lingual tonsil … they create Waldeyer’s ring.”— Lecture 28 · ~00:00:36–00:07:56
🎯 Cement it

🧪 Quick self-check
Why is the piriform fossa clinically important, and which five tonsils make up Waldeyer’s ring?
The internal laryngeal nerve lies superficially in the piriform fossa, so a sharp swallowed foreign body can injure it. Waldeyer’s ring = 1 pharyngeal + 2 tubal + 2 palatine + 1 lingual tonsil (secondary lymphatic tissue). Cricopharyngeus = upper esophageal sphincter; deglutition centre = pons + medulla.
6
Wire the cranial-nerve control of the pharynx and swallowing — the pharyngeal plexus, the motor and sensory maps, and the nerves of deglutition.
content: L28 · ~00:03–00:24
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State the pharyngeal plexus = glossopharyngeal (IX) + vagus (X). (L28 ~00:09–00:10)
  • Give the motor rule: all pharyngeal muscles are supplied by the vagus (X) EXCEPT stylopharyngeus, which is by the glossopharyngeal (IX). (The cranial part of XI joins the vagus, so you are not asked to choose between X and XI for these muscles.) (L28 ~00:03–00:15)
  • Give the sensory map: nasopharynx = V2 (maxillary), oropharynx = IX (glossopharyngeal), laryngopharynx / laryngeal inlet = internal laryngeal branch of the vagus. (L28 ~00:15–00:17)
  • Give the swallowing wiring: the reflex (pharyngeal) stage motor impulses run via CN V, IX, X, and XII, and the reflex centre is the nucleus of the tractus solitarius in the medulla. (L28 ~00:23–00:24)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“It is a plexus of nerves on the side and back of the pharynx. It is formed by 9th and 10th nerves … all the pharyngeal muscles are innervated by the 10th nerve, except for stylopharyngeus … glossopharyngeal, 9th nerve … motor impulses are transmitted by 5th, 9th, 10th, and 12th nerve.”— Lecture 28 · ~00:03:55–00:23:55
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M13 Cranial Nerves Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which two nerves form the pharyngeal plexus, which pharyngeal muscle is the lone exception to vagal motor supply, and which four nerves carry the swallowing motor impulses?
Pharyngeal plexus = IX + X. All pharyngeal muscles are vagus-supplied except stylopharyngeus (glossopharyngeal, IX). Swallowing motor = V, IX, X, XII. Sensory: nasopharynx V2, oropharynx IX, laryngopharynx internal laryngeal of the vagus.
7
Know the four prevertebral muscles — for each, its origin, insertion, action, and nerve supply, and the anterior-vs-lateralis contrast.
content: L28 · ~00:25–00:33
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Name the four (Maini: know O / I / action / nerve for each): longus capitis, longus colli, rectus capitis anterior, rectus capitis lateralis. (L28 ~00:25–00:26)
  • Longus capitis: superiorly to the basilar part of the occipital bone, inferiorly to the anterior tubercles of the TPs of C3–C6; nerve = ventral rami of C1–C3; flexes the head. (L28 ~00:26–00:27)
  • Longus colli has three parts — superior oblique (TP of C3–C5 → anterior tubercle of atlas), vertical (bodies C5–T3 → bodies C2–C4), inferior oblique (bodies T1–T3 → TP of C5–C7); flexes the neck, oblique parts add ipsilateral lateral flexion, inferior oblique rotates the neck to the opposite side. (L28 ~00:27–00:31)
  • Nail the contrast: rectus capitis anterior attaches superiorly to the basilar part of the occipital bone; rectus capitis lateralis attaches superiorly to the jugular process (lateralis produces lateral flexion). Both are supplied by the ventral rami of C1–C2. (L28 ~00:31–00:33)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“There are 4 muscles we are going to talk about … you know what to remember about the muscles: origin, insertion, nerve supply, and action … anterior is attached to basilar process, lateralis is attached to jugular process.”— Lecture 28 · ~00:26:25–00:33:18
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M13 Cranial Nerves Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
What are the three parts of longus colli, and what is the single superior-attachment difference between rectus capitis anterior and lateralis?
Longus colli = superior oblique, vertical (intermediate), inferior oblique. Rectus capitis anterior attaches superiorly to the basilar part of the occipital bone; lateralis attaches to the jugular process. Four muscles total; know O/I/action/nerve for each.
8
Map the cervical sympathetic trunk — its three ganglia, their locations and relations, the gray-rami distribution, and why the superior ganglion dominates.
content: L28 · ~00:33–00:43
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Set the basics: 3 ganglia (superior, middle, inferior) with postganglionic cell bodies; the preganglionic fibres ascend from the lateral horn of T1–T2; the trunk runs from the neck of the first rib to the base of the skull, posterior to the carotid arteries (a carotid aneurysm can compress it) and between the carotid sheath and prevertebral fascia. (L28 ~00:33–00:37)
  • State the high-yield fact: ~99% of head-and-neck structures get their postganglionic sympathetics from the SUPERIOR cervical ganglion (located opposite C2–C3, behind the internal carotid, anterior to longus capitis; branch = internal carotid nerve). (L28 ~00:37–00:39)
  • Give the gray-rami split: superior → C1–C4, middle → C5–C6, inferior → C7–C8. Gray rami exist to carry sympathetics to three body-wall structures: sweat glands, arrector pili muscles, and blood vessels (every spinal nerve carries sympathetic, but no parasympathetic). (L28 ~00:37–00:40)
  • Finish the ganglia: middle = opposite C6, on the inferior thyroid artery; inferior is usually fused with T1 = stellate ganglion, lying between the C7 TP and neck of the first rib (branches to subclavian/vertebral arteries). Referred cardiac pain to the neck/head follows the cardiac sympathetic nerves. (L28 ~00:40–00:43)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“99% of the structures of head and neck region get their postganglionic sympathetic fibers from superior cervical ganglion only … gray rami communicantes to C1 to C4 … C5, C6 middle; C7, C8 inferior … inferior cervical ganglion and your first thoracic ganglion are fused together to form stellate ganglion. These cervical ganglia appear in your board exams.”— Lecture 28 · ~00:37:20–00:42:42
🎯 Cement it
📜 GA 0202 · M13 Cranial Nerves Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which cervical ganglion supplies ~99% of head-and-neck structures, how do the three ganglia split the gray rami, and what is the stellate ganglion?
The superior cervical ganglion (opposite C2–C3) supplies ~99% of head/neck. Gray rami: superior → C1–C4, middle → C5–C6, inferior → C7–C8. The stellate ganglion = inferior cervical fused with the T1 ganglion, between the C7 TP and neck of the first rib.
9
Lay out the four layers of deep cervical fascia — what each encloses, and the three structures in the carotid sheath.
content: L28 · ~00:44–00:53
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Name the four layers: investing, pretracheal, prevertebral, and the carotid sheath (Maini: don’t memorize every bony attachment — know what each layer encloses). (L28 ~00:44–00:52)
  • Investing layer: encloses sternocleidomastoid (anteriorly) and trapezius (posteriorly); thickens to form the stylomandibular ligament; forms the roof of the anterior & posterior triangles and attaches to the ligamentum nuchae. (L28 ~00:44–00:51)
  • Pretracheal encloses the thyroid gland and infrahyoid muscles and inferiorly merges with the fibrous pericardium; prevertebral encloses the prevertebral muscles and inferiorly blends with the anterior longitudinal ligament (ALL). (L28 ~00:47–00:49)
  • State the carotid sheath contents: the common/internal carotid artery, the internal jugular vein, and the vagus nerve. (L28 ~00:52)

“There are 4 layers: investing, pretracheal, prevertebral, and carotid sheath … it encloses sternocleidomastoid anteriorly and trapezius posteriorly … carotid sheath around the common and internal carotid artery, internal jugular vein, and vagus nerve.”— Lecture 28 · ~00:44:50–00:52:57

🧪 Quick self-check
Name the four layers of deep cervical fascia, and the three structures inside the carotid sheath.
