Topic 1 — The first three nerves at a glance

Names, modality, and the hole each one leaves the skull through.
Names of CN I, II, III ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
🏔️ On · Old · Olympus = Olfactory · Optic · Oculomotor
The three nerves of this module in order: CN I Olfactory, CN II Optic, CN III Oculomotor. The classic first-letter phrase begins “On Old Olympus…” — and the first three all start with O.
💡 Triple-O opener: three O-words for three O-nerves. Olfactory (smell), Optic (sight), Oculomotor (moves the eye).
Grounded in: Maini L28 · CN I–III (Everything-MC bank)
Modality of I, II, III ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
🔤 S · S · M = Sensory · Sensory · Motor
The classic “Some Say Marry…” modality phrase: I = Sensory (SVA smell), II = Sensory (SSA sight), III = Motor (motor to eye muscles plus a parasympathetic add-on). So the first two are pure input; the third is output.
💡 “Some Say Money” for the first three: Sense, Sense, Move. CN III is the first one that does something rather than just feeling something.
Grounded in: bank Q9, Q20, Q35 · Maini L28
Foramen for each nerve ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
🕳️ CRIBRIFORM · CANAL · FISSURE (I · II · III)
CN I → foramina of the cribriform plate of the ethmoid. CN II → optic canal (in the sphenoid). CN III → superior orbital fissure. As you go I→II→III the opening gets bigger: sieve-holes, then a round canal, then a slot.
💡 “Cribriform, Canal, Fissure” — two C’s then an F. Smell drips through the sieve, sight rides the canal, the eye-mover slips through the fissure.
Grounded in: bank Q13, Q26, Q37 · Maini L28
Cranial part of CN XI 🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
🎰 THE FIBRES “CHANGE PARTY” → JOIN THE VAGUS
The cranial part of the accessory nerve (XI) hands its motor (SVE) fibres to the vagus (X), which then distributes them to the pharyngeal and laryngeal muscles. That is why we only test XI on trapezius & SCM (the spinal part).
🎙️ Maini: “They just change their party. They don’t like the name accessory… let’s go to Vegas.” (her pun: the cranial XI fibres run off to join the vagus).

Topic 2 — CN I: Olfactory

A sensory-only nerve of many little threads, plus three “smell” clinical words.
What CN I is & carries ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
👃 SVA CHEMO-SNIFFERS ON THE ROOF
CN I carries SVA fibres (special visceral afferent = smell). The receptor olfactory neurons sit in the olfactory epithelium near the roof of the nasal cavity, each bearing multiple cilia, and each is a chemoreceptor. Basal cells act as stem cells that replace worn-out receptors.
💡 Picture ceiling-mounted “smell antennas”: cilia dangle from the roof, dissolve the odour chemical, and fire. Basal cells are the maintenance crew swapping in fresh sensors.
Grounded in: bank Q9–Q12 · Maini L28
Olfactory pathway ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
🧵 THREADS → SIEVE → BULB → TEMPORAL LOBE
No single trunk — multiple fascicles pierce the cribriform plate, reach the olfactory bulb, and synapse on mitral cells & association neurons. Their axons form the olfactory tract, which ends in the primary olfactory cortex of the temporal lobe.
💡 Smell is the one sense that skips the thalamic “front door” relay for its first stop — threads go straight up through the sieve to the bulb, then back to the temporal lobe.
Grounded in: bank Q13–Q16 · Maini L28
Why smell fades with age 🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
🌸 “OLD PEOPLE WEAR A LOT OF PERFUME”
Olfactory receptors degenerate with aging, so smell dulls over time (anosmia can also follow trauma or COVID). The good news Maini stresses: these neurons can regenerate.
🎙️ Maini: “That’s why old people, they wear a lot of perfume, because they themselves cannot smell enough.”
Maini L28 · ~59:24 · bank Q17
The three “smell” words ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
🚫 NO smell · 👻 PHANTOM smell · 😴 STOP noticing
Anosmia = loss of smell. Phantosmia = a phantom smell that isn’t there (olfactory hallucination). Olfactory adaptation = a real smell you stop noticing after constant exposure.
💡 Sort by the prefix: An- = absent, Phant- = phantom, and adaptation is the “you got used to the room” one. Only phantosmia invents a smell.
Grounded in: bank Q17–Q19 · Maini L28

Topic 3 — CN II: Optic

Ganglion-cell axons, the retina’s wiring, and the three lesion patterns Maini draws.
What actually forms CN II ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
🎯 GANGLION-CELL AXONS (not rods/cones!)
Maini flags this as a frequent board question: the optic nerve is made of the axons of retinal ganglion cells — not the axons of rods and cones. Its fibre type is SSA (special somatic afferent) because the eye develops from ectoderm.
💡 Trap-proofing: the photoreceptors feed into the nerve, but the nerve is the ganglion cell’s cable. If a stem says “axons of rods and cones,” it’s wrong.
Grounded in: bank Q20–Q21 · Maini L28
Retinal wiring order ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
🔗 ROD/CONE → BIPOLAR → GANGLION → NERVE
Signal flow: rods/cones project onto bipolar cells, which project onto ganglion cells, whose axons become the optic nerve. Cones = colour, packed centrally. Rods = dim light, more peripheral, with a vitamin-A photopigment (hence ~20 min dark adaptation).
💡 Three cells, one cable: R/C → Bipolar → Ganglion. Central = Cones for Colour; peripheral rods see the shape in the dark but not the colour.
Grounded in: bank Q22–Q25 · Maini L28
The chiasma rule ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
❌ ONLY NASAL FIBRES CROSS
At the optic chiasma, only the nasal-retina fibres cross; temporal fibres stay uncrossed. And remember the flip: the temporal visual field lands on the nasal retina (light crosses through the pupil).
💡 “Nasal crossN’s over.” Nasal fibres are the travelers; temporal fibres stay home. Field-to-retina is always the opposite word (temporal field → nasal retina).
Grounded in: bank Q27–Q28 · Maini L28
Where CN II leaves the orbit ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
🦴 OPTIC CANAL, IN THE SPHENOID
The optic nerve exits the eyeball and leaves the orbit through the optic canal, which Maini places in the sphenoid bone, to enter the cranial cavity.
💡 Don’t confuse it with CN III’s route: CN II has its own dedicated canal; CN III uses the neighbouring superior orbital fissure.
Grounded in: bank Q26 · Maini L28
The three lesion patterns ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
👁️ NERVE = whole eye · CHIASMA = tunnel · TRACT = opposite side
One optic nerve cut → total blindness in that eye. Chiasma lesion (kills crossing nasal fibres) → bitemporal hemianopia (“tunnel”), classically from a pituitary adenoma sitting right beside it. Optic tract lesion (e.g. left) → loss of the opposite (right) field in both eyes.
💡 Walk it front-to-back: before the crossing = one eye; at the crossing = both temporal fields (pituitary pressing the middle); after the crossing = one whole side. Pituitary patients often see the eye doctor first.
Grounded in: bank Q29–Q33 · Maini L28

Topic 4 — CN III: Oculomotor

The muscles it runs, the two it doesn’t, and the full palsy picture.
Muscles CN III supplies ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
💪 ALL RECTI BUT LATERAL + IO + the LID-LIFTER
Extrinsic (5): superior rectus, inferior rectus, medial rectus, inferior oblique, and the eyelid-raiser levator palpebrae superioris. Intrinsic (2, parasympathetic): ciliary muscle + sphincter (constrictor) pupillae. It does NOT supply lateral rectus (CN VI) or superior oblique (CN IV).
💡 CN III runs the whole eye-muscle crew except the two “LR6SO4” loners — Lateral Rectus (6) and Superior Oblique (4). Maini’s reminder: “do not forget levator palpebrae superioris.”
Grounded in: bank Q38–Q40, Q46–Q47 · Maini L28
CN III’s autonomic half ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
🌙 PARASYMPATHETIC = CILIARY + SPHINCTER PUPILLAE
CN III also carries preganglionic parasympathetic (GVE) fibres to the ciliary muscle (rounds the lens for near focus) and the sphincter pupillae (constricts the pupil). It emerges from the anterior midbrain at the interpeduncular fossa and enters the orbit via the superior orbital fissure.
💡 The “rest-and-focus” half: para-CN III makes the pupil small and the lens fat for reading. Lose it and both jobs fail (see the palsy card).
Grounded in: bank Q35–Q37, Q40, Q44 · Maini L28
Which way the eye points 🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
↘️ “DOWN AND OUT”
In a complete third-nerve palsy the eyeball sits down and out: the only two survivors, superior oblique (down) and lateral rectus (out), pull it there unopposed.
🎙️ Maini: “Down and out. Lateral rectus is also out, so the eyeball will be deviated inferiorly and laterally.”
Maini L28 · ~1:17:14 · bank Q41
Full CN III palsy picture ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
🩹 DOWN-&-OUT · DROOP · BLOWN PUPIL · NO FOCUS
Add it up: eye down & out (strabismus); ptosis (levator palpebrae out); mydriasis = dilated pupil (sphincter out); lost accommodation (ciliary out). The direct light reflex is gone in that eye, but the consensual reflex in the other eye is intact.
💡 Every CN III job fails at once: can’t aim it (down/out), can’t lift the lid (droop), can’t shrink the pupil (blown), can’t focus near (no accommodation). The other eye still constricts when you shine light in the bad one.
Grounded in: bank Q41–Q45, Q48 · Maini L28

Topic 5 — Horner vs CN III (the “no-sympathetic” trap)

Both droop the lid. The pupil and the squint tell them apart.
The one-look rule 🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
👁️ SMALL = HORNER · BIG = THIRD NERVE
Both cause ptosis, so the pupil is the decider: a small (constricted) pupil = Horner (sympathetic failure → lost dilator pupillae); a big (dilated) pupil = third-nerve palsy (parasympathetic failure → unopposed dilator).
🎙️ Maini: “If it is miosis, Horner syndrome. If it is mydriasis, third nerve palsy… pupil is the best thing to look at.”
Maini L28 · ~1:21:06 · bank Q49–Q50, Q53, Q56
THE TRAP ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
🚫 NO SQUINT IN HORNER
Horner has no strabismus/squint because sympathetic fibres never innervate any extraocular muscle. Third-nerve palsy does squint (its extraocular muscles are paralysed). So squint present = think CN III, not Horner.
💡 The “no-sympathetic” trap: sympathetics only run the pupil dilator, sweat glands, and vessels of the eye region — zero eye-movement muscles. A squinting, drooping eye is not a Horner.
Grounded in: bank Q52 · Maini L28
Rest of the Horner picture ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
🅿️ P.A.M. = Ptosis · Anhidrosis · Miosis
Horner’s core triad: Ptosis (mild/partial), Anhidrosis, Miosis. Maini also notes facial flushing. The miosis comes from losing the dilator pupillae (sympathetic), leaving the parasympathetic constrictor unopposed.
💡 “PAM is a bit sympathetic-shy”: droopy lid, dry face, tiny pupil — the whole sympathetic supply to that side of the face has gone quiet.
Grounded in: bank Q51, Q53–Q55 · Maini L28

Topic 6 — Prevertebral muscles & the cervical sympathetic trunk

Four little neck-flexors and the three ganglia that feed the head.
The four prevertebral muscles ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
4️⃣ 2 LONGUS + 2 RECTUS CAPITIS
Know four (origin, insertion, action, nerve): longus capitis, longus colli, rectus capitis anterior, rectus capitis lateralis. Longus capitis: basilar occiput ↔ anterior tubercles of C3–C6, ventral rami C1–C3. All are neck/head flexors (lateralis = lateral flexion).
💡 Two “long” ones down the front of the spine, two “straight little” ones (rectus) at the very top by the skull. That’s the whole prevertebral family.
Grounded in: bank Q57–Q60, Q69 · Maini L28
Longus colli’s three parts 🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
🔀 BODY→TP · TP→BODY · BODIES→BODIES
Longus colli has three parts: inferior oblique = body (T1–T3) to transverse process (C5–C7); superior oblique = transverse process (C3–C5) to body (anterior tubercle of atlas); vertical = bodies (C5–T3) to bodies (C2–C4).
🎙️ Maini: “Body to TP, TP to body, and bodies to bodies. That is how this muscle has 3 parts.”
Maini L28 · ~30:23 · bank Q61–Q64
Rectus capitis A vs Lateralis 🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
🧭 ANTERIOR→BASILAR · LATERALIS→JUGULAR
Both hang off the atlas below; the top attachment is the tell. Rectus capitis anterior → basilar part of the occiput. Rectus capitis lateralis → jugular process of the occiput. Both take ventral rami C1–C2.
🎙️ Maini: “Anterior is attached to basilar process. Lateralis is attached to jugular process.”
Maini L28 · ~32:22 · bank Q66–Q69
Three cervical ganglia ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
🔢 GRAY RAMI 1–4 / 5–6 / 7–8
Three ganglia (postganglionic sympathetic cell bodies), fed by preganglionics from T1–T2. Gray rami: Superior → C1–C4, Middle → C5–C6, Inferior → C7–C8. The SCG feeds ~99% of head & neck; the inferior + T1 ganglion fuse into the stellate ganglion.
💡 Count top-down 1234 / 56 / 78 — the numbers just march down the neck. Gray rami exist to send sympathetics to sweat glands, arrector pili, and blood vessels of the body wall.
Grounded in: bank Q70–Q79, Q85 · Maini L28

Topic 7 — Deep cervical fascia & pharynx review

Four fascial layers, two mandibular ligaments, and the swallowing hooks Maini gave.
Four fascial layers ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
🧅 INVEST · PRE-TRACHEAL · PRE-VERTEBRAL · CAROTID
Deep cervical fascia = 4 layers. Investing (wraps SCM + trapezius), pretracheal (thyroid + infrahyoids; blends with fibrous pericardium below), prevertebral (prevertebral muscles; blends with the ALL), and the carotid sheath. Sheath contents = VAN: Vein (IJV), Artery (common/internal carotid), Nerve (vagus).
💡 Layers wrap outside-in like an onion; the carotid sheath is the tube in the middle carrying the “VAN” — vein, artery, nerve.
Grounded in: bank Q87–Q94 · Maini L28
Two mandibular ligaments 🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
🧭 SPHENO = INTERNAL · STYLO = EXTERNAL
The investing layer thickens between the styloid process and the mandible to form the stylomandibular ligament (to the angle of the mandible, more external). Its partner, the sphenomandibular ligament, runs deeper to the lingula (more internal).
🎙️ Maini: “Spine of the sphenoid is more internal. Styloid process is more external.” (so spheno → lingula, stylo → angle).
Maini L28 · ~51:41 · bank Q89
How peristalsis works 🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
🎾 A BALL PUSHED DOWN A RUBBER TUBE
Peristalsis pushes the bolus onward: a constriction ring forms behind it while the segment ahead relaxes, so the bolus is squeezed forward — the same trick as forcing a ball down a tube its own diameter.
🎙️ Maini: “Think about a rubber tube, and a ball you’re trying to push through… create a constriction ring posterior to the ball, and move it along the tube — the ball moves forward.”
Maini L28 · ~22:17 · bank Q103
Pharynx sensory map 🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
🏠 SAME CAVITY → SAME SENSORY NERVE
Each pharynx region shares the nerve of the cavity it continues from: nasopharynx = V2 (maxillary), oropharynx = IX (glossopharyngeal), laryngopharynx = internal laryngeal (from vagus). Motor to the pharyngeal muscles = vagus, except stylopharyngeus = IX.
🎙️ Maini: “It’s like the extension of the same cavity, and so they share the sensory innervation.”
Maini L28 · ~17:07 · bank Q1, Q5–Q7
Waldeyer’s tonsil ring ✏️ Illustrative — my study aid
💍 1 · 2 · 2 · 1 = 6 TONSILS
Waldeyer’s ring counts 1 pharyngeal (the adenoid, in the roof) + 2 tubal + 2 palatine + 1 lingual = six tonsils. All are secondary lymphatic tissue (primary = bone marrow & thymus).
💡 Symmetric count 1-2-2-1: singles top (pharyngeal) and bottom (lingual), pairs in the middle (tubal, palatine). A “security ring” guarding the throat’s entrances.
