Topic 1 — 🎤 Dr. Maini’s OWN memory hooks (quoted verbatim)

These six hooks are hers, straight from lecture. Learn these first — they are the ones she actually said out loud.
Eye-muscle nerves (IV, VI, III)
👁️ SO4 · LR6 · AO3
Superior Oblique = CN 4 (trochlear). Lateral Rectus = CN 6 (abducens). All Other extraocular muscles = CN 3 (oculomotor). This is Maini’s own formula for wiring the eye muscles to their nerves.
Dr. Maini said:“It goes like SO4, LR6, AO3… superior oblique, that is your trochlear nerve.” — and for the leftovers: “if you filter out superior oblique and lateral rectus, then rest 4 of them… all the four” (belong to CN III).
Dr. Maini — L20 The Orbit · MC bank Q1, Q8, Q9
Superior oblique action (CN IV)
↘️ “DOWN AND OUT”
When the superior oblique (CN IV) acts, the eyeball goes down and laterally. Maini hands you the phrase to remember it: “down and out.” (Clinically, a IV palsy = trouble looking down-and-in, e.g. descending stairs.)
Dr. Maini said:“It rotates the eyeball down and laterally. So you can remember this as down and out.”
Dr. Maini — L20 The Orbit · MC bank Q4, Q7
Carotid sheath contents (CN X)
🤺 THE “THREE MUSKETEERS”
Inside the carotid sheath travel three structures together: the common carotid artery, the internal jugular vein, and the vagus nerve (CN X). Maini calls them the three musketeers.
Dr. Maini said:“Common carotid and vagus… three musketeers… wrapped in the carotid sheath.”
Dr. Maini — L05 Cervical Viscera · MC bank Q66
Geniculate ganglion (CN VII)
🦵 GENICULATE = at the GENU (the bend)
The geniculate ganglion sits at the genu — the bend of the facial nerve inside the facial canal. Maini gives you the name’s meaning as the memory hook (genu = knee/bend, as in “genuflect”).
Dr. Maini said:“It is called geniculate because it is at the genu, the bend.”
Dr. Maini — L08 Face & Scalp · MC bank Q36
Naming the abducens (CN VI)
👉 ABDUCENS runs the ABDUCTOR
The abducens nerve (CN VI) supplies the lateral rectus, the muscle that abducts the eye (turns it outward). Maini ties the name to the job so you never lose it.
Dr. Maini said:“This nerve supplies the abductor of the eye, so abducens.”
Dr. Maini — L17 The Brain · MC bank Q10
Stylopharyngeus (CN IX)
💅 “VERY STYLISH MUSCLE”
Of all the pharyngeal muscles, stylopharyngeus is the ONLY one not supplied by the vagus — it is CN IX (glossopharyngeal), because it comes from the third pharyngeal arch. In her Week 11/12 lab Maini nicknamed it the “very stylish muscle.”
Dr. Maini said:“Stylopharyngeus… very stylish muscle… ninth nerve… because it originates from third pharyngeal arch.” (from her auto-transcribed lab Q&A)
Dr. Maini — L29 Week 11/12 Labs · MC bank Q56, Q63

Topic 2 — Foramina & their tenants (the big lists)

Which nerve leaves through which hole. The single most-tested list — learn the door, then the roster behind it.
Middle-fossa order (Maini’s sequence)
🚪 SOF → ROTUNDUM → OVALE → SPINOSUM
Maini has you list the sphenoid openings medial to lateral: Superior orbital fissure → foramen Rotundum (V2) → foramen Ovale (V3) → foramen Spinosum (middle meningeal artery). That order also walks you down the trigeminal divisions.
💡 Read them like a row of doors getting rounder then bigger: fissure, Rotundum, Ovale, spinosum. V2 takes the round one, V3 takes the oval one.
Dr. Maini quote — L01 Osteology · MC bank Q31
Superior orbital fissure roster
🎟️ SOF CLUB: III · IV · V1 · VI
The superior orbital fissure carries CN III, CN IV, V1 (ophthalmic), and CN VI. (Optic nerve II uses its own optic canal; V2 uses rotundum; V3 uses ovale.)
💡 The SOF is the crowded club door — four nerves squeeze through together (III, IV, V1, VI). Only the optic nerve is exclusive enough to get its own canal.
Dr. Maini — L19 The Orbit · MC bank Q5, Q12, Q13, Q18
Jugular foramen roster
🕳️ 9-10-11 LEAVE TOGETHER + the vein
The jugular foramen transmits CN IX, X, and XI — and it is where the sigmoid sinus becomes the internal jugular vein. Three nerves, one exit.
💡 Count them off — 9, 10, 11 ride out one door, and the sinus turns into the IJV in the same hole. If a stem groups IX/X/XI, it’s the jugular foramen.
Dr. Maini — L28 Pharynx · MC bank Q55, Q65, Q79, Q96, Q105
Internal acoustic meatus roster
👂 IAM CARPOOL: VII + VIII
The internal acoustic meatus carries CN VII (facial) and CN VIII (vestibulocochlear) together into the petrous temporal bone. (This is why one tumor here — an acoustic neuroma — can hit both.)
💡 7 and 8 carpool into the same meatus. VII then drives on out the stylomastoid foramen; VIII stays in the inner ear for good.
Dr. Maini — L22 The Ear · MC bank Q33, Q47, Q53, Q96
Trigeminal exits, top to bottom
🎟️ “STANDING ROOM ONLY” — SOF · Rotundum · Ovale
V1 ophthalmic → Superior orbital fissure. V2 maxillary → foramen Rotundum. V3 mandibular → foramen Ovale. V1 and V2 are purely sensory; V3 is sensory and motor.
💡 “S.R.O. = Standing Room Only” walks the exits in order V1→V2→V3. (My study aid, not Maini’s.) Only the last ticket — V3 / ovale — also carries the motor seat.
Dr. Maini — L01 Osteology / L10 Infratemporal · MC bank Q18–Q20
Solo doors: XII & VII-out
🚪 XII = its own canal · VII exits STYLOmastoid
The hypoglossal nerve (XII) gets its own hypoglossal canal in the occipital bone. The facial nerve (VII), after the facial canal, exits the skull at the stylomastoid foramen, then pierces the parotid gland.
💡 The tongue nerve has a private door named after it (hypoglossal canal). VII enters at the meatus but leaves by a different door — the stylomastoid.
Dr. Maini — L08 Face · L01 Osteology · MC bank Q34, Q86, Q93

Topic 3 — Eye movers: IV Trochlear & VI Abducens

Two pure-motor nerves, each running exactly one eye muscle. IV is the oddball off the BACK of the brainstem; VI is the fragile long-runner.
Trochlear (IV) — emergence
🔙 IV = the only nerve off the BACK of the brainstem
The trochlear nerve (CN IV) is the only cranial nerve to emerge from the posterior (dorsal) surface of the midbrain. Its tendon then loops through a fibrocartilaginous pulley — the trochlea — which is what gives the nerve its name.
💡 If a stem says “dorsal/posterior midbrain”, think trochlear. And “trochlea” = pulley: the muscle’s tendon runs through a little pulley to change its angle of pull.
Dr. Maini — L17 The Brain / L20 Orbit · MC bank Q2, Q3
Abducens (VI) — lesion
👁️ LR GONE → EYE LOOKS IN (medial)
A CN VI palsy paralyzes the lateral rectus → the unopposed medial rectus pulls the eye inward → medial deviation of the affected eye with horizontal diplopia (double vision).
💡 The only muscle that drags the eye outward is gone, so the eyeball drifts IN toward the nose. Medial deviation + horizontal double vision = abducens.
Dr. Maini — L20 Orbit · MC bank Q11
Abducens (VI) — the fragile one
🎈 RAISED PRESSURE → VI FAILS FIRST
Because of its long intracranial course, the abducens (CN VI) is often the first extraocular nerve affected by raised intracranial pressure, producing horizontal diplopia. Both IV and VI are purely motor to a single muscle.
💡 Think of VI as the longest, most exposed cable in the skull — stretch the head with pressure and it’s the first to snap, giving early double vision.
Clinical anatomy w/ CN VI (Dr. Maini) · MC bank Q14, Q15

Topic 4 — Trigeminal V (V1 · V2 · V3)

A sensory ganglion on the “rock,” then three divisions through three holes. Only V3 is also motor — the chewing division.
Ganglion & three divisions
🪨 V SITS ON THE ROCK (petrous apex)
CN V has three divisions: V1 ophthalmic, V2 maxillary, V3 mandibular. Its large sensory ganglion (trigeminal / semilunar) sits at the apex of the petrous part of the temporal bone — and it is a sensory ganglion, NOT autonomic.
💡 Petrous = “rock” in Latin, so the ganglion perches on the tip of the temporal rock. It holds sensory cell bodies — it is not one of the parasympathetic ganglia.
Dr. Maini — L11 Infratemporal Fossa · MC bank Q17, Q24, Q25
V3 — the only motor division
🦷 V3 = THE ONLY CHEWER (arch 1)
V3 (mandibular, via foramen ovale) is the ONLY trigeminal division with motor fibers — to the muscles of mastication (temporalis, masseter, medial & lateral pterygoids). V + the chewing muscles come from the 1st pharyngeal arch. Nearly all V3 branches are given off in the infratemporal fossa.
💡 Three siblings, only the youngest has a job: V3 chews; V1 and V2 just feel. See “muscles of mastication” → V3 → foramen ovale → first arch.
Dr. Maini — L10 Infratemporal Fossa · MC bank Q21–Q23, Q30, Q32
Anterior 2/3 of the tongue (V + VII)
👅 FRONT TONGUE: FEEL = V3 · TASTE = VII
For the anterior two-thirds of the tongue: general sensation (touch/pain) = lingual nerve (branch of V3); taste = chorda tympani (from CN VII), which hitchhikes on the lingual nerve to get there.
💡 Two nerves share the front of the tongue: the lingual (V3) feels it, and the chorda tympani (VII) tastes it — riding piggyback on the lingual. (Back 1/3 is all CN IX — see Topic 6.)
Dr. Maini — L06 Pharyngeal Apparatus · MC bank Q27, Q28, Q100
V1 & the corneal reflex + neuralgia
⚡ V1 FEELS the blink · V2/V3 = tic douloureux
Corneal (blink) reflex: the sensory limb is V1, the motor limb is CN VII. Trigeminal neuralgia (tic douloureux) = sudden, severe, shock-like pain in the V2 or V3 dermatome.
💡 V1 senses, VII blinks. And the classic “electric-shock face pain” lives in the lower two divisions (V2/V3), not the ophthalmic.
Reflex anatomy w/ V1 · L16 clinical · MC bank Q26, Q29

Topic 5 — Face & Ear: VII Facial & VIII Vestibulocochlear

The two that carpool into the internal acoustic meatus. VII drives on out to the face; VIII stays for hearing and balance.
Facial (VII) — the route
🛣️ IAM → facial canal (genu) → STYLOMASTOID → parotid
The facial nerve (VII) enters the internal acoustic meatus with VIII, bends at the genu (geniculate ganglion) inside the facial canal, exits at the stylomastoid foramen, then passes through the parotid gland, where it splits into its five terminal branches (incl. the marginal mandibular).
💡 Trace the road: in the meatus → around the bend → out the stylomastoid → through the parotid → fan out. Different door in, different door out.
Dr. Maini — L08 Face & Scalp · MC bank Q33–Q37, Q42
Facial (VII) — the jobs
😀 FACE · front-tongue TASTE · TEARS + SPIT
VII is motor to the muscles of facial expression (e.g. orbicularis oculi) plus stylohyoid, posterior belly of digastric, and stapedius. It carries taste from the anterior 2/3 of the tongue (cell bodies in the geniculate ganglion) and parasympathetic secretomotor fibers (via chorda tympani) to the submandibular and sublingual glands (and lacrimal).
💡 VII does the smile, the front-of-tongue taste, and the tears + spit glands. (Posterior 1/3 taste belongs to IX — don’t hand it to VII.)
Dr. Maini — L08 Face & Scalp · MC bank Q37–Q43
Facial (VII) — Bell’s palsy
😐 IDIOPATHIC WHOLE-SIDE DROOP + eye rolls up
Bell’s palsy = idiopathic paralysis of one whole side of the face. Bell’s phenomenon = when the patient tries to close the weak eye, the eyeball rolls upward (visible because the lid won’t shut). Herpes zoster of the geniculate ganglion + VIII = Ramsay Hunt (ear vesicles + facial palsy + hearing symptoms).
💡 A peripheral VII lesion sags the whole half of the face — forehead included. If the eye won’t close and rolls up, that’s Bell’s phenomenon giving the diagnosis away.
Dr. Maini — L08 Face & Scalp · MC bank Q44–Q46
Vestibulocochlear (VIII)
👂 HEAR + BALANCE · never leaves the skull
CN VIII is purely sensory, with two parts: cochlear (hearing) and vestibular (balance). Unlike VII, it does not exit the skull — it ends in the inner ear within the petrous temporal bone.
💡 VIII carpools with VII into the meatus, then splits into a “hearing” wire (cochlear) and a “balance” wire (vestibular) — and never comes back out.
Dr. Maini — L22 The Ear · MC bank Q47, Q48, Q51, Q52, Q54
Vestibulocochlear (VIII) — lesion
🔔 COCHLEAR → RING/DEAF · VESTIBULAR → SPIN
Cochlear-fiber lesion → tinnitus (ringing) and/or deafness. Vestibular-fiber lesion → vertigo and disturbed balance.
💡 Split the symptom by the wire: the hearing wire fails → ringing/deaf; the balance wire fails → the room spins.
Dr. Maini — L22 The Ear · MC bank Q49, Q50

Topic 6 — Jugular trio: IX Glossopharyngeal, X Vagus, XI Accessory

Three nerves out one door (jugular foramen). Two clean “all-except” rules, the wandering vagus, and the shrug-and-turn nerve.
Glossopharyngeal (IX) — function
👅 IX = 1 MUSCLE + PAROTID + BACK-tongue
Motor to stylopharyngeus ONLY; parasympathetic to the parotid gland (relayed through the otic ganglion); general sensation AND taste from the posterior 1/3 of the tongue; plus the carotid sinus & body and the sensory (afferent) limb of the gag reflex. IX is the nerve of the 3rd pharyngeal arch.
💡 IX runs a single muscle (stylopharyngeus), waters the parotid (otic ganglion), and tastes/feels the back third of the tongue. Touch the back of the throat → IX senses the gag.
Dr. Maini — L06 / L10 / L28 · MC bank Q56–Q63
Vagus (X) — the wanderer
🚶 X WANDERS: throat → heart → gut
The vagus (X) exits the jugular foramen, rides the carotid sheath, and supplies parasympathetics to the thorax and gut down to the left colic (splenic) flexure. It is the motor (efferent) limb of the gag reflex and runs all pharyngeal muscles EXCEPT stylopharyngeus, and the one tongue muscle palatoglossus.
💡 The vagus is the vagabond — it wanders farther than any other cranial nerve, throat to gut. IX feels the gag; X does the gag.
Dr. Maini — L05 / L28 · MC bank Q65, Q66, Q73–Q75
Vagus (X) — the laryngeal nerves
🗣️ ABOVE cords = internal · BELOW = recurrent
Recurrent laryngeal nerve = motor to ALL intrinsic laryngeal muscles EXCEPT cricothyroid (that one is the external laryngeal nerve), and it runs in the tracheo-esophageal groove. Sensory divide at the vocal folds: above = internal laryngeal nerve; below = recurrent laryngeal nerve.
💡 Two rules in one: for movement, recurrent does everything but cricothyroid; for sensation, draw a line at the vocal folds — internal above, recurrent below.
Dr. Maini — L26 The Larynx · MC bank Q67, Q68, Q71, Q72, Q76
Vagus (X) — recurrent laryngeal lesions
🪜 ONE RLN = HOARSE · BOTH = APHONIA + STRIDOR
One recurrent laryngeal nerve injured (thyroid surgery, or a posteriorly enlarging thyroid) → hoarseness (dysphonia). Both recurrent laryngeals → aphonia + inspiratory stridor.
💡 Climb the ladder by how much is cut: one side → a hoarse voice; both sides → no voice plus a whistle on breathing in. The RLN is the surgeon’s tripwire in thyroid surgery.
Dr. Maini — L05 / L26 · MC bank Q69, Q70
Accessory (XI) — the two-hole nerve
⬆️ SPINAL ROOT climbs UP the foramen magnum
The spinal root of CN XI arises from the upper cervical cord, ascends THROUGH the foramen magnum, joins the cranial root, then exits the jugular foramen (with IX and X). The cranial root joins the vagus; the spinal root goes to sternocleidomastoid + trapezius, joined by C2–C4 fibers.
💡 XI is the nerve at two holes: it goes up through the foramen magnum, then out the jugular foramen. The cranial part defects to the vagus; the spinal part drives the shrug muscles.
Dr. Maini — L03 / L28 · MC bank Q77–Q80, Q85
Accessory (XI) — test & lesion
🤷 SHRUG + TURN the head (SCM & trapezius)
Test CN XI by shrugging the shoulders (trapezius) and turning the head against resistance (SCM). A lesion → weak shrug, drooping shoulder, scapular winging, and difficulty turning the head. Note: one SCM turns the head to the opposite side. XI runs exposed in the posterior triangle.
💡 Can’t shrug, can’t turn the head → accessory. It runs superficially across the posterior triangle, so it’s easily nicked in neck surgery.
Dr. Maini — L03 / L05 · MC bank Q81–Q84

Topic 7 — Tongue: XII Hypoglossal

The motor nerve of the tongue. Its own canal, one “except,” some C1 hitchhikers, and a lesion that points at the problem.
Hypoglossal (XII) — function
👅 ALL “-GLOSSUS” MUSCLES — except PALATOglossus
CN XII is purely motor to all intrinsic and extrinsic tongue muscles EXCEPT palatoglossus (which is vagus, X). Genioglossus is the muscle that protrudes the tongue.
💡 Every “-glossus” muscle is the tongue nerve’s… except palatoglossus, the one the vagus stole. (My study aid, not Maini’s.) It’s the mirror of the vagus / stylopharyngeus rule — learn the pair.
Dr. Maini — L06 Pharyngeal Apparatus · MC bank Q87, Q89, Q91, Q92
Hypoglossal (XII) — C1 hitchhikers
🚗 C1 rides XII → ansa + thyrohyoid + geniohyoid
C1 fibers hitchhike along the hypoglossal nerve, then leave it to form the superior root of the ansa cervicalis (to infrahyoid muscles) and to supply thyrohyoid and geniohyoid. The nerve itself exits via the hypoglossal canal in the occipital bone.
💡 XII gives some passengers (C1) a lift, then drops them off to run the strap muscles — so “geniohyoid / thyrohyoid” are C1 fibers, not true XII fibers.
Dr. Maini — L03 Cervical Triangles · MC bank Q90, Q93
Hypoglossal (XII) — lesion
👅 TONGUE POINTS TO THE LESION
A unilateral CN XII lesion makes the protruded tongue deviate TOWARD the damaged side (the intact genioglossus of the normal side pushes it across), often with wasting on the affected side.
💡 The tongue points at the problem — deviates toward the lesion, not away. (Standard memory device — not attributed to Maini.)
Dr. Maini — CN XII clinical · MC bank Q88, Q94

Topic 8 — Muscle “all-except” rules & big-picture maps

The hardest lists collapse into a few “everything-EXCEPT” rules and four whole-set groupings. Learn the exception, not the roster.
The three muscle-group “except” rules
✌️ Pharynx→X / Palate→X / Tongue→XII (each has ONE exception)
Pharyngeal muscles = vagus (X), EXCEPT stylopharyngeus (IX). Palatal muscles = vagus (X), EXCEPT tensor veli palatini (V3). Tongue muscles = hypoglossal (XII), EXCEPT palatoglossus (X). Three lists, three exceptions.
💡 Vagus is the “default throat nerve” for pharynx AND palate — each list loses just one muscle (stylopharyngeus to IX; tensor veli palatini to V3). And the tongue is all XII except the one palatoglossus that vagus took.
Dr. Maini — L29 Labs (palate all X except tensor veli palatini → V3; tongue all XII except palatoglossus) · MC bank Q56, Q73, Q87
Parasympathetic outflow & its ganglia
🌿 PSNS = 3, 7, 9, 10 · ganglia: ciliary(III) · otic(IX)
Only four cranial nerves carry parasympathetic fibers: III, VII, IX, X. Ganglia Maini pins to nerves: ciliary ganglion = CN III; otic ganglion = CN IX (to the parotid). (VII relays through pterygopalatine + submandibular ganglia.)
💡 The parasympathetic cranial club is 3-7-9-10. Match ganglion to nerve: ciliary→III, otic→IX — both confirmed word-for-word in Maini’s lecture.
Dr. Maini — L10/L11 ganglia · MC bank Q97, Q98, Q99
Purely sensory vs purely motor
👁️ SENSORY: I · II · VIII  |  MOTOR: IV · VI · XI · XII
Purely sensory: CN I (olfactory), II (optic), VIII (vestibulocochlear). Purely (somatic) motor: CN IV, VI, XI, XII. (III is left out of “pure motor” because it also carries parasympathetic fibers.) There are 12 pairs of cranial nerves total.
💡 Two tiny sets to memorize outright: the 3 pure-sensory (1, 2, 8) and the 4 pure-motor (4, 6, 11, 12). Everything else is mixed.
Dr. Maini — CN overview / fiber types · MC bank Q95, Q102, Q104
Cavernous sinus residents
🏛️ IN THE WALL: III · IV · V1 · V2 · VI (VI floats inside)
Running in or along the wall of the cavernous sinus: CN III, IV, V1, V2 (in the lateral wall) and VI (running free through the sinus beside the internal carotid). All then head for the orbit / face.
💡 The cavernous sinus is a nerve hallway next to the carotid: III, IV, V1, V2 line the wall while VI walks down the middle — which is why VI is vulnerable here.
