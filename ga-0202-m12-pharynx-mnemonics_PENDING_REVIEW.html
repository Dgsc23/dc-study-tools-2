These six are the analogies and word-hooks Dr. Maini actually says in Lecture 27. Quoted verbatim — learn these first.
Dr. Maini’s own words
The whole pharynx in one picture
🏢 3 ROOMS, 1 CORRIDOR
The pharynx is the common chamber behind the nasal cavity, oral cavity, and larynx. It has no anterior wall — it opens forward into all three, which is why it is C-shaped, not a full tube.
“If you have three rooms and one common corridor for those three rooms — it’s like that.” — Dr. Maini, L27

Stylopharyngeus innervation
💅 SO STYLISH IT HAS ITS OWN NERVE
Every pharyngeal muscle is supplied by the vagus (CN X) EXCEPT stylopharyngeus, which is supplied by the glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX). It is the lone exception.
“How I remember this muscle was: stylopharyngeus muscle is so stylish, it has its own nerve.” — Dr. Maini, L27

Glossopharyngeal (CN IX) pattern
3️⃣ SO MANY 3s ABOUT THE 9th
Stylopharyngeus is a 3rd-arch muscle, so it takes the 9th nerve (CN IX) — the same nerve that serves the posterior one-third of the tongue. Dr. Maini points out how the number 3 keeps orbiting the 9th nerve.
“9th nerve, 3rd arch… so many 3s about 9th now, posterior one-third.” — Dr. Maini, L27

Naming “salpingo-” structures
🧘 SALPINX = A TUBE (like the fallopian tube)
The salpingopharyngeal fold and the salpingopharyngeus muscle are named for the auditory TUBE they descend from. Salpingopharyngeus originates from the cartilage of the auditory tube.
“Salpinx word means a tube… it’s a fancy term for a tube.” (tying it to the fallopian tube / salpingitis) — Dr. Maini, L27

Cricopharyngeus
🎗 CRICOPHARYNGEUS = A SLING (like puborectalis)
Cricopharyngeus, the lower part of the inferior constrictor, slings around the pharyngoesophageal junction without a raphe and acts as the upper esophageal sphincter (UES) — closed except during swallowing.
“Do you remember puborectalis… which was like a sling around the anorectal junction. Cricopharyngeus muscle is like that.” — Dr. Maini, L27

Torus tubarius (word-hook)
⛰ TORUS = A RIDGE
The torus tubarius is the ridge (tubal elevation) raised in the lateral wall of the nasopharynx by the cartilage of the auditory tube. The salpingopharyngeal fold hangs off it; the pharyngeal recess sits behind it.
“Torus means a ridge… like an elevation.” — Dr. Maini, L27

Topic 1 — The pharynx as a whole

One funnel, three parts, base of skull down to C6.
The three parts, top to bottom
⬇️ NOSE, MOUTH, VOICE-BOX (Naso · Oro · Laryngo)
Three parts, superior to inferior: nasopharynx (behind the nose), oropharynx (behind the mouth), laryngopharynx (behind the larynx). Each opens forward into its own “room.”
💡 Read them the way air and food arrive: nose first, mouth next, voice-box last. The nasopharynx is air-only; the oro- and laryngopharynx also carry food.
bank: Overview & Extent · L27
Vertical extent
📏 SKULL to C6 (12 cm funnel)
The pharynx runs from the base of the skull down to the lower border of the cricoid cartilage — the C6 level. It is funnel-shaped, ~12 cm long, and becomes the esophagus below.
💡 A 12 cm funnel bolted to the skull base, ending at C6 where it necks down into the esophagus. Larynx and laryngopharynx share that same C6 floor.
bank: Overview & Extent · L27
Posterior (skeletal) support
🪜 BACK WALL WALKS DOWN C1 to C6
Going down the pharynx, the posterior wall rests on successively lower vertebrae: nasopharynx on C1 (anterior arch of atlas), oropharynx on C2 + upper C3, laryngopharynx on C3–C6.
💡 Descend the pharynx and you descend the neck one floor at a time: C1 at the top room, C2–C3 in the middle, C3–C6 at the bottom, ending at C6.
bank: Nasopharynx / Oropharynx / Laryngopharynx · L27
Why it’s C-shaped
🗡 C-SHAPED, NOT A TUBE
The pharynx has essentially no anterior wall — it is open in front into the nasal cavity, oral cavity, and larynx. That open front makes it a C, not a complete tube.
💡 Picture a C, not an O: the missing front of the C is where the three rooms connect. In the teaching view the posterior wall is cut away so you can look in from behind.
bank: Overview & Extent · L27

Topic 2 — Nasopharynx (the busy lateral wall)

Air-only, rigid, and one lateral wall built entirely around the auditory tube.
Boundaries & supports
🧱 ROOF = SPHENOID + OCCIPUT · FLOOR = SOFT PALATE
Entirely respiratory and rigid (bony support all around, so it can’t collapse). Roof: body of sphenoid + basilar occiput. Floor: sloping upper surface of the soft palate. Posterior wall: anterior arch of the atlas (C1). Runs from skull base to the level of the soft palate.
💡 A rigid air vent that can’t cave in: bone above (sphenoid + occiput), the soft palate as a sloping floor, and C1 at its back.
bank: Nasopharynx · L27
Lateral-wall features (the hard list)
👂 FOLLOW THE TUBE: opening · torus · fold · recess
The lateral wall is one story built around the auditory tube: (1) the pharyngeal OPENING of the auditory tube, (2) the TORUS tubarius (ridge its cartilage raises), (3) the salpingopharyngeal FOLD hanging off the torus, (4) the pharyngeal RECESS behind the torus. Tubal tonsils ring the opening.
💡 Walk the tube into the wall: it pokes a hole (opening), bulges a ridge (torus), trails a curtain (fold), and leaves a pocket behind it (recess). Four features, one cause.
bank: Nasopharynx · L27
Maini’s EXCEPT trap
⚠️ TRAP: PHARYNGEAL TUBERCLE is NOT lateral-wall
Maini’s exam-style trap: the four lateral-wall features are the auditory-tube opening, torus tubarius, salpingopharyngeal fold, and pharyngeal recess. The pharyngeal TUBERCLE is a roof / skull-base landmark (the superior constrictor’s insertion) — not a lateral-wall feature.
💡 Tubarius (torus) is on the wall; tubercle is on the skull base. Same first syllable, opposite location — that’s the whole trick of the question.
bank: Nasopharynx (EXCEPT Q) · L27
Pharyngeal tonsil / adenoids
👃 ADENOIDS = INFLAMED PHARYNGEAL TONSIL
A single pharyngeal tonsil sits in the roof of the nasopharynx. Enlarged / inflamed = adenoids. In a child it blocks the posterior nasal apertures, causing mouth-breathing and adenoid facies (recessed jaw, flat face). Early adenoidectomy can normalize the face because facial bones grow until puberty.
💡 A swollen tonsil parked in the roof plugs the back of the nose, so the child breathes through the mouth. Fix it early and the still-growing face catches up.

Topic 3 — Oropharynx

The digestive entrance: soft palate down to the epiglottis.
Extent & floor
🍽️ SOFT PALATE to EPIGLOTTIS (floor = back 1/3 of tongue)
The digestive entrance, from the soft palate to the upper border of the epiglottis. Floor = posterior one-third of the tongue + the tongue–epiglottis interval. Posterior support = C2 + upper C3 (+ the C2–C3 disc).
💡 The “mouth room”: roof is the underside of the soft palate, floor is the back third of the tongue, and it ends where the epiglottis begins.
bank: Oropharynx · L27
Tonsillar fossa
🏛️ TWO PILLARS, TONSIL BETWEEN
Two folds bound the tonsillar fossa: the palatoGLOSSAL fold (front, toward the tongue) and the palatoPHARYNGEAL fold (behind). The palatine tonsil sits in the fossa between them.
💡 Two curtains (arches) with the palatine tonsil framed between them. “Glossal” is the front pillar (nearest the tongue); “pharyngeal” is the back pillar.
bank: Oropharynx · L27
Glossoepiglottic folds & valleculae
🌿 3 FOLDS, 2 VALLECULAE
Three glossoepiglottic folds connect tongue to epiglottis — one median + two lateral. The paired pockets between them (right and left) are the valleculae.
💡 One fold in the middle, one on each side (3 total); the two gutters they leave are the valleculae — the little “valleys.”
bank: Oropharynx · L27

Topic 4 — Laryngopharynx & piriform fossa

The slit behind the larynx, and the fishbone trap with a nerve underneath.
Extent & communications
🎙️ EPIGLOTTIS to C6 (behind the larynx)
Lies behind the laryngeal inlet and the back of the larynx. Runs from the upper border of the epiglottis to the lower border of the cricoid (C6), communicating above with the oropharynx and below with the esophagus. Posterior wall on C3–C6; lateral wall shielded by the thyroid cartilage (Maini’s “armor”).
💡 A narrow slit tucked behind the voice-box, funneling food from the oropharynx down into the esophagus at C6.
bank: Laryngopharynx · L27
Piriform fossa
🐟 PIRIFORM = PEAR-SHAPED FISHBONE TRAP
The piriform fossa is a pear-shaped, shallow recess on each side of the laryngeal inlet. Sharp foreign bodies (a fishbone) lodge here — and the internal laryngeal nerve runs just under its mucosa.
💡 A shallow pear-shaped gutter beside the inlet where a fishbone snags. Right beneath the lining sits a nerve waiting to be nicked.
bank: Laryngopharynx · L27
Internal laryngeal nerve injury
🚫 NICK THE NERVE = LOSE THE GAG
The internal laryngeal nerve is sensory (GSA) to the larynx above the vocal folds (upper half). Injure it in the piriform fossa and you lose the gag reflex, leading to aspiration and aspiration pneumonitis.
💡 It’s the upper larynx’s “smoke detector.” Cut its sensation and nothing triggers the cough/gag, so food slips into the airway and inflames the lungs.
bank: Laryngopharynx / Innervation / Clinical · L27

Topic 5 — Pharyngeal wall layers

Four layers, inside to out, plus the space that lets it all glide.
The four layers, in order
🪵 INSIDE-OUT: MUCOSA · PB FASCIA · MUSCLE · BP FASCIA
From internal to external: (1) mucosa, (2) pharyngobasilar fascia (the tough submucous sheet), (3) the muscular layer (circular constrictors + longitudinal), (4) buccopharyngeal fascia (outer wrap). Hook: “My Pharynx Moves Boluses.”
💡 Like an onion: wet lining inside, a strong fibrous sheet, then muscle, then an outer coat. The pharyngobasilar sheet is so strong it blends with the periosteum of the skull base.
bank: Wall Layers · L27
Epithelium
🧻 STRATIFIED SQUAMOUS, NON-KERATINIZED
Lined by stratified squamous, non-keratinized epithelium. Multi-layered to survive abrasion from the food bolus; non-keratinized because keratin lives only in skin (it toughens skin, and wet mucosae don’t need it).
💡 Many layers = a punching bag for the passing bolus. No keratin because that’s a skin-only feature — mucous membranes stay soft and wet.
bank: Wall Layers · L27
Retropharyngeal space
💛 RETROPHARYNGEAL SPACE = THE GLIDE ZONE
Between the pharynx and the vertebral column is the retropharyngeal space, filled with loose areolar connective tissue. It lets the pharynx glide up and down during swallowing. Obliterate it (fibrosis, tumor, abscess scarring) and the pharynx fixes to the spine, causing dysphagia.
💡 A slip-layer of loose packing so the pharynx can slide during a swallow. Glue that layer shut and swallowing seizes up.
bank: Wall Layers / Clinical · L27

Topic 6 — Constrictor muscles

Three stacked circular cups, all meeting at the midline seam.
The three constrictors + the raphe
🥂 3 STACKED CUPS to a MIDLINE SEAM
Superior, middle, and inferior constrictors form the outer CIRCULAR layer and overlap like stacked cups. Their fibers run transversely and meet posteriorly at the midline pharyngeal raphe (except cricopharyngeus). They contract in sequence to drive the bolus down.
💡 Three cups nested one inside the next, all zipped up the back at a single seam (the raphe). Squeeze them top-to-bottom and the bolus is milked downward.
bank: Constrictor Muscles · L27
Superior & middle origins
🅗 SUPERIOR = hamulus arc · MIDDLE = hyoid
Superior constrictor origin traces a front arc: pterygoid hamulus, pterygomandibular raphe, mylohyoid line, side of tongue; it inserts on the pharyngeal tubercle. Buccinator shares the pterygomandibular raphe (buccinator in front, superior constrictor behind). Middle constrictor arises from the stylohyoid ligament + greater & lesser horns of the hyoid.
💡 The superior’s origin traces the same front boundary as the cheek muscle (buccinator) — they hand off at the pterygomandibular raphe. The middle grabs the hyoid.
bank: Constrictor Muscles · L27
Inferior constrictor = 2 parts
🔀 THYROPHARYNGEUS (raphe) + CRICOPHARYNGEUS (UES)
Inferior constrictor arises from the oblique line of the thyroid cartilage + the cricoid, and has two parts: thyropharyngeus (upper — joins the raphe) and cricopharyngeus (lower — slings the pharyngoesophageal junction with NO raphe, = upper esophageal sphincter).
💡 Upper half behaves (forms the seam); lower half is the sphincter loop with no seam. That loop is the UES that stays shut except when you swallow.
bank: Constrictor Muscles · L27
Oblique-line trio (Maini’s trap)
⚠️ OBLIQUE LINE = the “THYROID” three (not sternohyoid)
Three muscles attach to the oblique line of the thyroid cartilage: inferior constricTOR, sternoTHYROID, and THYROhyoid. Maini’s trap: sternoHYOID does NOT (it runs to the manubrium and hyoid, skipping the thyroid).
💡 If the muscle’s job touches the thyroid, it’s on the oblique line — inferior constrictor, sternoTHYROID, THYROhyoid. SternoHYOID never mentions the thyroid, so it’s the odd one out.
bank: Constrictor Muscles · L27

Topic 7 — Longitudinal muscles, innervation & tonsils

The three inner “-pharyngeus” lifters, one nerve rule, and the ring of guard-tonsils.
The three longitudinal muscles
🛗 THREE “-PHARYNGEUS” LIFTERS: Palato · Salpingo · Stylo
The inner LONGITUDINAL layer = palatopharyngeus, salpingopharyngeus, stylopharyngeus. They elevate / shorten the pharynx & larynx during swallowing and all insert onto the thyroid cartilage. Origins: palato = hard palate + palatine aponeurosis (forms the palatopharyngeal arch); salpingo = auditory-tube cartilage; stylo = styloid process.
💡 Three elevators sharing one landing pad (the thyroid cartilage). Each is named for where it starts: the palate, the tube (salpinx), and the styloid.
bank: Longitudinal Muscles · L27
Innervation rule
🔌 ALL VAGUS (X) EXCEPT STYLOPHARYNGEUS (IX)
Most pharyngeal muscles come from the 4th & 6th arches, so the vagus (CN X) supplies them via the pharyngeal plexus. The one exception: stylopharyngeus (a 3rd-arch muscle) is supplied by the glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX). (See Maini’s “so stylish” hook up top.)
💡 One nerve rules the pharynx (vagus) with a single rebel — the stylish 3rd-arch muscle that took the 9th nerve.
bank: Innervation · L27
The four guard-tonsils
💌 4 GUARDS: PHARYNGEAL · TUBAL · PALATINE · LINGUAL
A lymphoid ring of four named tonsils guards the pharyngeal entrances: pharyngeal (roof of nasopharynx), tubal (around the auditory-tube opening), palatine (tonsillar fossa), lingual (root of tongue). The pharyngeal and lingual tonsils are single / unpaired.
💡 Four checkpoints ringing the doorways — roof, tube, throat-sides, and tongue-base. Two of them (pharyngeal, lingual) are lone single guards.
