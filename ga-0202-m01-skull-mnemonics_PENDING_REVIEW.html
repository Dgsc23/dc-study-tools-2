Topic 1 — Dr. Maini’s OWN memory hooks

Straight from the lecture, in her words. Lead with these — they are the ones she actually handed the class.
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
Sphenoid
🦇 SPHENOID = A BAT
The sphenoid is a deep cranial bone with a lesser wing, a greater wing, and a downward pterygoid process. Those “wings” are why Maini calls it bat-shaped — and it carries foramen rotundum, ovale & spinosum plus the sella turcica.
“Sphenoid is a bat-shaped bone, looks like a bat.”Maini, L01
💡 Picture a bat with spread wings hanging in the middle of the skull. Once it is a bat, the two wings (greater + lesser) and its holes come for free.
Maini L01 · bank: Sphenoid parts / bat-shaped
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
Ovale + spinosum
❗ OVALE + SPINOSUM = AN EXCLAMATION MARK
Foramen ovale (large oval) and foramen spinosum (tiny dot) sit right next to each other. Both are part of the sphenoid, and both ARE visible in the inferior view.
“Ovale and spinosum, they stay together… if you write an exclamation mark, it goes like this.”Maini, L02
💡 The big oval is the tall stroke, the little spinosum is the dot underneath — together they draw a “!”. Spinosum is even named for the neighbouring spine of the sphenoid.
Maini L02 · bank: Sphenoid foramina; foramen spinosum naming
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
Middle cranial fossa openings
🌈 DRAW THE ARCH: SOF → ROTUNDUM → OVALE → SPINOSUM
Four openings sweep front-to-back in an arch: Superior Orbital Fissure, foramen Rotundum, foramen Ovale, foramen Spinosum — all part of the sphenoid. Trap: rotundum is NOT seen in the inferior view.
“All these four foramina, they make an arch… it’s going to take you four seconds, but you’re going to remember this forever.”Maini, L01 / L02
💡 Do exactly what she says: sketch the little curved arch and label the four in order. It is her single highest-yield study move for this module.
Maini L01/L02 · bank: SOF-rotundum-ovale-spinosum arch
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
Crista galli
🐓 CRISTA GALLI = A ROOSTER’S COMB
Crista galli is the midline ridge of the ethmoid bone, standing up in the centre of the cribriform plate.
“Galli is the name for rooster, and this is like a rooster’s comb, that red-coloured thing they have.”Maini, L01
💡 “Crista galli” literally means rooster’s crest. See the red comb standing up between the two perforated plates — that is the ethmoid’s ridge.
Maini L01 · bank: Crista galli (ethmoid)
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
Cribriform plate
🧂 CRIBRIFORM = A FILTER FULL OF HOLES
The cribriform plate of the ethmoid is peppered with holes that transmit fascicles of the olfactory nerve (CN I). It helps floor the anterior cranial fossa.
“Cribriform means something like a filter, with multiple perforations.”Maini, L01
💡 A sieve / salt-shaker lets tiny things sift through — here the tiny things are the smell-nerve strands rising from the nose into the brain.
Maini L01 · bank: Cribriform plate contents (CN I)
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
Emissary veins
🛂 EMISSARY VEIN = A DIPLOMATIC EMISSARY
Parietal emissary foramina carry emissary veins, which connect the extracranial veins with the intracranial veins.
“Emissary means something coming from another place… like an embassy official, an emissary to the USA.”Maini, L01
💡 An emissary is a messenger who links two countries. An emissary vein links the two “countries” of the head — inside the skull and outside it.
Maini L01 · bank: Emissary veins (parietal foramina)
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
Mental foramen
🤔 MENTAL FORAMEN = HAND-ON-CHIN “THINKING”
The mental foramen of the mandible lets the mental nerve out onto the chin (sensory to the lower lip & chin). It is the far end of the inferior alveolar nerve.
“The mental nerve leaves the mandible through this foramen — mentally, when you think… you keep your hand on your chin.”Maini, L01
💡 Strike the thinker pose: hand resting on the chin. Your fingers land right over the mental foramen — “mental” as in thinking.
Maini L01 · bank: Mental foramen / mental nerve
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
Ethmoid
🕸️ “ETHMOID IS EVERYWHERE”
The ethmoid builds the roof of the nasal cavity (cribriform plate), a lateral wall, the upper nasal septum (perpendicular plate), and the superior + middle conchae. Trap: the lower septum is the vomer, and the inferior concha is its own bone.
“I told you — ethmoid is everywhere.”Maini, L01 / L02
💡 When a nasal-cavity part is asked and you are unsure, ethmoid is the safe first guess — except the two it does NOT own: lower septum (vomer) and inferior concha (separate bone).
Maini L01/L02 · bank: Ethmoid contributions
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
Chiasmatic sulcus
🏠 “WHO LIVES IN JOHN’S HOUSE? JOHN.”
The chiasmatic (prechiasmatic) sulcus lodges the optic chiasma — it is named for what it holds.
“What is located in the chiasmatic sulcus? The optic chiasma. Who lives in John’s house? John.”Maini, L01
💡 Her rule for the whole skull: many grooves and canals are named after their tenant — carotid sulcus holds the carotid, chiasmatic sulcus holds the chiasma. Read the name, name the content.
Maini L01 · bank: Chiasmatic sulcus (optic chiasma)
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
Superciliary arch
👁️ SUPER-CILIARY = ABOVE THE BROW HAIR
The superciliary arches are the ridges just above the orbital margins; the smooth glabella sits between them (both part of the frontal bone).
“Cilia means hair — which hair? Your brow.”Maini, L02
💡 Super (above) + cilia (eyelash/brow hair) = the ridge above your eyebrow. Same “cilia = hair” root shows up all over anatomy.
Maini L02 · bank: Frontal eminence / glabella
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
Names that describe the shape
🔤 PIRIFORM = PEAR · ROTUNDUM = ROUND · LACERUM = LACY
Piriform aperture = the pear-shaped anterior nasal aperture. Foramen rotundum = round. Foramen lacerum = jagged/“lacy” margins, and it is the odd one built from three bones (sphenoid, temporal, occipital).
“Piriform, because it’s pear-shaped… rotundum comes from the shape, that is round… lacerum, the name comes from the lacy, jagged margin.”Maini, L01
💡 Half the foramina tell you their own shape. When the name sounds like a shape (pear, round, lacy), trust it — Maini leans on this constantly.
Maini L01 · bank: Piriform aperture; foramen lacerum (3 bones)
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
Suture naming
🧩 A SUTURE IS NAMED FOR THE TWO BONES IT LIES BETWEEN
Fronto-nasal (frontal+nasal), coronal (frontal+parietal), sagittal (parietal+parietal), squamous (temporal+parietal), lambdoid (parietal+occipital), occipitomastoid (occipital+temporal). Sutures are the body’s most stable, least mobile joints.
“Sutures are named after the articulating bones — the bones they are located in between.”Maini, L01
💡 Don’t memorise a list — read the two bones and glue their names. The only oddball is the palate’s cruciform suture (a cross of three: intermaxillary + palatomaxillary + interpalatine).
Maini L01 · bank: Suture naming; cruciform suture
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
Carotid canal vs internal acoustic meatus
👀 SAME NEIGHBOUR, DIFFERENT VIEW
The opening beside the jugular foramen depends on the view: INFERIOR view → carotid canal (internal carotid artery); INTERNAL base view → internal acoustic meatus (to the inner ear). Students mix these up.
“In the inferior view, the carotid canal lies right next to the jugular foramen… please write this in bold — inferior view only.”Maini, L02
💡 Before you answer “what’s next to the jugular foramen,” check which view you’re in. Bottom of skull = carotid canal; inside the base = internal acoustic meatus.
Maini L02 · bank: Inferior vs internal base neighbour of jugular foramen
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
Scaphocephaly
🛶 SCAPHA = A BOAT
Scaphocephaly is a long, narrow, boat-shaped head from premature fusion of the sagittal suture.
“It’s a condition where the child has a boat-shaped head. Scapha means a boat.”Maini, L02
💡 Sagittal suture fuses too early, so the skull can’t widen — it grows long and keeled like an upturned canoe. Scapha = boat.
Maini L02 · bank: Scaphocephaly
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own
Quick spoken hooks
⚡ “PAT” · “NOT ONION” · “BLESSING”
Parietal — “where you pat your younger brother” (top/side of head). Inion = external occipital protuberance (“not onion”). Bregma = coronal+sagittal junction (“from blessing — you touch the forehead”).
“Two parietal bones, where you pat your younger brother… inion, not onion… bregma comes from blessing, you touch the forehead.”Maini, L01
💡 These are throw-away lines she said in class, but they stick: pat → parietal, not onion → inion, blessing on the forehead → bregma up top.
Maini L01 · bank: Cranial bones; inion; bregma

Topic 2 — Bones of the skull (study aids)

The two families and the exam-trap bones. Cards below are my devices, not Maini’s words — the facts are hers.
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Two families
🧠 NEURO = BRAIN-BOX · VISCERO = FACE
Maini splits the skull in two: the neurocranium (cranial bones that surround the brain) and the viscerocranium (facial bones that support the visceral organs — nasal & oral cavities).
💡 Neuro = neurons = the brain box. Viscero = viscera = the face around the airway and mouth. Every skull bone lands in one bucket.
Study aid · bank: Skull divisions (neuro- vs viscerocranium)
🧩 Study aid (mine)
The 8 cranial bones
🪜 “STEP OF” THE BRAIN BOX
Eight neurocranium bones, six names: Sphenoid, Temporal (×2), Ethmoid, Parietal (×2), Occipital, Frontal. Ethmoid & sphenoid sit deep “inside” but still count as cranial.
💡 Say “STEP OF the brain box.” Temporal and parietal are the paired ones (×2 each), which turns 6 names into 8 bones.
Study aid · bank: Cranial bone list
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Facial-bone traps
🎭 SEPTUM & CONCHA TRAPS
Upper nasal septum = ethmoid (perpendicular plate); lower septum = vomer. Superior + middle conchae = ethmoid; inferior concha = its own separate bone (Maini’s “none of the above” trap). Lacrimal = the tiny bone in the medial wall of the orbit.
💡 Vertical split of the septum: ethmoid on top, vomer on the bottom. Conchae: two are ethmoid, the bottom one stands alone.
Study aid · bank: Nasal septum; conchae; lacrimal
🧩 Study aid (mine)
The 4 sinus bones
🫁 FOUR PNEUMATIZED BONES SPELL A FACE
Only four bones hold paranasal air sinuses: Frontal (forehead), Ethmoid (between the eyes), Maxillary (cheeks), Sphenoid (deep centre). They lighten the skull and add resonance to the voice.
💡 Trace them top → middle → cheeks → deep across a face. Separate trap: mastoid air cells are a closed system — they do NOT communicate with the nasal cavity.
Study aid · bank: Paranasal sinus bones; mastoid air cells

Topic 3 — Sutures & the fragile points

Where sutures cross, bone is thin and fracture-prone. Learn the junctions and the danger under the pterion.
🧩 Study aid (mine)
The midline junction points
📍 NASION · BREGMA · LAMBDA (front to back)
Nasion = internasal + frontonasal sutures. Bregma = coronal + sagittal. Lambda = sagittal + lambdoid. Vertex = the highest point (just behind bregma).
💡 Walk the midline nose-to-back: Nasion → Bregma → Lambda. Bregma is where the crown (coronal) meets the arrow (sagittal); lambda looks like the letter λ.
Study aid · bank: Nasion; bregma; lambda; vertex
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Pterion — the danger spot
🩸 4 BONES OVER THE MIDDLE MENINGEAL ARTERY
Pterion is an H-shaped meeting of four bones: frontal, parietal, temporal, sphenoid (greater wing). The middle meningeal artery runs deep to it — a blow here can fracture it and cause an epidural (extradural) hemorrhage.
💡 “Four bones meet at the H; the middle meningeal hides beneath.” It is also the future site of the anterolateral fontanelle.
Study aid · bank: Pterion (bones + MMA); pterion fracture
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Asterion
⭐ ASTERION = 3 SUTURES MEET
Asterion is where the squamous, lambdoid, and occipitomastoid sutures meet, low on the back-side of the skull. It is the future site of the posterolateral (mastoid) fontanelle.
💡 Aster = star. Picture a little three-point star (three sutures) low behind the ear. Pterion has four bones up front; asterion has three sutures out back.
Study aid · bank: Asterion (3 sutures)
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Why the junctions matter
💥 MANY SUTURES = THIN = FRACTURE-PRONE
Maini stresses points like nasion, pterion, asterion, lambda because multiple sutures converge there, leaving the bone fragile. A hard blow tends to fracture at these convergences.
💡 Think of seams on a garment: where several seams cross, the fabric tears first. Same for the skull’s suture crossings.
Study aid · bank: Clinical importance of suture-junction points

Topic 4 — Foramina & what runs through them

The single hardest list. Each hook ties one opening to its one contents — grounded in the bank.
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Foramen magnum
🕳️ THE BIG HOLE = BRAINSTEM MEETS CORD
The foramen magnum transmits the junction of the medulla oblongata and the spinal cord — where the brainstem becomes the cord.
💡 Magnum = biggest, and it carries the biggest structure leaving the brain: the cord itself.
Study aid · bank: Foramen magnum contents
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Jugular foramen & carotid canal
🩸 THE TWO BIG BRAIN VESSELS, SIDE BY SIDE
Jugular foramen (between temporal + occipital bones) drains the internal jugular vein. The neighbouring carotid canal carries the internal carotid artery. Vein out, artery in.
💡 The brain’s main artery-in and main vein-out live next door to each other. In the inferior view, find the jugular foramen and the carotid canal is right beside it.
Study aid · bank: Jugular foramen; carotid canal contents
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Stylomastoid foramen
😀 THE FACE EXITS BETWEEN STYLOID & MASTOID
The facial nerve (CN VII) leaves the skull through the stylomastoid foramen, tucked between the styloid and mastoid processes.
💡 Face exits at stylo-mastoid. The name literally hands you its two landmarks and the nerve rhymes with the exit: the facial nerve.
Study aid · bank: Stylomastoid foramen (facial nerve)
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Cribriform & optic canal
👃 SMELL SIFTS · 👁️ SIGHT THREADS THE OPTIC CANAL
Cribriform plate (ethmoid) = fascicles of the olfactory nerve (CN I). Optic canal (sphenoid) = the optic nerve (CN II).
💡 Smell sifts through the ethmoid sieve; sight threads the optic canal. Senses 1 and 2 enter through the two front-most openings.
Study aid · bank: Cribriform (CN I); optic canal (CN II, sphenoid)
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Multi-bone foramina
🧱 LACERUM = 3 BONES (S-T-O) · JUGULAR = 2 (T-O)
Foramen lacerum is bordered by three bones: Sphenoid, Temporal, Occipital. The jugular foramen is bordered by two: Temporal + Occipital.
💡 Both share the “T-O” pair (temporal + occipital). Lacerum just adds the sphenoid up front. Remember: “STO for the lacy one.”
Study aid · bank: Foramen lacerum (3 bones); jugular foramen (2 bones)
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Palatine & supraorbital
🏷️ “OF THE SAME NAME” PACKAGES
Several openings pass an artery, vein, and nerve of the same name: supraorbital foramen (supraorbital n./a./v.) and the greater & lesser palatine foramina (palatine vessels & nerve). The mandibular foramen passes the inferior alveolar nerve, exiting the mental foramen as the mental nerve.
💡 When a foramen’s name matches a nerve name, expect the matching artery + vein too — one tidy neurovascular bundle.
Study aid · bank: Supraorbital; palatine foramina; mandibular/mental

Topic 5 — Cranial fossae & their floors

Three stepped floors, front to back. Know which bones build each floor and what sits inside.
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Anterior cranial fossa floor
🅵🅴🆂 = FRONTAL + ETHMOID + SPHENOID
Floor of the anterior fossa: orbital plate of the Frontal, cribriform plate of the Ethmoid, and lesser wing of the Sphenoid. Landmarks: crista galli, foramen cecum (a blind hole — nothing passes).
💡 Three bones, front-to-back: F-E-S. Frontal roofs the orbits, ethmoid sieves in the middle, sphenoid’s lesser wing caps the back edge.
Study aid · bank: Anterior cranial fossa floor; foramen cecum
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Middle cranial fossa
🦇 SPHENOID CENTRE + TEMPORAL WINGS
The middle fossa is floored by the greater wing + body of the sphenoid and the squamous + petrous temporal bone. Its posterior boundary = the crest of the petrous temporal. It holds the sphenoid’s foramina arch and the sella turcica.
💡 The bat (sphenoid) sits in the centre with the temporal bones as side walls, and the sharp petrous ridge is the back fence.
Study aid · bank: Middle cranial fossa floor / posterior boundary
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Sella turcica
🪑 TURKISH SADDLE FOR THE PITUITARY
The sella turcica (body of the sphenoid) seats the pituitary gland. Three parts front-to-back: tuberculum sellae, hypophyseal fossa (the pituitary), dorsum sellae. The sphenoid sinus sits just below — the route for a trans-sphenoidal pituitary approach.
💡 A saddle (sella = saddle) with the pituitary sitting in the seat. Because the sphenoid sinus is right underneath, surgeons can reach the gland through the nose.
Study aid · bank: Sella turcica parts; trans-sphenoidal approach
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Posterior cranial fossa
〽️ CLIVUS + VENOUS GROOVES (S IS FOR SIGMOID)
The posterior fossa is floored mainly by the occipital (its inner basilar slope = the clivus) and the petrous temporal. Venous-sinus grooves to know: Sigmoid (the S-shaped one), transverse, and superior + inferior petrosal.
💡 The S-shaped groove is the Sigmoid sinus (sigma = S). The clivus is the smooth ski-slope of the basilar occipital, running down toward the foramen magnum.
Study aid · bank: Clivus; sigmoid / petrosal sinus grooves

Topic 6 — Development, fontanelles & anomalies

How the skull is built, the soft spots and their future landmarks, and the named malformations.
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Ossification
🧱 FLAT = MEMBRANE · BASE = CARTILAGE
Flat bones (frontal, parietal — the roof and sides) form by intramembranous ossification. Irregular bones and the base of the skull form by endochondral (cartilaginous) ossification — the base is dense/“petrous” to protect the brain.
💡 “Flat From Membrane; Base is Cartilage.” Thin roof = a membrane that hardened; tough base = cartilage that hardened.
Study aid · bank: Membranous vs endochondral ossification
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Embryonic origins
🌱 CREST ON TOP · ARCHES MAKE THE FACE
Neurocranium = mainly neural crest; the base (chondrocranium) adds occipital somites; the viscerocranium (face) = the first two pharyngeal arches.
💡 Brain box from the neural crest, face from the first two arches. The base borrows a bit of somite to build its tough floor.
Study aid · bank: Neurocranium / viscerocranium origins
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Fontanelles → future landmarks
🔷 ANT = BREGMA · 🔺 POST = LAMBDA · SIDES = -TERIONS
Anterior fontanelle (diamond) → bregma. Posterior (triangle) → lambda. Anterolateral → pterion. Posterolateral (mastoid) → asterion.
💡 Midline soft spots become the midline points (bregma, lambda); the side soft spots become the “-terions” (pterion, asterion). Shape tags: diamond in front, triangle behind.
Study aid · bank: Fontanelles & their future sites
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Anterior fontanelle clinic
🩺 SUNKEN · BULGING · DELAYED
The anterior fontanelle is the one pediatricians palpate (largest, last to close — about 18 months). Sunken = dehydration; bulging = raised intracranial pressure (hydrocephalus/tumor); delayed closure = rickets (vitamin D / calcium deficiency).
💡 One soft spot, three readings: down = dry, up = pressure, late = rickets. It closes last, so it is the useful clinical window.
Study aid · bank: Fontanelle clinical signs / closure
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Craniosynostosis shapes
🛶 BOAT · 🗼 TOWER · ↔️ LOPSIDED
Scaphocephaly = sagittal fusion → long boat head. Oxycephaly / turricephaly = coronal fusion → tall tower head. Plagiocephaly = one-sided coronal/lambdoid fusion → asymmetric head. Separate: acrania = skull-cap bones fail to form; microcephaly = small head from abnormal CNS development.
💡 Name reads the shape: scapha = boat, turris = tower, plagio = oblique/slanted. Acrania and microcephaly are failures to form/grow, not premature fusion.
Study aid · bank: Scapho-/oxy-/plagiocephaly; acrania; microcephaly

Topic 7 — The mandible

The one movable skull bone. Its parts, its joint, its nerve tunnel, and the two glands the mylohyoid line divides.
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Mandible parts
🦴 BODY · RAMUS · ANGLE (= GONION)
Maini divides the mandible into body, ramus, and angle. The angle is also called gonion. Its arched, tooth-bearing edge is the alveolar process — and the maxilla + mandible are the only two skull bones with alveolar arches.
💡 Body (horizontal), ramus (vertical branch), and the angle where they turn — that corner is gonion.
Study aid · bank: Mandible divisions; alveolar arches; gonion
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Which process makes the joint?
🔗 CONDYLAR = TMJ · CORONOID = MUSCLE
The condylar process (head + neck) forms the temporomandibular joint, seating in the mandibular (articular) fossa of the temporal bone. Its neck has the pterygoid fovea for the lateral pterygoid muscle. The coronoid process is the muscle-only one.
💡 Condylar → joint; coronoid → muscle. The rounded head articulates; the flat blade is just a muscle anchor.
Study aid · bank: Condylar process (TMJ); pterygoid fovea
🧩 Study aid (mine)
The nerve tunnel
🚇 IN AT MANDIBULAR · OUT AT MENTAL
The inferior alveolar nerve enters the mandibular foramen (guarded by the tongue-shaped lingula — the dental-block landmark), runs through the bone, and exits the mental foramen as the mental nerve (lower lip & chin).
💡 One nerve, two doors: it goes in the mandibular foramen and out the mental foramen, changing its name on the way out. The lingula is where the dentist aims the needle.
Study aid · bank: Mandibular foramen; lingula; mental nerve
🧩 Study aid (mine)
Mylohyoid line divides two glands
👅 ABOVE = SUBLINGUAL · BELOW = SUBMANDIBULAR
On the inner mandible, the mylohyoid line anchors the mylohyoid muscle (floor of the mouth). Above the line = sublingual fossa/gland; below = submandibular fossa/gland. The mylohyoid groove carries the mylohyoid nerve.
💡 Sub-LINGUAL = under the tongue, so it sits higher (above the line); sub-MANDIBULAR hangs lower (below the line).
Study aid · bank: Mylohyoid line; sublingual / submandibular fossae
