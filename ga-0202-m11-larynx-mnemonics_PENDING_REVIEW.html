Topic 1 — The nine cartilages (the skeleton)

Three big singles, three tiny pairs. Learn the shapes and the traps.
How many cartilages
🎤 Dr. Maini’s own words
🔢 9 = 3 BIG SOLOS + 3 TINY PAIRS
The larynx has NINE cartilages: 3 large UNPAIRED (thyroid, cricoid, epiglottis) plus 3 small PAIRED (arytenoid, corniculate, cuneiform). 3 + 6 = 9.
🎤 “There are 3 pairs of cartilages, and 3 unpaired cartilages… so in total, there are 3 plus 6, 9 cartilages.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 25 (~49:52)
Bank Q1–Q3 · Maini L25 49:52
The 3 unpaired (large)
🧩 Illustrative study hook (mine)
🌞 T-C-E: SHIELD · RING · LEAF
The three large unpaired cartilages: Thyroid, Cricoid, Epiglottis. Picture their shapes top to bottom: thyroid = shield (the “Adam’s apple” front wall), cricoid = signet ring, epiglottis = leaf.
💡 “Three Cartilages Each stand alone” — the three you can name without a partner. All three are also the biggest ones.
Bank Q2, Q4, Q34 · Maini L25/L26
The 3 paired (small)
🧩 Illustrative study hook (mine)
🔢 A-C-C (biggest → tiniest)
The three small paired cartilages, in size order: Arytenoid (1st pair, the workhorse), Corniculate (2nd pair), Cuneiform (3rd pair). Just march the alphabet from A: A → C → C.
💡 “A Cop Calls” — Arytenoid does the real work; the two C’s are just little add-ons riding on it. Pairs × 3 = the 6 that make 9.
Bank Q3, Q24, Q29, Q30 · Maini L25 49:52
Cricoid cartilage
🎤 Dr. Maini’s own words
💍 SIGNET RING = ONLY COMPLETE RING
The cricoid is a hyaline signet ring: narrow anterior arch, broad posterior lamina. It is the ONLY laryngeal cartilage that forms a complete ring encircling the airway, and it marks the lowest limit of the larynx.
🎤 “It’s like your ring… a ring with a signet ring… with a large solitaire… This is the only cartilage which completely encircles the larynx, which makes a good question.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 25 (~1:02:47, 1:03:53)
Bank Q17–Q19, Q23 · Maini L25 1:03:53
Thyroid cartilage
🎤 Dr. Maini’s own words
👑 NECKLACE MISSING ITS BACK / ARMOR
The thyroid is the largest cartilage: two laminae meeting in front at the laryngeal prominence (Adam’s apple). It is posteriorly deficient — open behind — and sits behind everything else like a back-wall shield.
🎤 “It’s like that necklace which doesn’t have a posterior limb…” and later: “There is thyroid cartilage at the back of everything… like an armor.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 25 (~52:40) & Lecture 26 (~7:56)
Bank Q4, Q8, Q9 · Maini L25 52:40 / L26 7:56
Arytenoid cartilage
🎤 Dr. Maini’s own words
🗨️ 3-SIDED PYRAMID: VOCAL + MUSCULAR PROCESS
Each arytenoid is a three-sided pyramid with a base, an apex, and two processes: the VOCAL process (holds the Vocal ligament) and the MUSCULAR process (holds the Muscles). It sits on the superior border of the cricoid lamina.
🎤 “Like a four-sided pyramid… sorry, three-sided pyramid… with a base, an apex, and two processes. There is a vocal process, and there is a muscular process.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 25 (~1:06:33)
💡 My tag for the two processes: Vocal process = Vocal cord; Muscular process = Muscles. Same first letter both times.
Bank Q24–Q28 · Maini L25 1:06:33
Corniculate cartilage
🎤 Dr. Maini’s own words
➕ THE “OOPS, ADD A PIECE” EXTENSION
The corniculate cartilages are tiny yellow-elastic nodules sitting on the apex of each arytenoid, prolonging it backward and medially. They have no job of their own — they are just an extension.
🎤 “A retinoid cartilages… came out slightly shorter than expected… God added these tiny pieces on top… as an extension. That happens with us… we are making something, doesn’t come out that well, and then we add something on top.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 25 (~1:10:25)
Bank Q7, Q29, Q31 · Maini L25 1:10:25
Triticeal cartilage — THE TRAP
🎤 Dr. Maini’s own words
⚠️ TRITICEAL = NOT PART OF THE LARYNX
The triticeal (triticeous) cartilages sit in the free border of the thyrohyoid membrane — they are NOT laryngeal cartilages. Maini flags them as a pure distractor: if you see “triticeal” in a list of larynx cartilages, it is the wrong answer.
🎤 “They’re not part of larynx. They are just to confuse you. They are here to come in your life as a question, as a choice, which is going to confuse you.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 25 (~1:17:51)
Bank Q32 · Maini L25 1:17:51
Epiglottis
🎤 Dr. Maini’s own words
🍃 LEAF-SHAPED ELASTIC = “LID TO THE GLOTTIS”
The epiglottis is a leaf / spoon-shaped ELASTIC cartilage that folds back over the inlet in swallowing. Its name is the memory device: epi-glottis = the lid on top of the glottis. In humans it is somewhat degenerate, so the false cords back it up.
🎤 “It’s a leaf-shaped or spoon-shaped cartilage…” and “your epiglottis was named after glottis. Epiglottis is the lid to glottis.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 25 (~58:42) & Lecture 26 (~16:16)

Topic 2 — Ligaments & membranes

Extrinsic reaches OUT; intrinsic stays IN. Then the two membranes that carry the folds.
Extrinsic vs intrinsic
🎤 Dr. Maini’s own words
🏠 OUT-of-larynx = EXTRINSIC · WITHIN = INTRINSIC
An EXTRINSIC ligament/membrane ties the larynx to a non-laryngeal structure (hyoid, trachea). An INTRINSIC one connects the laryngeal cartilages to each other. Ask only: does the far end land outside the larynx?
🎤 “Extrinsic means they attach the laryngeal structures to something outside the larynx… Intrinsic is a structure that connects the two structures of the larynx.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 25 (~50:18)
Bank Q42, Q52 · Maini L25 50:18
The 3 EXTRINSIC connectors
🧩 Illustrative study hook (mine)
🤝 2 GO TO HYOID, 1 GOES TO TRACHEA
The three extrinsic connectors: Thyrohyoid membrane (thyroid–hyoid), Hyoepiglottic ligament (epiglottis–hyoid), Cricotracheal ligament (cricoid–trachea). Two anchor UP to the hyoid; one anchors DOWN to the trachea.
💡 “Two hang from the Hyoid hook, one drops to the Trachea.” Every one lands on a bone/tube outside the larynx — that is what makes them extrinsic.
Bank Q43, Q44 · Maini L25 1:18:17
Quadrangular membrane — its two edges
🧩 Illustrative study hook (mine)
⬆️ TOP = ARY-EPIGLOTTIC · ⬇️ BOTTOM = VESTIBULAR
The quadrangular membrane (a 4-sided sheet, epiglottis → arytenoid) has two named free borders: the SUPERIOR edge = aryepiglottic ligament/fold; the INFERIOR edge = vestibular ligament/fold (the false vocal cord).
💡 “Ary on top, Vestibular below” — A over V, alphabetical top-to-bottom. Both edges are just the thickened rims of the same quadrangular sheet.
Bank Q45–Q47, Q53 · Maini L26 5:11
Conus elasticus
🎤 Maini’s word + 🧩 my list hook
🔼 CONE of 3: MEDIAN CT + VOCAL CORDS + LATERAL CT
The conus elasticus is a cone built from three parts: the median cricothyroid ligament (front midline), the vocal ligaments (the top edge), and the paired lateral cricothyroid sheets. The lateral part fools you — its top attaches to the vocal cords, not straight to the thyroid.
🎤 “This looks conical… that is the structure, conus elasticus.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 26 (~15:38)
💡 My count-hook: Median + Vocal + Lateral = the 3 walls of the cone. Cricoid ring at the base, vocal cords at the rim.
Bank Q49–Q51 · Maini L26 13:45–15:38
Vocal ligament
🧩 Illustrative study hook (mine)
🎵 VOCAL LIGAMENT = THYRO-ARYTENOID LIGAMENT
The vocal ligament is the same thing as the thyroarytenoid ligament: it runs from the vocal process of the arytenoid forward to the thyroid. Drape mucosa over it and you get the vocal fold (true cord). Both it and the quadrangular membrane are intrinsic.
💡 “Ligament naked, fold when dressed” — a bare ligament becomes a “fold/cord” only once mucosa covers it. Same trick for the vestibular and aryepiglottic edges.

Topic 3 — Cavity, folds & the two rimas

Three floors from the top down, two folds, two gaps. Keep true vs false straight.
The 3 compartments (top → bottom)
🧩 Illustrative study hook (mine)
🏢 VESTIBULE · VENTRICLE · INFRAGLOTTIC
Top to bottom: VESTIBULE (inlet → vestibular folds), VENTRICLE (the narrow slot between vestibular & vocal folds), INFRAGLOTTIC cavity (vocal folds → lower border of cricoid).
💡 “Two V-floors, then the basement”: Vestibule and Ventricle up top, then drop below the cords into the infra-glottic basement. Each floor is bounded by a fold.
Bank Q61, Q62, Q64 · Maini L26 18:32–23:27
Vocal cord = a tunable string
🎤 Dr. Maini’s own words
🎸 VOCAL CORD = GUITAR STRING (arytenoid tunes it)
The vocal cord runs arytenoid → thyroid like a string. Moving the arytenoid changes the string’s tension, and that is how pitch/voice changes — exactly like re-tuning a guitar string.
🎤 “If you play guitar… to play it, you have to change tension in the strings… this a retinoid moves… and changes the tension in the cord, and that is how we speak.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 26 (~10:01)
Bank Q58 · Maini L26 10:01
True vs false folds
🎤 Dr. Maini’s own words
🎤 VOCAL = TRUE & MOBILE · VESTIBULAR = FALSE & FIXED
The vocal (true) folds have muscles that abduct/adduct them — they move. The vestibular (false) folds sit above them, have no muscle, and normally stay put, snapping shut only as an anti-choking reflex backup.
🎤 “Vestibular folds otherwise are called as false vocal cords… they do not have any muscle… Vocal cords are [mobile] because muscles act on them.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 26 (~6:53, 24:46)
Bank Q53–Q55, Q57, Q108 · Maini L26 6:53
The two rimas
🧩 Illustrative study hook (mine)
⚖️ VESTIBULI (false gap) · GLOTTIDIS (the REAL glottis)
Rima = the gap. Between the vestibular folds = rima vestibuli; between the vocal folds = rima glottidis, which Maini calls “your actual glottis.” The glottis = vocal folds + rima glottidis together.
💡 Match the fold to its gap: Vestibular fold → rima Vestibuli; vocal (Glottis) fold → rima Glottidis. Same word, upper vs lower.
Bank Q65, Q66, Q69 · Maini L26 25:42
Saccule
🧩 Illustrative study hook (mine)
💧 SACCULE = THE OIL POCKET FOR THE CORDS
The saccule is a mucosa-lined pocket extending anteriorly off the ventricle. Its mucous glands secrete mucus that lubricates the vocal cords so they don’t dry out.
💡 “Saccule = the oil can” tucked in front of the ventricle, greasing the strings so they can keep vibrating.
Bank Q63 · Maini L26 21:04
Laryngeal inlet boundaries
🧩 Illustrative study hook (mine)
🚪 FRONT LID · BACK PILLARS · SIDE FOLDS
The laryngeal inlet is bounded ANTERIORLY by the epiglottis (the front lid), POSTERIORLY by the arytenoid cartilages + mucosa (the back pillars), and LATERALLY by the aryepiglottic folds (the side rails).
💡 Walk the doorway: lid in front, two pillars in back, a fold down each side. The side rails are just the top edge of the quadrangular membrane.

Topic 4 — Intrinsic muscles by action

All act on the cords. Learn the ONE opener, the closers, the tensor, the relaxers.
The SOLE opener
🎤 Maini’s point + 🧩 my letter hook
🚪 PCA = “PLEASE COME APART” (only abductor)
The Posterior CricoArytenoid is the ONLY abductor — the only muscle that opens the rima glottidis so you can breathe. Lose both and the cords stay shut. Origin: posterior cricoid lamina; insertion: muscular process of the arytenoid.
🎤 “This is the only, the sole, abductor of vocal cords… very important for your patient to live… if it is paralyzed, vocal cords will stay approximated, asphyxiation.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 26 (~37:09, 43:51)
💡 My letter hook: Posterior Cricoarytenoid = “Please Come Apart” — the one that pulls the cords apart.
Bank Q74, Q75, Q102 · Maini L26 37:09
The closers (adductors)
🧩 Illustrative study hook (mine)
🔒 LATERAL CA + TRANSVERSE & OBLIQUE ARYTENOIDS
Three muscles ADDUCT (shut the rima glottidis for phonation): the Lateral cricoarytenoid plus the Transverse and Oblique arytenoids. Note the flip: LATERAL CA closes, POSTERIOR CA opens.
💡 “LAT + the two arytenoid buddies slam the door.” Remember the CA pair by position: Posterior Pulls apart, Lateral Locks together.
Bank Q76, Q77, Q82 · Maini L26 37:47–41:08
The tensor
🎤 Dr. Maini’s own words
🔒 CRICOTHYROID = THE CHIEF TENSOR (pitch)
The Cricothyroid is the chief/only tensor: it tilts the thyroid down & forward, lengthening and stretching the cords to raise pitch. Origin: arch of cricoid; insertion: inferior horn & lower border of thyroid. Paralyze it → a flat, one-tone voice.
🎤 “The patient will speak in monotonous… they cannot change the tone of the voice, and they will speak the way I’m speaking right now.”— Dr. Maini demonstrating it herself, Lecture 26 (~35:53)
Bank Q72, Q73, Q100 · Maini L26 35:53
The relaxers
🧩 Illustrative study hook (mine)
📺 “TV” = THYROARYTENOID + VOCALIS SLACKEN
The Thyroarytenoid (lateral) and Vocalis (medial) lie inside the vocal folds, parallel to the cords. They shorten and slacken the folds — the opposite of the cricothyroid tensor.
💡 “TV = relax on the couch”: Thyroarytenoid + Vocalis kick back and slacken the cords. They sit within the fold, so they can only loosen it.
Bank Q59, Q78, Q79, Q81 · Maini L26 38:44–41:08
One-line action map
🧩 Illustrative study hook (mine)
📝 OPEN·CLOSE·TENSE·SLACKEN
OPEN = Posterior CA (sole). CLOSE = Lateral CA + Transverse/Oblique arytenoids. TENSE = Cricothyroid. SLACKEN = Thyroarytenoid + Vocalis. Every intrinsic muscle falls in exactly one of these four buckets.
💡 Sort any name into four boxes before you panic. Only Posterior opens; the rest of the “arytenoid” crowd closes; cricothyroid is the lone tensor.

Topic 5 — Nerves & the SVE / GSE trick

All vagus. One muscle and one sensory zone break from the pack.
Motor supply
🧩 Illustrative study hook (mine)
🎙️ ALL VAGUS — CRICOTHYROID IS THE ODD ONE
Every intrinsic laryngeal muscle is vagus (CN X). Split: Cricothyroid = EXTERNAL laryngeal nerve (the exception); all the rest = RECURRENT laryngeal nerve.
💡 “CriCothyroid catches the eXternal; everyone else Recurs.” One odd muscle out — memorize the single exception and the rest fall to the recurrent.
Bank Q83–Q85, Q89 · Maini L26 42:16–43:16
Sensory supply
🧩 Illustrative study hook (mine)
⬆️ INTERNAL ABOVE CORDS · ⬇️ RECURRENT BELOW
Sensation splits at the vocal folds: INTERNAL laryngeal nerve = mucosa ABOVE the cords (vestibule + ventricle); RECURRENT laryngeal = mucosa BELOW the cords (infraglottic). The internal branch pierces the thyrohyoid membrane to get in.
💡 “Internal In from the top; Recurrent Reaches up from below.” The cords are the border; the recurrent covers both jobs it can reach from below (motor + lower sensory).
Bank Q87, Q90, Q92 · Maini L26 50:52–52:02
Maini’s exam trick: SVE vs GSE
🎤 Dr. Maini’s own words
🎯 PHARYNGEAL-ARCH MUSCLE → SVE (not GSE)
When a head/neck motor fiber question stumps you: if the muscle is a pharyngeal-arch muscle (larynx & pharynx, arches 4 & 6) the answer is SVE. Only myotomal muscles (tongue, extraocular) are GSE. So the laryngeal muscles — external & recurrent laryngeal — carry SVE.
🎤 “Don’t go for GSE… if that muscle belongs to pharyngeal arch muscles, SVE is the right answer. If it is myotomal muscles, like your tongue or extraocular muscles, it is GSE.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 26 (~48:04)
Bank Q88 · Maini L26 48:04
Superior laryngeal nerve splits
🧩 Illustrative study hook (mine)
✂️ INTERNAL = SENSORY (In) · EXTERNAL = MOTOR (Exit)
The superior laryngeal nerve (off the vagus) forks into: Internal laryngeal = purely sensory (GSA), pierces the thyrohyoid membrane; External laryngeal = motor (SVE) to the cricothyroid. The recurrent laryngeal’s tip is renamed the inferior laryngeal nerve.
💡 “Internal feels, External moves” — the internal branch senses, the external branch works the one tensor muscle.

Topic 6 — Arteries & clinical payoffs

Top half vs bottom half; and the four nerve lesions Maini keeps testing.
Blood supply
🎤 Maini’s S-L-F hook + 🧩 my top/bottom split
🩸 TOP: SUP. LARYNGEAL ← SUP. THYROID ← ECA
Upper half: superior laryngeal artery ← superior thyroid ← external carotid. Lower half: inferior laryngeal artery ← inferior thyroid ← thyrocervical trunk ← subclavian. The superior artery pierces the thyrohyoid membrane with the internal laryngeal nerve.
🎤 For the ECA branches: “S, L, and F, anterior branches… superior thyroid, and next was lingual, and next was facial.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 25 (~28:13)
💡 “Superior rides with Superior”: superior laryngeal + superior thyroid (S of S-L-F) up top from the carotid; inferior pair comes up from the subclavian side.

Lesion 1 — monotone voice
🎤 Dr. Maini’s own words
📜 MONOTONE = CRICOTHYROID / EXTERNAL LARYNGEAL
Paralyze the cricothyroid (its nerve = external laryngeal) and the patient can still speak but cannot change pitch — a flat, monotonous voice. At-risk in thyroidectomy because the nerve runs parallel to the superior thyroid artery.
🎤 “They cannot change the tone of the voice, and they will speak the way I’m speaking right now.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 26 (~35:53)
Bank Q100, Q103 · Maini L26 35:53
Lesion 2 — the deadly one
🎤 Dr. Maini’s own words
☠️ BILATERAL PCA PALSY = AIRWAY SHUTS = DEATH
The posterior cricoarytenoid is the only opener. Paralyze it on both sides and the cords stay adducted — the rima glottidis can’t open, and the patient asphyxiates. This is why the “sole abductor” fact matters clinically.
🎤 “Vocal cords, they will stay closed, glottis will not open up, and patient will die… you cannot breathe if both the vocal cords stay adducted.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 26 (~37:12)
Bank Q102 · Maini L26 37:12
Lesion 3 — silent aspiration
🧩 Illustrative study hook (mine)
🥤 INTERNAL LARYNGEAL CUT = NO GAG → ASPIRATION
The internal laryngeal nerve is the sensory limb above the cords. Cut it and you lose the gag/cough reflex up there — food can slip in unnoticed, raising aspiration risk. Sensory limb gone = reflex gone.
💡 “No feeling above the cords, no cough to protect them.” The motor side is intact — the failure is that nothing warns the airway.
Bank Q101 · Maini L26 52:12–52:38
Surgery: which artery threatens which nerve
🧩 Illustrative study hook (mine)
🪥 SUPERIOR→EXTERNAL · INFERIOR→RECURRENT
In thyroidectomy: the SUPERIOR thyroid artery runs with the EXTERNAL laryngeal nerve (ligate the artery near its origin to spare the nerve); the INFERIOR thyroid artery runs with the RECURRENT laryngeal nerve in the tracheoesophageal groove.
💡 “Superior with the little external, inferior with the big recurrent.” Match top artery to top-half nerve, bottom artery to the deep recurrent.
