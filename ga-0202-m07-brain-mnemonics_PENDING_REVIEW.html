Topic 1 — 🎙️ Dr. Maini’s OWN memory hooks (her words)

Every hook here is quoted straight from her Lecture 16 / 17 recordings.
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own words
Thalamus = the sensory relay
✈️ THALAMUS = LONDON HEATHROW LAYOVER
The thalamus is the relay center for almost every ascending sensation on its way to the cortex. A sensory signal changes neurons here, like changing planes at a hub. The one exception is smell (olfaction), which bypasses the thalamus.
“So your thalamus is your London Heathrow. This is where there is a halt, you leave one flight, and start next flight.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 16 (~50:11)
Grounds Everything-MC Q57–Q58 · L16 Thalamus
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own words
Midbrain cross-section
😠 MIDBRAIN = THE ANGRY MAN’S FACE
On a midbrain cross-section: the red nuclei = the eyes and the substantia nigra = the eyebrows (so substantia nigra sits on top of the red nucleus). Both are midbrain structures. The cerebral aqueduct runs through the middle, ringed by periaqueductal gray.
“Substantia nigra is like the eyebrows, red nucleus is like the eyes.” — and she pulls it back on her quiz: “Do you guys remember that man with the eyes and eyebrows?”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 16 (~1:12:44) / Lecture 17 quiz
Grounds Q67–Q68, Q75 · L16 Midbrain section
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own words
Cranial nerves IX, X, XI
🤝 IX-X-XI = THREE CLOSE FRIENDS
CN IX, X, XI travel together: they emerge from the post-olivary sulcus and leave the skull together through the jugular foramen. (She deliberately avoids “three musketeers” so they don’t blur with another trio.)
“These are 3 close friends, 9, 10, 11. They stay together, post-olivary sulcus. And then jugular foramen.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 17 (~31:19)
Grounds Q82 · L17 CN IX-X-XI
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own words
Trochlear nerve (CN IV)
🧣 CN IV DRAPES FORWARD LIKE A SCARF
CN IV (trochlear) is the only cranial nerve that emerges from the dorsal (posterior) aspect of the brainstem — just below the inferior colliculus — then wraps around to the front, the way a scarf drapes from behind to the front.
“It moves anteriorly, like if you put a scarf, you know? From posterior side to anterior.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 17 (~26:14)
Grounds Q78 · L17 CN IV
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own words
Dural venous drainage
🌀 SIGMOID SINUS = THE “SIGMOID COLON” OF THE SINUSES
Just as the sigmoid colon is the end of the colon, the sigmoid sinus is the last dural venous sinus — it becomes the internal jugular vein as it exits through the jugular foramen. (She offers it as her own “weird connection.”)
“Sigmoid colon is the end part of colon? So, sigmoid sinus is the end part of sinuses … whatever helps you remember.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 17 (~1:16:22)
Grounds Q119 · L17 Venous drainage
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own words
Cerebellum — the vermis
🐛 VERMIS = THE WORM BETWEEN THE HEMISPHERES
The vermis is the worm/caterpillar-shaped band that connects the two cerebellar hemispheres (“vermis” literally means worm). Its shallow midline is why the falx cerebelli is narrow, unlike the deep falx cerebri.
“Looks like a caterpillar, or a worm. This is called as vermis.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 16 (~1:29:03)
Grounds Q87 · L16 Vermis
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own words
Hypothalamus
🅆 W-SHAPED · THE BODY’S THERMOSTAT
On the sagittal view she teaches the hypothalamus as the “W-shaped structure” below the thalamus. Function: it’s the thermostat — plus thirst, hunger, endocrine control, sleep-wake cycle, and autonomic control.
“I tell my students the W-shaped structure … this is hypothalamus.” — and in L17: “hypothalamus is your thermostat.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 16 (~55:24) / Lecture 17 (~11:30)
Grounds Q60 · L16–17 Hypothalamus
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own words
Deep gray masses
🧩 “BASAL NUCLEI,” NOT BASAL GANGLIA
She insists on basal nuclei as the correct term. A ganglion is a group of cell bodies located OUTSIDE the CNS — and these masses are inside it — so “basal ganglia” is the clinician’s term but technically wrong. The three: caudate, putamen, globus pallidus.
“Nuclei is the best terminology, basal ganglia is not the right terminology.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 16 (~8:56)
Grounds Q10–Q12 · L16 Basal nuclei
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own words
Abducens nerve (CN VI)
👁️ ABDUCENS = THE NERVE THAT ABDUCTS THE EYE
The name tells you the job: CN VI abducts the eye by supplying the lateral rectus. It emerges from the middle of the pontomedullary sulcus.
“It innervates the muscle that helps you abduct your eye … so abducens.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 17 (~28:39)
Grounds Q81 · L17 CN VI
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own words
Reticular activating system
⏰ RETICULAR FORMATION = YOUR “STAY-AWAKE” NETWORK
The reticular formation / activating system is a network of gray & white matter in the medulla and pons. Sensations passing through it keep you alert — a light or a voice keeps you awake, and as the sensations die down, you fall asleep.
“This pathway activates you, keeps you alert … as the sensations die, we tend to sleep.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 17 (~38:04)
Grounds Q73 · L17 Reticular formation
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own words
Trap: the lingual gyrus
👅 LINGUAL GYRUS = SHAPE ONLY, NOT THE TONGUE
Named-for-shape trap she calls out directly: the lingual gyrus (medial/occipital surface, near the visual cortex) has nothing to do with the tongue — no tongue muscle, control, or sensation. It just looks tongue-shaped.
“Has got nothing to do with the tongue, it’s because of the shape. It’s called as lingual gyrus.”— Dr. Maini, Lecture 16 (~44:28)
Grounds Q52 · L16 Lingual gyrus
🎙️ Dr. Maini’s own words
Her Latin decoder
🔤 THE NAME TELLS YOU THE PART
Maini decodes the roots for you: pars = “part” (pars orbitalis / triangularis / opercularis), operculum = “a lid”, corpora quadrigemina = “four bodies” (2 superior + 2 inferior colliculi), arbor vitae = “branching tree” (cerebellar white matter), and folia = “leaves” (cerebellar folds).
“Pars means part.” · “Operculum means a lid.” · “Corpora means bodies. Quadrigamina means 4 bodies.” · “Folia means leaves.”— Dr. Maini, Lectures 16 & 17

Topic 3 — 💡 Cranial nerves, foramina & their contents

Illustrative aids for the module’s longest lists — my hooks, not Maini’s words. Facts are bank-verified.
💡 Illustrative aid — NOT Dr. Maini’s words
Where each cranial nerve exits
🚪 TOP-TO-BOTTOM EXIT MAP (I–XII)
I olfactory tract/bulb (no true nerve) · II optic → chiasma · III interpeduncular fossa · IV dorsal midbrain, below inferior colliculus · V side of the pons · VI middle of pontomedullary sulcus · VII & VIII through internal acoustic meatus · IX-X-XI post-olivary sulcus · XII pre-olivary sulcus.
💡 Olive rule to lock in: PRE-olive = 12 (one nerve, in front); POST-olive = 9-10-11 (Maini’s “three friends,” behind). Nothing between the olive and pyramid is XII’s neighbor by accident.
Grounds Q77–Q85 · L17 Cranial nerve attachments
💡 Illustrative aid — NOT Dr. Maini’s words
Foramina and what runs through them
🕳️ 3 HOLES, 3 GUEST LISTS
Jugular foramen → CN IX, X, XI (and the sigmoid sinus becoming the IJV). Internal acoustic meatus → CN VII, VIII + labyrinthine artery. Foramen magnum → medulla-to-spinal-cord + the two vertebral arteries entering.
💡 Hook for the ear canal: “Seven ate the labyrinth” — VII, VIII, and the labyrinthine artery (three tenants) share the internal acoustic meatus.
Grounds Q82, Q84, Q108, Q111 · L17
💡 Illustrative aid — NOT Dr. Maini’s words
4th-ventricle apertures
💧 LUSCHKA = LATERAL (2) · MAGENDIE = MEDIAN (1)
CSF leaves the 4th ventricle into the subarachnoid space through two lateral foramina of Luschka and one median foramen of Magendie.
💡 Both start with the same letter as their position: Luschka = Lateral (and there are two of them, one per side); Magendie = Median (just the one, in the midline).
Grounds Q97 · L17 CSF apertures
💡 Illustrative aid — NOT Dr. Maini’s words
Trigeminal (CN V) motor muscles
🦷 THIN ROOT = MOTOR · THICK ROOT = SENSORY
The small motor root of CN V supplies the muscles of mastication plus tensor tympani, tensor veli palatini, mylohyoid, and the anterior belly of digastric. The thick root is sensory (most of the face).
💡 Thin = motor, Thick = sensory (Maini’s own split). For the four “extras”: the two tensors (tympani + veli palatini) and the two floor-of-mouth muscles (mylohyoid + anterior digastric).

Topic 4 — 💡 Pathways & sequences

The flow-charts Maini makes you recite — illustrative ordering hooks, facts bank-verified.
💡 Illustrative aid — NOT Dr. Maini’s words
CSF circulation
🚿 CSF FLOWS DOWN, THEN OUT, THEN HOME
Lateral ventricles → interventricular foramina → 3rd ventricle → cerebral aqueduct → 4th ventricle → Luschka + Magendie → subarachnoid space → arachnoid granulations → superior sagittal sinus (back into blood).
💡 Three beats: DOWN the ventricles (1→3→4 via foramina/aqueduct), OUT to the subarachnoid space (Luschka/Magendie), HOME to the blood (arachnoid granulations → superior sagittal sinus). Block the granulations → hydrocephalus.
Grounds Q100–Q101 · L17 CSF pathway
💡 Illustrative aid — NOT Dr. Maini’s words
Sensory vs motor neuron counts
🔢 SENSORY = 4 STOPS · MOTOR = 2 STOPS
Sensory (4 neurons): ganglion → medulla → thalamus → postcentral gyrus. Motor (2 neurons): upper motor neuron in the precentral gyrus → lower (alpha) motor neuron in the anterior/ventral horn.
💡 Four sensory relay stops = “Ganglion, Medulla, Thalamus, Postcentral” (in from the periphery, up to the cortex). Motor is the short trip: brain → cord, just two.
Grounds Q29–Q30 · L16–17 Motor/sensory pathways
💡 Illustrative aid — NOT Dr. Maini’s words
Internal carotid artery, in order
🩸 4 PARTS: CERVICAL → PETROUS → CAVERNOUS → CEREBRAL
The internal carotid runs cervical → petrous → cavernous → cerebral. The cerebral part gives the ophthalmic artery, then ends as the anterior cerebral (medial surface) and middle cerebral (lateral surface) arteries.
💡 Bottom-to-top through the bone: neck (Cervical) → petrous canal (Petrous) → cavernous sinus (Cavernous) → into the brain (Cerebral). MCA is the top stroke artery.
Grounds Q104–Q107 · L17 ICA
💡 Illustrative aid — NOT Dr. Maini’s words
Vertebro-basilar build-up
🔗 2 → 1 → 2 (VERTEBRALS → BASILAR → POSTERIOR CEREBRALS)
The two vertebral arteries (branches of the subclavian) unite into the single basilar artery, which ends by splitting into the two posterior cerebral arteries. The occipital lobe’s blood ultimately traces back to the vertebrals.
💡 Count it 2 → 1 → 2: two vertebrals merge to one basilar, then fork back to two PCAs. It’s the only place two arteries join into one.
Grounds Q108–Q112 · L17 Vertebro-basilar

Topic 5 — 💡 Deep gray, capsules & lobes

The nested white-matter capsules and the lobe map — illustrative hooks, facts bank-verified.
💡 Illustrative aid — NOT Dr. Maini’s words
The three capsules, inside → out
🧅 INTERNAL → EXTERNAL → EXTREME
Moving outward: Internal capsule (between caudate/globus pallidus and thalamus — carries the corticospinal tract, a common stroke site) → External capsule (between putamen and claustrum) → Extreme capsule (between claustrum and insular gray). The claustrum is the thin gray strip between the outer two.
💡 The names literally march outward: Internal (deepest, motor tract), then External, then Extreme (nearest the insula). Claustrum is the “filling” sandwiched between External and Extreme.
Grounds Q44–Q48 · L16–17 Capsules
💡 Illustrative aid — NOT Dr. Maini’s words
Basal nuclei roster
🚗 CAUDATE + PUTAMEN + GLOBUS PALLIDUS (THALAMUS IS THE IMPOSTOR)
The three basal nuclei: caudate, putamen, globus pallidus. Putamen + globus pallidus = lentiform nucleus; add the caudate = corpus striatum. The thalamus is NOT a basal nucleus (it’s diencephalon) — a favorite Menti trap.
💡 Lens shape = Lentiform = Putamen + Globus pallidus. The whole striped set = corpus striatum. If “thalamus” is in the choices for a basal-nuclei question, it’s the wrong one.
Grounds Q12–Q13, Q43 · L16–17 Basal nuclei
💡 Illustrative aid — NOT Dr. Maini’s words
Lobe → primary function
🗺️ FRONT-MOVES, PARIETAL-FEELS, OCCIPUT-SEES, TEMPLE-HEARS, INSULA-TASTES
Frontal = motor (+ Broca’s speech, reasoning) · Parietal = sensory / somesthetic · Occipital = vision · Temporal = hearing + smell (+ Wernicke’s) · Insula (deep 5th lobe) = taste.
💡 One verb each: front moves, parietal feels, occiput sees, temple hears, insula tastes. Precentral gyrus = motor; postcentral gyrus = sensory (they hug the central sulcus).
Grounds Q20–Q24, Q27–Q28 · L16–17 Lobes
💡 Illustrative aid — NOT Dr. Maini’s words
Colliculi of the midbrain
👁️👂 SUPERIOR = SIGHT · INFERIOR = SOUND
The superior colliculi are part of the visual pathway; the inferior colliculi are part of the auditory pathway. Together (2 + 2) they are the corpora quadrigemina on the dorsal midbrain.
💡 Alliteration keeps it straight: Superior = Sight, and the lower pair handles what your ears feed in (Inferior = auditory). Eyes look up, ears sit lower.
Grounds Q64–Q65 · L17 Colliculi

Topic 6 — 💡 Diencephalon, limbic, tremor & development

The remaining roster lists — illustrative hooks, facts bank-verified.
💡 Illustrative aid — NOT Dr. Maini’s words
Diencephalon components
🏛️ 6 PARTS: THALAMUS & FRIENDS (NOT THE MIDBRAIN)
The diencephalon = thalamus, hypothalamus, optic chiasma, pineal gland, mammillary bodies, and infundibulum. Note the trap: the midbrain is NOT diencephalon (it’s brainstem).
💡 Anchor on the two thalami and hang the rest off them: hypothalamus below, optic chiasma in front, pineal behind, mammillary bodies + infundibulum on the floor. Six parts, all deep-midline.
Grounds Q123, L17 (~6:15) · Diencephalon
💡 Illustrative aid — NOT Dr. Maini’s words
Limbic cortex — the 4 gyri
💗 U-P-I-C: UNCUS · PARAHIPPOCAMPAL · ISTHMUS · CINGULATE
Maini groups four structures as the limbic cortex: uncus, parahippocampal gyrus, isthmus, and cingulate gyrus (the isthmus is the band linking the parahippocampal to the cingulate). The fornix is limbic too. Job: emotion, memory, reward/punishment.
💡 Read them as a loop from front-bottom around to top: Uncus → Parahippocampal → Isthmus → Cingulate. “UPIC” = the emotional ring.
Grounds Q54–Q56 · L16 Limbic system
💡 Illustrative aid — NOT Dr. Maini’s words
Tremor localizer
🤝 REST = BASAL NUCLEI · INTENTION = CEREBELLUM
A resting tremor is the hallmark of basal-nuclear dysfunction (Parkinsonism: slow, sluggish, rigid). An intention tremor — worse as the hand nears a target, with finger-nose overshoot, dysarthria, hypotonia — is the hallmark of cerebellar dysfunction.
💡 If it shakes at rest, blame the basal nuclei; if it shakes on intention (reaching), blame the cerebellum. Same word pairs the tremor to the structure.
Grounds Q49, Q89–Q90 · L16 Tremor types
💡 Illustrative aid — NOT Dr. Maini’s words
Brain vesicle derivatives
🌱 TELE→HEMISPHERES · DI→THALAMUS SET · MET→PONS+CEREBELLUM · MYEL→MEDULLA
Telencephalon → cerebral hemispheres. Diencephalon → thalami, hypothalamus, infundibulum, pineal, mammillary bodies. Mesencephalon → midbrain. Metencephalon → pons + cerebellum. Myelencephalon → medulla (+ spinal cord). The whole neural tube is from ectoderm.
💡 Two “M”s of the hindbrain, alphabetical by size going down: Metencephalon (pons + cerebellum) sits above Myelencephalon (medulla). “Myel” → myelinated cord below.
