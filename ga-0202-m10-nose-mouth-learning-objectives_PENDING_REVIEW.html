1
Explain what the nasal cavity does to inhaled air, name the parts of the nasal vestibule, and lay out the external nose’s cartilaginous and bony framework.
content: L23 · ~00:47–00:52
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State the three jobs done to inhaled air: it is warmed (NOT cooled), humidified, and filtered — Maini’s flagged “silly question that people miss.” (L23 · 00:48)
  • Add the drainage: the cavity also receives secretions from the paranasal sinuses and the nasolacrimal duct (tears). (L23 · 00:48)
  • Name the entrance: the nasal vestibule is the dilated space just inside the nostril aperture, lined by skin bearing stiff hairs called vibrissae (they trap dust and soot); posteriorly each cavity opens into the nasopharynx through the choana. (L23 · ~00:51)
  • Build the external nose: cartilaginous framework = septal cartilage + greater & lesser alar cartilages (around the alae/nostrils); bony framework = nasal bones + nasal part of the frontal bone + frontal process of the maxilla; the root point where frontal meets nasal bones = the nasion. (L23)

🧪 Quick self-check
As air passes through the nasal cavity, is it warmed or cooled — and what are the stiff hairs of the vestibule called?
Air is warmed (not cooled), humidified, and filtered — the classic trap is picking “cooled.” The stiff vestibule hairs are the vibrissae, which trap dust and soot. Posteriorly the cavity opens into the nasopharynx via the choana.
2
Build the nasal septum from its three main contributors, add the minor bone, and work the deviated-septum and lacrimal-bone traps.
content: L23 · ~00:53–00:56
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Name the bony majority (posterior part): the perpendicular plate of the ethmoid + the vomer. (L23 · 00:53)
  • Add the anterior support: septal cartilage; plus a small contribution from the nasal crest of the palatine bone. (L23 · 00:53)
  • Localize a deviation: a deviated septum is the cartilaginous part that bends (bone doesn’t bend); a symptomatic one is fixed by a submucous resection (SMR) + septoplasty. (L23 · 00:53)
  • Beat Maini’s “sneak-in” trap: the lacrimal bone is NOT part of the septum — it helps form the medial orbital wall and the lateral wall of the nasal cavity. (L23 · 00:55)

🧪 Quick self-check
Which two bones form the bony majority of the nasal septum, and if a septum is deviated, which part bends?
The perpendicular plate of the ethmoid + vomer form the bony (posterior) majority; septal cartilage supports the front. A deviation is in the cartilaginous part (bone doesn’t bend). The lacrimal bone is a distractor — it belongs to the orbit and lateral nasal wall, not the septum.
3
Assign the bones that build the roof, floor, and lateral wall of the nasal cavity.
content: L23 · ~00:54–00:56
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Floor = the hard palate (the same as the roof of the oral cavity): palatine process of the maxilla + horizontal plate of the palatine bone. (L23 · 00:54)
  • Roof: cribriform plate of the ethmoid (transmits the olfactory nerves) + body of the sphenoid + frontal bone + nasal bone. (L23)
  • Lateral wall: the frontal process of the maxilla + the medial pterygoid plate (part of the sphenoid), among other contributions. (L23)

🧪 Quick self-check
The floor of the nasal cavity is the same as the roof of what, and which two bones form it?
The floor of the nasal cavity = the roof of the oral cavity (the hard palate): palatine process of the maxilla + horizontal plate of the palatine bone. The roof’s cribriform plate (ethmoid) transmits the olfactory nerves.
4
Sort the three conchae by their parent bone, state what they do, and name the recess and the spaces beneath them.
content: L23 · ~00:56
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Assign the conchae: the superior and middle conchae are projections of the ethmoid bone; the inferior nasal concha is a separate bone of its own. (L23 · 00:56)
  • Give their function: they increase the surface area of the lateral wall to warm and humidify inhaled air. (L23)
  • Name the spaces: the pocket underneath each concha is a meatus (superior, middle, inferior); the recess above the superior concha is the sphenoethmoidal recess. (L23)

🧪 Quick self-check
Which conchae belong to the ethmoid, which stands alone as its own bone, and what is the space beneath a concha called?
Superior + middle conchae = projections of the ethmoid; the inferior concha is a separate bone. The space under each concha is a meatus; above the superior concha sits the sphenoethmoidal recess. Conchae exist to increase surface area for warming/humidifying air.
5
Name the four paranasal sinuses and trace exactly where each one — plus the nasolacrimal duct — drains.
content: L23 · ~01:00–01:12
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • List the four sinuses, each named for its bone: frontal, ethmoid, sphenoid, maxillary; the ethmoid is unusual — a honeycomb of anterior, middle & posterior air cells, each with its own opening. (L23)
  • Drain the “upper” ones: sphenoid sinus → sphenoethmoidal recess; posterior ethmoidal cells → superior meatus. (L23)
  • Drain into the middle meatus: the middle ethmoidal cells open on the ethmoidal bulla; along the hiatus semilunaris the frontal sinus enters at the superior end (via the infundibulum / frontonasal duct), the anterior ethmoidal cells at the middle, and the maxillary sinus at the inferior end. (L23 · 01:06)
  • Non-sinus drainage + clinic: the nasolacrimal duct opens into the inferior meatus; sinusitis is slow to clear on antibiotics because the sinuses are poorly vascularized (often ~3 weeks). (L23 · ~01:11)

🧪 Quick self-check
Into which meatus does the maxillary sinus drain, and which non-sinus structure drains into the inferior meatus?
The maxillary sinus drains into the middle meatus, at the inferior end of the hiatus semilunaris. The nasolacrimal duct (tears) drains into the inferior meatus. Sphenoid → sphenoethmoidal recess; posterior ethmoid → superior meatus; frontal + anterior ethmoid → middle meatus.
6
Supply the nasal cavity with blood and nerves — the two parent arteries, Kiesselbach’s plexus, and the sensory nerves.
content: L23 · ~01:12–01:14
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Name the two parent arteries: the maxillary (an ECA branch) and the ophthalmic (an ICA branch); the ophthalmic gives the anterior & posterior ethmoidal arteries through the ethmoidal foramina. (L23)
  • Localize the nosebleed: Kiesselbach’s area (Little’s area) in the anterior septum/vestibule is the anastomosis of the sphenopalatine, ethmoidal, and septal (superior labial branch of the facial) arteries — the most common site of anterior epistaxis. (L23 · 01:13)
  • Route the nerves: smell = the olfactory nerves through the cribriform plate of the ethmoid; general (touch/pain) sensation = the ophthalmic (V1) and maxillary (V2) divisions of the trigeminal. (L23)

🧪 Quick self-check
Where is the most common site of anterior nosebleeds, and which two divisions of CN V give the nasal cavity its general sensation?
Kiesselbach’s (Little’s) area in the anterior septum — an anastomosis of sphenopalatine, ethmoidal, and the septal branch (superior labial of the facial). General sensation = ophthalmic (V1) + maxillary (V2). Smell travels via the olfactory nerves through the cribriform plate.
7
Name the four lingual papillae, split the tongue at the terminal sulcus, and lay out Maini’s taste-by-location map.
content: L25 · ~00:03–00:21
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • List the four papillae: filiform (thread-like, blanket the dorsum), fungiform (mushroom-shaped, near the tip/periphery), foliate, and circumvallate/vallate (a V-shaped row just anterior to the terminal sulcus, taste buds sitting in a walled “well”). (L25 · 00:03)
  • Divide the tongue: the terminal sulcus (a backward-pointing V) separates the anterior two-thirds from the posterior one-third; the anterior 2/3 develops from the 1st pharyngeal arch. (L25)
  • Mark the midline pit: at the apex of the terminal sulcus is the foramen cecum, a blind remnant of the thyroglossal duct (where the thyroid began). (L25 · 00:21)
  • Map taste by receptor location: sweet at the tip, salty on the sides of the tip (Na+), sour from acids (H+), bitter at the posterior tongue, umami centrally (amino acids). (L25)

🧪 Quick self-check
Which papillae sit in a V-row anterior to the terminal sulcus, and what is the foramen cecum a remnant of?
Circumvallate (vallate) papillae form the V-row anterior to the terminal sulcus, with taste buds in a walled well. The foramen cecum, at the apex of the sulcus, is a remnant of the thyroglossal duct. The four papillae: filiform, fungiform, foliate, circumvallate.
8
Separate the extrinsic from the intrinsic tongue muscles, give each extrinsic its origin and action, and explain the “fence muscle.”
content: L25 · ~00:22–00:27
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Genioglossus: from the mental spine (genial tubercle) of the mandible; fan-shaped, forms the main bulk of the tongue; protrudes (posterior fibers) and depresses (anterior fibers) the tongue. (L25)
  • Hyoglossus: from the body & greater horn of the hyoid; depresses and retracts the tongue; it’s Maini’s “fence muscle” — it separates the nerves (hypoglossal, lingual) behind it from the arteries (lingual) in front. (L25 · 00:27)
  • Styloglossus (styloid process → retract + elevate the posterior tongue for swallowing) and palatoglossus (from the palatine aponeurosis → draws tongue & soft palate together to close the oropharyngeal isthmus). (L25)
  • Name the four intrinsic muscles (they alter the tongue’s shape): superior longitudinal, inferior longitudinal, transverse, and vertical. (L25)

🧪 Quick self-check
Which extrinsic tongue muscle forms the main bulk and protrudes the tongue, and which one is the “fence muscle”?
Genioglossus (from the genial tubercle) is the fan-shaped main bulk and the protruder. The hyoglossus is the “fence muscle” — nerves behind it, arteries in front. Intrinsic muscles (superior/inferior longitudinal, transverse, vertical) change the tongue’s shape.
9
Wire up the tongue — motor, general sensation, and taste for each region — then add its artery and lymph drainage.
content: L25 · ~00:16 + innervation block
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Motor: every tongue muscle is CN XII (hypoglossal) EXCEPT the palatoglossus, which is CN X (vagus) — because it belongs to the palate. (L25 · 00:16)
  • Anterior two-thirds: general sensation = lingual nerve (V3), cell bodies in the trigeminal ganglion; taste = chorda tympani (CN VII), cell bodies in the geniculate ganglion. (L25)
  • Posterior one-third: BOTH general sensation and taste = the glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX). (L25)
  • Vessels & lymph: the lingual artery is the 2nd branch of the ECA; lymph from the tip → submental nodes, from the posterior third → superior deep cervical nodes. (L25)

“Palatoglossus is innervated by 10th nerve, because it also belongs to [the] palatine [group].”— Oral Cavity (L25) · ~00:16:16
🎯 Cement it

🧪 Quick self-check
Every tongue muscle is CN XII except which one — and which single nerve carries both taste and general sensation for the posterior third?
The exception is palatoglossus (CN X, vagus). The posterior one-third gets both taste and general sensation from CN IX (glossopharyngeal). Anterior 2/3: general = lingual/V3 (trigeminal ganglion), taste = chorda tympani/VII (geniculate ganglion).
10
Close the mouth off from the oropharynx — the palatoglossus action, the palatoglossal arch, and the lingual frenulum.
content: L25 · ~00:08–00:17
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State the action: when the palatoglossus contracts it draws the tongue and soft palate together to close the oropharyngeal isthmus (the opening from the oral cavity into the oropharynx) while you chew. (L25 · 00:14)
  • Name the arch: the palatoglossus plus its overlying mucosa raises the palatoglossal arch on each side of that isthmus. (L25 · 00:17)
  • Tether the tongue: the lingual frenulum is the midline mucosal fold connecting the underside of the tongue to the floor of the mouth. (L25 · 00:08)

What does the palatoglossus close during chewing, and what does the lingual frenulum connect?
The palatoglossus closes the oropharyngeal isthmus (mouth-to-oropharynx opening) during chewing, raising the palatoglossal arch. The lingual frenulum tethers the underside of the tongue to the floor of the mouth.
11
Rank the three salivary glands and trace their autonomic supply — the parotid vs. submandibular/sublingual parasympathetic pathways.
content: L25 · ~00:32–00:40
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Rank them: the parotid is the largest gland (swells in mumps), but the submandibular produces the greatest share of saliva (~60%). (L25 · 00:32)
  • Parotid parasympathetic: CN IX (glossopharyngeal) — Maini’s “P is parotid, 9 is a mirror image of P” — from the inferior salivatory nucleus (medulla), synapsing in the otic ganglion, then riding the auriculotemporal nerve. (L25 · 00:34)
  • Submandibular & sublingual parasympathetic: CN VII via the chorda tympani, from the superior salivatory nucleus (pons), synapsing in the submandibular ganglion (fibers hitch a ride on the lingual nerve). (L25)
  • Sympathetic (all three): from the superior cervical ganglion, postganglionic fibers form the ECA plexus and “piggyback” along its branches to the glands; note the von Ebner glands on the tongue secrete lingual lipase. (L25 · ~00:38)

🧪 Quick self-check
Which gland is largest vs. produces the most saliva, and which cranial nerve carries parotid parasympathetics?
Parotid = largest gland; submandibular = greatest secretion (~60%). Parotid parasympathetics = CN IX (inferior salivatory nucleus → otic ganglion → auriculotemporal). Submandibular/sublingual = CN VII via chorda tympani (superior salivatory nucleus → submandibular ganglion).
12
Separate the “dys-” terms Maini contrasts, then match each tongue/oral lesion to its cause.
content: L25 · ~00:00–00:30
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Dysarthria = a disorder of articulation (the speech sounds, from a cerebellar or oral/tongue cause) with language itself intact. (L25 · 00:00)
  • Dysphagia = difficulty swallowing; dysphonia is NOT a tongue problem — it reflects laryngeal (voice-box) pathology. (L25)
  • Lesions: a chancre is the hard, sore tongue lesion of syphilis; a canker sore (aphthous ulcer) appears when immunity is low and heals faster with vitamin C (needed for collagen synthesis). (L25)
  • Cancer: Maini notes tongue/oral cancer was very common where she trained, driven by tobacco chewing. (L25)

🧪 Quick self-check
What does dysarthria mean, and which “dys-” term points to the larynx rather than the tongue?
Dysarthria = a disorder of articulation (speech sounds), with language intact. Dysphonia points to the larynx (voice), not the tongue; dysphagia = difficulty swallowing. Chancre = syphilis; canker sore heals with vitamin C; oral cancer is linked to tobacco chewing.
