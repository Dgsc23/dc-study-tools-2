Topic 1 — Triangles of the neck

Two big triangles, then slice each one up. Learn the muscle that forms each wall.
The two triangles 💡 my study aid
🔺 SCM SPLITS THE NECK: FRONT vs BACK
The sternocleidomastoid (SCM) divides each side of the neck. Anterior triangle = midline (front), anterior border of SCM (behind), lower border of the mandible (top). Posterior triangle = SCM (front), trapezius (back), clavicle (base).
💡 The SCM is the fence down the side of the neck: everything in front of it is the anterior triangle, everything behind it is the posterior triangle.
Bank Q4, Q20 · L03 — triangle boundaries
Posterior triangle 💡 my study aid
🚧 BACK-TRIANGLE = SCM + TRAP + CLAVICLE
Posterior triangle borders: SCM in front, trapezius behind, clavicle as the base. The inferior belly of omohyoid crosses it and splits it into the occipital triangle (above) and the supraclavicular / subclavian triangle (below).
💡 Two muscles and a bone box it in (SCM · Trap · Clavicle). The little omohyoid belly is the horizontal line that cuts the box into an upper and a lower room.
Bank Q4, Q5 · L03 — posterior triangle
Anterior triangle subdivisions 💡 my study aid
🍽️ 4 SLICES: SUBMENTAL · DIGASTRIC · CAROTID · MUSCULAR
The digastric and omohyoid bellies carve the anterior triangle into four smaller triangles: submental, digastric (submandibular), carotid, and muscular.
💡 Walk from the chin downward: Submental (under the chin), Digastric (under the jaw), Carotid (the pulse), Muscular (the straps). “Sam Digs Carrot Muffins” if you need the order.
Bank Q21, Q22 · L03 — anterior triangle
Shared walls (a Maini quiz item) 💡 my study aid
🧱 THE DIVIDING BELLY IS THE SHARED WALL
Maini asks which muscle is the common boundary between two triangles. Anterior belly of digastric = wall between submental & digastric. Posterior belly of digastric = wall between carotid & digastric. Superior belly of omohyoid = wall between muscular & carotid.
💡 The belly that draws the dividing line is the shared wall between the two rooms it separates. Front-belly up top, back-belly next door, omohyoid down low.

Topic 2 — Suprahyoid muscles & their nerves

Four muscles that lift the hyoid. The nerve supply is where Maini sets her traps.
Suprahyoid group 💡 my study aid
⬆️ SUPRA = LIFT: DIGASTRIC · STYLO · MYLO · GENIO
The suprahyoid muscles all elevate the hyoid bone (key in swallowing). Four of them: digastric, stylohyoid, mylohyoid, geniohyoid. Mylohyoid is the fan-shaped muscle forming the floor of the oral cavity; geniohyoid lies on top of mylohyoid.
💡 “Supra” = above the hyoid = they pull it up. (The infrahyoids below will pull it down.) Every -hyoid name is literally telling you what it grabs onto.
Bank Q37, Q48, Q51 · L03 — suprahyoid muscles
Digastric = 2 bellies, 2 nerves 💡 my study aid
🎭 BACK-BELLY = FACE (VII) · FRONT-BELLY = FIVE (V3)
The digastric is a “composite” muscle: two bellies, two different nerves. Posterior belly → facial nerve (CN VII). Anterior belly → nerve to mylohyoid (a branch of the inferior alveolar, from V3, the mandibular division of the trigeminal).
💡 Two bellies built from two different pharyngeal arches, so two different nerves. Back = Face (7), Front = Five (V3). The nerve to mylohyoid also runs the mylohyoid muscle next door.
Bank Q41-Q44 · L03 — digastric innervation
Post. digastric + stylohyoid 🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
🔤 “THEY MAKE AN F — F FOR FACIAL”
The posterior belly of digastric and the stylohyoid lie side by side and share one nerve: the facial nerve (CN VII). Maini points out the two muscles together trace an F shape — F for Facial.
🎓 Dr. Maini, verbatim (L03 ~40:16)“These two muscles… they make an F here… That is facial nerve. Posterior belly of digastric, stylohyoid, innervated by facial nerve.”
Bank Q47 · L03 transcript ~40:16–40:42
C1 hitchhikers 💡 my study aid
🚗 C1 HITCHHIKES ON CN XII → GENIOHYOID (& THYROHYOID)
Geniohyoid is run by C1 fibers carried along the hypoglossal nerve (CN XII) — not the trigeminal, not the facial. (The infrahyoid thyrohyoid gets the very same C1-via-XII supply, which is why it’s the odd one out downstairs.)
💡 CN XII is the tongue nerve, but C1 fibers hitch a ride on it and jump off to power geniohyoid and thyrohyoid. Same taxi, two drop-offs.

Topic 3 — Infrahyoid straps & the ansa cervicalis

Four straps that pull the hyoid down. One breaks the nerve pattern.
Infrahyoid group 💡 my study aid
⬇️ INFRA = DEPRESS: STERNO/STERNO/THYRO/OMO-HYOID
The infrahyoid (strap) muscles depress the hyoid bone: sternohyoid, sternothyroid, thyrohyoid, omohyoid. They live in the muscular triangle (that’s why it’s named “muscular”). Sternothyroid stops at the oblique line of the thyroid cartilage, so it depresses the larynx.
💡 “Infra” = below the hyoid = they pull it down. Read each name origin-to-insertion: sterno-thyroid = sternum to thyroid cartilage (never reaches the hyoid).
Bank Q54, Q57, Q58, Q30 · L03 — infrahyoid muscles
Omohyoid 💡 my study aid
🦴 OMO = SHOULDER: THE ONLY STRAP ON THE SCAPULA
Omohyoid is the only infrahyoid muscle that reaches the scapula (its inferior belly comes off the superior border of the scapula). Its intermediate tendon is slung to the clavicle and first rib by a loop of deep fascia.
💡 “Omo” is Greek for shoulder — so omohyoid is the strap that runs all the way out to the shoulder blade. The others stay on the sternum / thyroid cartilage.
Bank Q55, Q56 · L03 — omohyoid
Thyrohyoid’s odd nerve 🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
🅃 “T-MUSCLE” = TH = THYROHYOID (the exception)
Every infrahyoid is run by the ansa cervicalis — except thyrohyoid, which is run by C1 fibers carried on the hypoglossal nerve (CN XII). Maini’s tag for it is the “T-Muscle.”
🎓 Dr. Maini, verbatim (L03 ~55:50)“I gave this trick to most of the cohorts, T-Muscle. TH, thyrohyoid… this is the only muscle which has different nerve supply… C1 fibers through 12th nerve.”
Bank Q18, Q60 · L03 transcript ~55:50
Ansa cervicalis roots 💡 my study aid
🔁 LOOP = 1 ON TOP (C1), 2-3 BELOW (C2,C3)
The ansa cervicalis is a nerve loop that supplies most of the infrahyoid muscles. Superior root = C1 fibers (riding the hypoglossal, then peeling off). Inferior root = C2 and C3. The two roots meet to form the loop (“ansa” = loop / handle).
💡 Number the loop: 1 comes over the top (superior root, C1), 2 and 3 come up from below (inferior root). They join into a hoop that drops branches onto the straps.
Bank Q15-Q17 · L03 — ansa cervicalis

Topic 4 — Cervical plexus & neck nerves

C1 to C4 fans out. The four skin branches all pop out at one spot.
Cervical plexus + nerve point 💡 my study aid
📍 C1-C4, ALL 4 SKIN BRANCHES EXIT AT ERB’S POINT
The cervical plexus is built from the ventral rami of C1 to C4. Its four cutaneous (superficial) branches all emerge at one spot: the nerve point of the neck (Erb’s point), at the middle of the posterior border of SCM.
💡 One doorway, four nerves. Find the midpoint of the back edge of SCM and all four skin nerves are pouring out of that single “nerve point.”
Bank Q7, Q11 · L03 — cervical plexus
The 4 cutaneous branches 💡 my study aid
🔤 “GLAT”: GREAT-AURICULAR · LESSER-OCC · TRANSVERSE · SUPRACLAV
The four skin branches: lesser occipital, greater auricular (heads up toward the ear/auricle), transverse cervical, and supraclavicular (which splits into medial, intermediate, lateral).
💡 Read each name as its destination: occipital = back of head, auricular = ear, transverse cervical = across the neck, supraclavicular = over the collarbone. The names are the map.
Bank Q9, Q10, Q12 · L03 — cutaneous branches
Phrenic nerve 🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
🫁 “3, 4, 5, TO KEEP YOU ALIVE”
The phrenic nerve is a muscular branch of the cervical plexus with root value C3, C4, C5. It runs the diaphragm — so its roots literally “keep you alive” (breathing).
🎓 Dr. Maini, verbatim (L03 ~11:39)“Phrenic nerve is also one of the muscular branch. What is the root value? 3, 4, 5, to keep you alive, right? It’s a branch of cervical plexus.”
Bank Q13 · L03 transcript ~11:39
SCM, trapezius & spinal accessory 💡 my study aid
🏋️ CN XI DRIVES SCM + TRAP · C2-C4 RIDE ALONG
SCM and trapezius get motor supply from the spinal accessory nerve (CN XI), with C2, C3, C4 fibers joining it. Find CN XI in the posterior triangle — that’s Maini’s favorite “which triangle?” answer.
💡 Two big surface muscles, one motor nerve (11), with cervical fibers tagging along for proprioception. And the nerve that runs them crosses the posterior triangle to get there.

Topic 5 — Carotid arteries

One sheath, two carotids, eight external branches. Maini hands you two of these hooks herself.
Carotid sheath contents 🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
⚔️ “THE THREE MUSKETEERS” = CCA + IJV + VAGUS
Three structures ride together inside the carotid sheath: the common carotid artery, the internal jugular vein, and the vagus nerve (CN X). After the common carotid bifurcates, the external carotid leaves the sheath; internal carotid, IJV and vagus stay.
🎓 Dr. Maini, verbatim (L03 ~24:52)“Common carotid artery, internal jugular vein and vagus… wrapped up in a common sheath called carotid sheath, and they’re called as the three musketeers, because they lie together.”
Bank Q35, Q36 · L03 ~24:52, L05 ~confirmed
Bifurcation level 💡 my study aid
✌️ COMMON CAROTID SPLITS AT THE TOP OF THYROID CARTILAGE
The common carotid bifurcates into external and internal carotid at the superior border of the thyroid cartilage. Right common carotid comes off the brachiocephalic trunk; left comes straight off the aortic arch.
💡 The Adam’s-apple cartilage marks the fork in the road: below it, one common carotid; at its top edge, it splits into the two branches.
Bank Q62-Q64 · L03 — carotid origins
Internal vs external carotid 💡 my study aid
🚫 INTERNAL = NO NECK BRANCHES (the quiet one)
Easy tell: the internal carotid gives NO branches in the neck — it heads straight up and enters the skull through the carotid canal. The external carotid is the one throwing off all the neck branches to face and scalp.
💡 Internal keeps to itself (no neck branches, goes into the skull); external spreads out to everything outside the skull. Branchy artery in the neck = external, every time.
Bank Q65, Q66 · L03 — ICA vs ECA
External carotid’s 8 branches 🎓 Dr. Maini’s own
🔤 “SOME ANATOMISTS LIKE FREAKING OUT POOR MEDICAL STUDENTS”
The mnemonic Maini wrote on the slide for the 8 external-carotid branches (root to tip): Superior thyroid, Ascending pharyngeal, Lingual, Facial, Occipital, Posterior auricular, Maxillary, Superficial temporal. Grouped: 3 anterior (superior thyroid, lingual, facial), 3 posterior (ascending pharyngeal, occipital, posterior auricular), 2 terminal (maxillary, superficial temporal).
🎓 Dr. Maini, verbatim (L03 ~1:12:45)“Some anatomists like freaking out poor medical students… That’s why I already wrote the mnemonic.” (First letters = the ECA branches.)
Bank Q71-Q74 · L03 ~1:12:45, L05 ~22:08
Carotid sinus vs carotid body 💡 my study aid
🩺 SINUS = SQUEEZE (baro) · BODY = BREATH-GAS (chemo)
Carotid sinus (a dilatation at the start of the internal carotid) holds baroreceptors — blood pressure. Carotid body holds chemoreceptors — senses ↑CO2, ↑H+, ↓O2. Both report through the glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX).
💡 Baro = pressure (the sinus feels the squeeze); chemo = chemistry (the body sniffs the blood gases). One messenger for both: the 9th nerve.

Topic 6 — Subclavian artery & neck veins

The anterior scalene is the landmark. What’s in front of it, behind it, and how the artery is cut into parts.
Anterior scalene relations 💡 my study aid
🚦 ARTERY & PLEXUS HIDE BEHIND · VEIN RIDES IN FRONT
Maini’s guaranteed quiz shape: what’s anterior vs posterior to the anterior scalene? POSTERIOR = subclavian artery + brachial plexus (trunks, from C5-T1, emerging between anterior and middle scalene). ANTERIOR = subclavian vein + the transverse cervical and suprascapular arteries.
💡 “Artery is At the back” with the plexus; the vein is out in front. That’s also why scalene (thoracic outlet) syndrome squeezes the artery + plexus together, not the vein.
Bank Q1, Q81, Q90, Q92, Q91 · L03 / L05
Subclavian artery in 3 parts 💡 my study aid
1️⃣2️⃣3️⃣ BEFORE · BEHIND · BEYOND the scalene
The anterior scalene cuts the subclavian artery into 3 parts: Part 1 = medial to the muscle, Part 2 = deep / posterior to it, Part 3 = lateral to it.
💡 Track the artery left-to-right across the muscle: 1 before it (medial), 2 behind it (deep), 3 beyond it (lateral). Same “muscle divides the vessel into 3” trick as pec minor with the axillary artery.
Bank Q82 · L03 — subclavian parts
Part-1 branches 💡 my study aid
🔤 “VIT”: VERTEBRAL · INTERNAL THORACIC · THYROCERVICAL
Part 1 gives three branches: Vertebral (up the transverse foramina to the foramen magnum), Internal Thoracic, and the Thyrocervical trunk. Part 2 = costocervical trunk (→ deep cervical + superior intercostal). Part 3 = usually nothing (occasionally a dorsal scapular).
💡 VIT for “vital” branches off part 1. Most action is part 1; part 2 gives one trunk; part 3 is usually a no-show.
Bank Q83, Q84, Q87-Q89 · L03 — subclavian branches
Thyrocervical trunk 💡 my study aid
🔱 TRUNK OF 3: INFERIOR THYROID · TRANSVERSE CERVICAL · SUPRASCAPULAR
The thyrocervical trunk (off part 1) fans into three: inferior thyroid (supplies the lower thyroid), transverse cervical, and suprascapular. Note: the inferior thyroid is the thyroid vessel that comes from the subclavian, not the external carotid.
💡 Three prongs from a short trunk: one goes to the thyroid (inferior thyroid), two head out toward the shoulder blade (transverse cervical, suprascapular) — the same two that ride in front of the anterior scalene.
Bank Q85, Q86 · L03 / L05 — thyrocervical
Internal jugular vein 💡 my study aid
🧠 IJV: SIGMOID SINUS IN → JOINS SUBCLAVIAN → BRACHIOCEPHALIC
The internal jugular vein begins at the jugular foramen as the continuation of the sigmoid sinus, drains brain, face & neck, rides in the carotid sheath, then unites with the subclavian vein to form the brachiocephalic vein.
💡 Trace it top to bottom: sigmoid sinus turns into the IJV at the jugular foramen, it collects the head, then shakes hands with the subclavian to make the brachiocephalic.
Bank Q94-Q97 · L05 — neck veins
Thyroid vein drainage (the trap) 💡 my study aid
🩸 SUPERIOR + MIDDLE → IJV · INFERIOR → BRACHIOCEPHALIC
Thyroid venous drainage splits: superior and middle thyroid veins drain into the internal jugular vein, but the inferior thyroid vein is the odd one out — it drains straight into the brachiocephalic vein.
💡 Top two go to the IJV; the bottom one drops lower, all the way to the brachiocephalic. “Inferior drains inferiorly.”
