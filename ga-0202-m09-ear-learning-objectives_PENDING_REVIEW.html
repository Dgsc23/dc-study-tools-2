1
Divide the ear into external, middle, and internal parts, and explain the path of sound — conduit vs receptors.
content: The Ear · L22 · 00:00:15–00:01:32
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Name the three regions: external, middle, and internal (inner) ear. The auditory receptors sit deep and protected in the internal ear, where vibrations are converted into action potentials that travel to the temporal lobe to be interpreted. (L22 00:00:15–00:00:43)
  • State the division of labor: the external + middle ear are a conduit (a conducting passage) that carries sound-wave vibrations inward; only the internal ear holds receptors. (L22 00:00:43–00:01:09)
  • List the three parts of the external ear: auricle, external acoustic meatus, and tympanic membrane. (L22 00:01:18–00:01:32)

Which part of the ear actually contains the auditory receptors, and what job do the external and middle ear do?
The internal (inner) ear holds the receptors, where vibrations become action potentials sent to the temporal lobe. The external and middle ear are just a conducting conduit that funnels the vibrations inward.
2
Describe the auricle (pinna) — its cartilage, blood and nerve supply, and its named surface landmarks.
content: The Ear · L22 · 00:02:11–00:05:04, 00:39:17–00:41:08
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Structure: the visible part of the external ear is a single piece of elastic cartilage (the same elastic cartilage found in the epiglottis) covered by thin, hairy skin. The earlobe (lobule) has no cartilage — just skin and soft tissue — and is very vascular. (L22 00:02:11–00:03:08)
  • Blood supply: the superficial temporal, posterior auricular, and occipital arteries — all branches of the external carotid artery. (L22 00:03:15–00:03:30)
  • Nerve supply: sensory from the great auricular nerve (cervical plexus) and the auriculotemporal nerve (a branch of V3); motor to the rudimentary auricular muscles from the facial nerve (VII). (L22 00:03:34–00:05:04)
  • Surface parts (Maini says exam has “bold questions” here): helix (outer ridge), antihelix (parallel to it), scapha (boat-shaped fossa between them), tragus (just anterior to the meatus), antitragus (opposite it), the intertragic notch between the two, and the lobule. (L22 00:39:31–00:41:08)

What type of cartilage forms the auricle, and from which artery do all of its named arteries arise?
A single piece of elastic cartilage (the same as the epiglottis). All its arteries — superficial temporal, posterior auricular, occipital — are branches of the external carotid artery. The earlobe has no cartilage.
3
Detail the external acoustic meatus — its shape, length, support, secretions, and the nerve behind the ear-to-throat reflex.
content: The Ear · L22 · 00:06:40–00:08:42
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Shape & size: a sinuous (S-shaped) tube about 2.5 cm long. (L22 00:06:40–00:07:09)
  • Support: lateral one-third by elastic cartilage, medial two-thirds by the temporal bone. (L22 00:06:51–00:07:09)
  • Secretions: lined by skin with ceruminous (wax) and sebaceous glands; cerumen plus hairs form a mat that traps dust, soot, and small insects. (L22 00:07:12–00:07:39)
  • Innervation trap: the auricular branch of the vagus nerve carries GSA fibers from the meatus — which is why cleaning your ear can trigger a cough / throat sensation. (L22 00:07:56–00:08:42)

4
Describe the tympanic membrane and read its four quadrants clinically — the cone of light, pars flaccida, and chorda tympani.
content: The Ear · L22 · 00:08:47–00:15:13
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Structure: a thin, semi-transparent, oval, pearly-gray fibrous membrane — thin skin externally, mucous membrane internally, with a core of dense fibrous tissue. Central depression = umbo (where the malleus handle attaches). (L22 00:08:47–00:10:14)
  • Cone of light (Maini: “this can appear as a question in your exam”): radiates antero-inferiorly — right ear 5 o’clock, left ear 7 o’clock. (L22 00:09:59–00:10:37)
  • Quadrants & the lax part: divided into four quadrants for examination; the small superior lax part = pars flaccida (the inferior ~6/7 is the tense pars tensa). Puncturing the postero-superior quadrant risks the chorda tympani lying just behind it. (L22 00:10:37–00:12:13)
  • Supply: external surface — deep auricular branch of the maxillary artery; sensory to the internal (medial) surface — the glossopharyngeal nerve (IX). (L22 00:14:33–00:15:13)

🧪 Quick self-check
In the LEFT ear, where does the cone of light point, and which structure is at risk if you puncture the postero-superior quadrant?

5
Work through the tympanic-membrane clinical picture — myringotomy, why the eardrum ruptures, and how it costs you hearing.
content: The Ear · L22 · 00:12:18–00:14:28, 00:16:38–00:17:16
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Myringotomy: a tiny surgical opening (with a small drainage tube) in the postero-inferior quadrant — the preferred site because no important structure lies behind it — to drain a fluid/pus collection from the middle ear before it ruptures the eardrum. (L22 00:12:18–00:14:28)
  • Why not let it burst: a spontaneous rupture leaves serrated (irregular) margins and heals poorly, whereas the clean surgical puncture heals without a scar. (L22 00:13:06–00:13:46)
  • Cost of a bad drum: a ruptured, thickened, or fibrosed tympanic membrane causes (conductive) hearing impairment. (L22 00:13:46–00:14:18)
  • Spread of infection: because the mastoid process is not solid but full of air cells, a middle-ear infection can spread posteriorly (via the aditus) to cause mastoiditis. (L22 00:16:38–00:17:16)

🧪 Quick self-check
Which quadrant of the eardrum is preferred for a myringotomy and why, and where can an untreated middle-ear infection spread?
The postero-inferior quadrant — nothing important lies behind it. Untreated middle-ear infection can spread through the aditus to the mastoid air cells → mastoiditis.
6
Trace the ear’s three first-arch derivatives — which embryonic structure gives rise to the meatus, the eardrum, and the middle ear.
content: The Ear · L22 · 00:09:15–00:09:53, 00:15:13–00:15:36
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • The tympanic membrane develops from the first pharyngeal membrane. (L22 00:09:15–00:09:53)
  • The middle ear (tympanic cavity) develops from the first pharyngeal pouch. (L22 00:15:13)
  • The external acoustic meatus develops from the first pharyngeal cleft (groove). (L22 00:15:18–00:15:36)
  • Tie it together: the cleft invaginates and the pouch evaginates, and the two meet at the tympanic membrane — so the eardrum is the meeting point of external and middle ear. (L22 00:15:28–00:15:36)

7
Box the middle-ear (tympanic) cavity — its location and the structures on each of its walls.
content: The Ear · L22 · 00:16:24–00:26:08
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Location & roof/floor: the tympanic cavity sits in the petrous portion of the temporal bone; roof = tegmen tympani (a plate of temporal bone); floor = roof of the jugular fossa. (L22 00:17:51–00:20:22)
  • Anterior (incomplete) wall: two openings — the pharyngotympanic (auditory) tube and the canal for tensor tympani. Posterior wall: the aditus to the mastoid antrum, the pyramid (anchors the stapedius), and part of the facial canal. (L22 00:20:22–00:26:08)
  • Medial wall: the promontory (formed by the base of the cochlea), the oval window (fenestra vestibuli) and round window (fenestra cochlea), and part of the facial canal — which runs in both the medial and posterior walls. (L22 00:22:38–00:25:00)
  • Lateral wall: the tympanic membrane itself. (L22 00:23:31)

What forms the roof and floor of the middle ear, and what makes the bulge (promontory) on its medial wall?
Roof = tegmen tympani; floor = roof of the jugular fossa. The promontory on the medial wall is the base of the cochlea; the oval and round windows also sit on that medial wall.
8
Name the three auditory ossicles in order, their shapes and joints, and how they carry vibration to the inner ear.
content: The Ear · L22 · 00:28:01–00:31:57
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • The three tiniest bones in the body, in order from the eardrum inward: malleus, incus, stapes. (L22 00:28:06–00:28:46)
  • Shapes & parts: malleus = largest, a hammer, whose handle (manubrium) attaches to the tympanic membrane; incus = an anvil (body + long and short processes); stapes = a stirrup (head, neck, two crura, and a footplate that sits in the oval window). (L22 00:28:06–00:30:44)
  • Joints: the incudomalleolar joint (malleus–incus) is a saddle joint; the incudostapedial joint (incus–stapes) is a ball-and-socket joint. (L22 00:31:34–00:31:42)
  • The chain in action: eardrum vibration → malleus → incus → stapes → footplate of stapes at the oval window → the perilymph of the inner ear. (L22 00:30:44–00:31:00)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Those vibrations are transmitted to malleus, incus, [stapes], footplate of [stapes], inner ear. Oval window is the window to internal ear.”— L22 The Ear · ~00:30:52–00:31:00

🧪 Quick self-check
List the ossicles from eardrum inward, and name the joint between the incus and stapes.
Malleus → incus → stapes. The incudostapedial joint (incus–stapes) is a ball-and-socket joint; the incudomalleolar joint (malleus–incus) is a saddle joint. The stapes footplate sits in the oval window.
9
Contrast the two middle-ear muscles and explain the protective auditory reflex — and what its failure sounds like.
content: The Ear · L22 · 00:32:02–00:37:02
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Tensor tympani: a first-arch muscle innervated by V3 (nerve to medial pterygoid), originating from the cartilage of the auditory tube; it draws the tympanic membrane medially, tensing it to lessen sound transmission. (L22 00:32:02–00:35:26)
  • Stapedius: originates from the pyramid (posterior wall), inserts on the neck of the stapes, and is supplied by the facial nerve (VII); it dampens vibration by pulling the head of the stapes posteriorly. (L22 00:35:32–00:36:19)
  • The auditory (acoustic) reflex: in loud surroundings both muscles reflexly contract to protect the delicate inner-ear receptors from loud noise. (L22 00:36:40–00:36:51)
  • Failure: loss of stapedius (e.g., facial-nerve injury) → hyperacusis — abnormal sensitivity to ordinary sounds. (L22 00:36:51–00:37:02)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“This is a protective reflex. This is called as auditory reflex… these two muscles, they contract when you’re surrounded by very loud noises.”— L22 The Ear · ~00:36:40–00:36:51
🎯 Cement it
🎯 Everything-MC 📚 Master Ref 📜 M09 Ear Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which nerve supplies stapedius, and what condition results if it stops working?
The facial nerve (VII) supplies stapedius. Losing it → hyperacusis (over-sensitivity to normal-volume sound). The other muscle, tensor tympani, is V3-supplied and tenses the eardrum.
10
Explain the auditory tube — its three names, its true job, its structure, and its clinical quirks.
content: The Ear · L22 · 00:32:22–00:38:55
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Three names for one tube: auditory tube = pharyngotympanic tube = eustachian tube; it connects the middle ear to the nasopharynx. (L22 00:34:34–00:35:04, 00:38:47)
  • Its real function (Maini’s trap): it equalizes air pressure on the two sides of the tympanic membrane — it has nothing to do with conducting sound. Don’t confuse it with the external acoustic meatus. (L22 00:34:39–00:35:04)
  • Structure: about 3.5–4 cm long — the posterior one-third bony, the anterior two-thirds cartilaginous; a tubal tonsil (lymphoid tissue) sits at its nasopharyngeal opening. (L22 00:37:15–00:38:35)
  • Clinical: on a plane, yawning or a Valsalva maneuver forces air through the tube to re-equalize pressure; a tube blocked by flu secretions gives a nasal twang to the voice. (L22 00:32:46–00:34:05, 00:37:06–00:37:33)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Auditory tube has got nothing to do with hearing… it’s for pressure equalization.”— L22 The Ear · ~00:34:39–00:34:53
🎯 Cement it

🧪 Quick self-check
What is the auditory tube’s true function, and what are its two other names?
It equalizes air pressure across the tympanic membrane (not hearing). Also called the pharyngotympanic tube and the eustachian tube; it runs from the middle ear to the nasopharynx. Don’t confuse it with the external acoustic meatus.
11
Distinguish the bony vs membranous labyrinth and place the two inner-ear fluids correctly.
content: The Ear · L23 (inner ear) · ~00:00:15–00:05:03
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • The internal (inner) ear is the labyrinth, sitting in the petrous temporal bone; it has a bony labyrinth (an outer bony shell) and a membranous labyrinth inside it that follows the same contour (Maini’s snail / walnut-shell analogy). (L23 00:00:15–00:01:56)
  • Perilymph lies outside the membranous labyrinth (between it and the bony shell); endolymph fills the membranous labyrinth inside. (L23 00:01:54–00:02:40)
  • The bony labyrinth has three parts: the cochlea, the vestibule, and the three semicircular canals. (L23 00:03:06–00:03:26)
  • The membranous parts nest inside: cochlear duct inside the cochlea, utricle + saccule inside the vestibule, semicircular ducts inside the semicircular canals. (L23 00:04:38–00:05:03)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“If you crack the bony shell, a fluid will ooze out. That is perilymph… There is another fluid inside the membranous structure that is called as endolymph.”— L23 The Ear (inner ear) · ~00:01:44–00:02:03
🎯 Cement it
🎯 Everything-MC 📚 Master Ref 📜 M09 Ear Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which fluid is inside the membranous labyrinth, and which is outside it?
Endolymph fills the membranous labyrinth (inside); perilymph lies outside it, between the membranous and bony labyrinth. The bony labyrinth = cochlea + vestibule + 3 semicircular canals.
12
Build the cochlea — its coil and core, its three compartments, and where hearing is actually transduced.
content: The Ear · L23 (inner ear) · ~00:03:31–00:13:01
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • The cochlea is a coiled tube making about two and a half turns around a central bony core, the modiolus, which houses the spiral ganglion; the osseous spiral lamina is a thread of bone winding around the modiolus. (The “spiral” names all come from the coiled cochlear duct.) (L23 00:03:31–00:09:20)
  • Two membranes (the vestibular membrane as roof, the basilar membrane as floor) divide it into three compartments: the scala vestibuli and scala tympani (both perilymph) and, between them, the cochlear duct / scala media (endolymph). (L23 00:11:05–00:13:01)
  • The organ of Corti (spiral organ) sits on the basilar membrane; its hair cells are the hearing receptors, with cilia embedded in the gelatinous tectorial membrane. Bending the cilia against the tectorial membrane triggers the action potential. (audited bank; L23)
  • The output: bipolar cells of the spiral ganglion send their axons as the cochlear division of CN VIII (hearing). At the apex the helicotrema links scala vestibuli and tympani; low-frequency sound is coded near the apex/helicotrema, high frequency near the base. (audited bank; L23)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Cochlea… has two and a half turns around a bony modiolus… This whole cochlea is divided into 3 compartments by 2 membranes.”— L23 The Ear (inner ear) · ~00:03:31 / 00:11:05
🎯 Cement it
🎯 Everything-MC 📚 Master Ref 📜 M09 Ear Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
On which membrane does the organ of Corti sit, and which fluid fills the cochlear duct?
The organ of Corti sits on the basilar membrane; the cochlear duct (scala media) is filled with endolymph. The flanking scala vestibuli and scala tympani hold perilymph, and CN VIII’s cochlear division carries hearing.
13
Explain the equilibrium apparatus — the vestibule’s maculae and the semicircular canals’ cristae, and what motion each senses.
content: The Ear · L23 (inner ear) · ~00:04:01–00:38:50
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • The vestibule (a box-like part, ~5 mm) holds the utricle (larger) and saccule (smaller); each carries a sensory spot, the macula, covered by an otolithic membrane studded with tiny stones (otoliths). The maculae sense static equilibrium and linear acceleration — macula utriculi horizontal, macula sacculi vertical (e.g., an elevator). (L23 00:26:14–00:35:05)
  • The utricle and saccule communicate via the utriculosaccular duct, which connects to the endolymphatic duct and sac; the vestibule links to the posterior cranial fossa through the aqueduct of the vestibule. (audited bank; L23)
  • The three semicircular canals sit at right angles (90°) to one another and sense angular / rotational acceleration — dynamic equilibrium. Board-style point Maini flags: name them relative to the posterior surface of the petrous temporal bone (anterior = at a right angle to it, posterior = along it, lateral = horizontal). (L23 00:04:01–00:04:35; audited bank)
  • Each duct’s dilated end (the ampulla) holds the crista ampullaris, whose hair cells are embedded in a gelatinous cupula. Summary of the three receptors and their gels: organ of Corti → tectorial membrane; macula → otolithic membrane; crista ampullaris → cupula. (L23 00:36:44–00:38:50)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“These three semicircular canals… are oriented at right angle to each other… they inform your brain about the changes in the direction when you are rotating your head, or during the angular motion.”— L23 The Ear (inner ear) · ~00:04:01–00:04:24
🎯 Cement it
🎯 Everything-MC 🚩 Maini-Flags 📜 M09 Ear Hub
🧪 Quick self-check
Which inner-ear organ senses rotation, which senses linear motion, and what gel sits on each?
The crista ampullaris (in the semicircular canals) senses rotation / dynamic equilibrium — hair cells in a cupula. The macula (utricle & saccule) senses static / linear motion — hair cells under an otolithic membrane with otoliths.
14
Route the nerves and vessels through the ear, and match Dr. Maini’s clinical tests to what they assess.
content: The Ear · L22/L23 + ear review · L22 00:26:33–00:27:54
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • The internal acoustic meatus opens on the postero-medial petrous temporal bone and transmits CN VII (facial) and CN VIII (vestibulocochlear) plus the labyrinthine artery and vein; the facial nerve enters here, then runs in the facial canal. (audited bank; L23)
  • Brainstem landmark: CN VI, VII, and VIII all emerge/attach at the pontomedullary sulcus (junction). (audited bank)
  • In the middle ear, the tympanic branch of CN IX (glossopharyngeal) forms the tympanic plexus over the promontory, then leaves as the lesser petrosal nerve toward the otic ganglion (secretomotor to the parotid). (L22 00:26:33–00:27:54)
  • Clinical tests: Weber’s and Rinne’s tests assess hearing; the caloric test (irrigating the ear with warm and cold water) assesses vestibular (labyrinth / semicircular-canal) function. (audited bank — Maini’s ear review)

🧪 Quick self-check
What passes through the internal acoustic meatus, and what does the caloric test assess?
CN VII and CN VIII plus the labyrinthine vessels pass through the internal acoustic meatus. The caloric test (warm/cold water) assesses vestibular function; Weber’s and Rinne’s tests assess hearing.

🔗 How to use these 14 to study

1. Read each objective out loud, then try the self-check before revealing the answer. If you nailed it, tick Got it.

2. For any objective you miss, jump back to the M09 Ear Hub and drill exactly that topic (the Everything-MC bank is the fastest way to re-test it).

3. Dr. Maini flagged a few things as likely exam questions: the cone of light (right 5 o’clock / left 7 o’clock, Objective 4), the “bold questions” on the parts of the auricle (Objective 2), and naming the semicircular canals against the petrous surface (Objective 13). Spend extra reps there.

⚠️ Don’t mix up the classic ear traps. The auditory tube (pressure equalization) is not the external acoustic meatus (sound conduction). Perilymph is outside the membranous labyrinth; endolymph is inside it (and fills the cochlear duct). And the two equilibrium organs split by motion: the crista ampullaris (in the semicircular canals, with a cupula) = rotational / dynamic; the macula (in the utricle & saccule, with an otolithic membrane) = static / linear.
