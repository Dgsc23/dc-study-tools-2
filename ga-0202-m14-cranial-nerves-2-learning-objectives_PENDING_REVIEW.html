CN IV
Account for the trochlear nerve (CN IV) — its single muscle, its unique dorsal-midbrain exit, its action, orbital entry, and palsy.
source: Dr. Maini · L17 Brain / L20 The Orbit
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State that CN IV supplies exactly ONE muscle — the superior oblique (the “SO4” in Maini’s SO4 / LR6 / AO3 eye-muscle rule) — and is purely motor. (L20 Orbit)
  • Name its landmark: CN IV is the only cranial nerve to emerge from the dorsal (posterior) surface of the brainstem, at the midbrain. (L17 Brain)
  • Trace the muscle: the superior oblique tendon loops through a fibrocartilaginous pulley, the trochlea, and rotates the eye down and out (depression + lateral rotation). (L20 Orbit)
  • Apply it: CN IV enters the orbit through the superior orbital fissure; a CN IV palsy makes it hardest to look down-and-in (classic: trouble descending stairs). (L20/L21 Orbit)

“It goes like SO4, LR6, AO3… superior oblique, that is your trochlear nerve.”— Dr. Maini · L20 The Orbit (contd) · SO4/LR6/AO3 mnemonic

🧪 Quick self-check
Which single muscle does CN IV supply, from which brainstem surface does it emerge, and what eye movement results?
Superior oblique (SO4) — the only nerve from the dorsal/posterior surface of the midbrain. Via the trochlea it moves the eye down and out. Purely motor; enters through the superior orbital fissure.
CN V
Map the trigeminal nerve (CN V) — its three divisions and their skull exits, the one mixed division, mastication, its sensory ganglion, and tongue sensation.
source: Dr. Maini · L01 Osteology / L10–11 Infratemporal Fossa / L06 Pharyngeal Apparatus
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • List the three divisions and their exits: V1 ophthalmic → superior orbital fissure, V2 maxillary → foramen rotundum, V3 mandibular → foramen ovale. Middle-fossa order: SOF → rotundum → ovale → spinosum. (L01 Osteology)
  • Flag the odd one: V3 is the only mixed (sensory + motor) division; its motor fibers drive the muscles of mastication (e.g., temporalis). CN V + mastication = 1st pharyngeal arch, and nearly all V3 branches leave in the infratemporal fossa. (L10 Infratemporal Fossa)
  • Locate the ganglion: the large trigeminal (sensory) ganglion sits at the apex of the petrous temporal bone — it is a sensory ganglion, NOT autonomic. (L11 Infratemporal Fossa)
  • Own the tongue link: general (touch/pain) sensation of the anterior 2/3 of the tongue = lingual nerve (V3); the chorda tympani (CN VII taste) hitchhikes on it. Corneal-reflex afferent = V1; trigeminal neuralgia = shock-like V2/V3 pain. (L06 Pharyngeal Apparatus)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Superior orbital fissure, foramen rotundum, ovale, foramen spinosum.”— Dr. Maini · L01 Osteology of the Skull

🧪 Quick self-check
Through which foramina do V1, V2, and V3 leave the skull, and which division is mixed?
V1 = superior orbital fissure, V2 = foramen rotundum, V3 = foramen ovale. V3 is the mixed (sensory + motor) division — its motor fibers run the muscles of mastication. Sensory ganglion sits on the petrous temporal bone.
CN VI
State the abducens nerve (CN VI) essentials — its one muscle, its action, orbital entry, and why it’s the “diplopia” nerve.
source: Dr. Maini · L17 Brain / L19–21 The Orbit
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State that CN VI supplies exactly ONE muscle — the lateral rectus (“LR6”) — and is purely motor. (L20 Orbit)
  • Decode the name: abducens = it abducts the eye (turns it out/laterally); it’s isolated on testing when the patient looks straight laterally (temporally). (L21 Testing of Eye Muscles)
  • Route it: CN VI enters the orbit through the superior orbital fissure, alongside CN III, IV, and V1. (L19 Orbit)
  • Apply it: a CN VI lesion lets the medial rectus win, so the eye deviates medially with horizontal diplopia; its long intracranial course makes it the first eye nerve hit by raised intracranial pressure. (L20 Orbit)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“This nerve supplies the abductor of the eye, so abducens.”— Dr. Maini · L17 The Brain (contd) · CN VI

🧪 Quick self-check
Which muscle does CN VI supply, and which way does the eye deviate if it is lesioned?
Lateral rectus (LR6). A lesion → the eye deviates medially (unopposed medial rectus) with horizontal double vision. It enters via the superior orbital fissure and is first affected by raised intracranial pressure.
CN VII
Trace the facial nerve (CN VII) — its skull entry/exit, the geniculate ganglion, its motor + taste + secretomotor jobs, and Bell’s palsy.
source: Dr. Maini · L08 Face & Scalp / L22 The Ear
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Run the course: enters with CN VIII through the internal acoustic meatus → the bend (genu) carries the geniculate ganglion (cell bodies of taste/SVA from the anterior 2/3 tongue) → exits the stylomastoid foramen → passes through the parotid gland (5 terminal branches, e.g., marginal mandibular). (L08 Face & Scalp)
  • Give the motor job: the muscles of facial expression (e.g., orbicularis oculi), plus the posterior belly of digastric, stylohyoid, and stapedius. (L08 Face & Scalp)
  • Give the taste + secretomotor jobs: taste from the anterior 2/3 of the tongue; its chorda tympani carries parasympathetic secretomotor fibers to the submandibular and sublingual glands. (L06/L08)
  • Apply it clinically: Bell’s palsy = idiopathic paralysis of one whole side of the face; Bell’s phenomenon = the eyeball rolls up when they try to close the eye; Ramsay Hunt (herpes zoster oticus) hits the geniculate ganglion + CN VIII. (L08 Face & Scalp)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Facial nerve exits the skull through stylomastoid foramen… Right after that, it passes through the substance of the parotid gland.”— Dr. Maini · L08 Face & Scalp · facial nerve course

🧪 Quick self-check
Where does CN VII exit the skull, and what are its taste vs facial-muscle roles?
Exits at the stylomastoid foramen, then through the parotid. Carries taste from the anterior 2/3 of the tongue (chorda tympani; cell bodies in the geniculate ganglion) and gives motor to the muscles of facial expression. Idiopathic paralysis = Bell’s palsy.
CN VIII
Describe the vestibulocochlear nerve (CN VIII) — its two divisions, its purely-sensory nature, where it ends, and its lesion signs.
source: Dr. Maini · L22 The Ear
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Split it: two components — cochlear (hearing) and vestibular (balance/equilibrium). It is purely sensory (special somatic afferent) — the hearing nerve. (L22 The Ear)
  • Enter it: passes through the internal acoustic meatus with CN VII. (L22 The Ear)
  • Note the exception: unlike CN VII it does NOT leave the skull — it ends in the inner ear within the petrous temporal bone. (L22 The Ear)
  • Read the lesions: cochlear lesion → tinnitus and/or deafness; vestibular lesion → vertigo and loss of balance. Because VII and VIII share the IAM, an acoustic neuroma (vestibular schwannoma) there can knock out BOTH. (L22 The Ear)
🎯 Cement it
📜 M14 Cranial Nerves Hub 🧠 Everything-MC Drill
🧪 Quick self-check
What are CN VIII’s two divisions, and what does a lesion of each one cause?
Cochlear (hearing → tinnitus/deafness if lesioned) and vestibular (balance → vertigo if lesioned). Purely sensory; enters the internal acoustic meatus with CN VII and stays in the petrous temporal bone.
CN IX
Cover the glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX) — its skull exit, its one muscle, posterior-tongue sensation/taste, parotid secretomotor, and its reflex roles.
source: Dr. Maini · L28 Pharynx / L10 Infratemporal Fossa / L06 Pharyngeal Apparatus
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Exit it: leaves the skull through the jugular foramen (with X and XI); it is the nerve of the 3rd pharyngeal arch. (L28 Pharynx)
  • Give its one muscle: stylopharyngeus — the ONLY pharyngeal muscle NOT supplied by the vagus. (L28 Pharynx; L29 Lab)
  • Own the tongue: carries BOTH general sensation AND taste from the posterior 1/3 of the tongue. (L06 Pharyngeal Apparatus)
  • Add the extras: parasympathetic secretomotor to the parotid gland via the otic ganglion; branches include the tympanic nerve (of Jacobson) → tympanic plexus, the carotid sinus/body afferents, and the afferent (sensory) limb of the gag reflex. (L10 Infratemporal Fossa)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“The mucosa overlying this area of the tongue receives its sensory innervation from the ninth cranial nerve, the glossopharyngeal.”— Dr. Maini · L06 Pharyngeal Apparatus · posterior tongue

🧪 Quick self-check
What single muscle and what tongue territory does CN IX serve, and which gland does it feed?
Stylopharyngeus (the only non-vagal pharyngeal muscle); general sensation + taste from the posterior 1/3 of the tongue; and parasympathetic secretomotor to the parotid via the otic ganglion. Nerve of the 3rd pharyngeal arch; exits the jugular foramen.
CN X
Follow the vagus nerve (CN X) — its skull exit, carotid-sheath course, its laryngeal branches, and its huge parasympathetic reach.
source: Dr. Maini · L05 Cervical Viscera / L26 The Larynx
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Route it: exits the jugular foramen, then runs in the carotid sheath with the common carotid artery and internal jugular vein (the “three musketeers”). (L05 Cervical Viscera)
  • Split the larynx supply: the recurrent laryngeal nerve supplies all intrinsic laryngeal muscles EXCEPT the cricothyroid (that one = the external laryngeal nerve, from the superior laryngeal). The RLN ascends in the tracheoesophageal groove. (L26 Larynx; L29 Lab)
  • Divide the sensation: above the vocal folds → internal laryngeal nerve; below the vocal folds → recurrent laryngeal nerve. Unilateral RLN injury → hoarseness; bilateral → aphonia + inspiratory stridor. (L05/L26)
  • Add the reach: palatoglossus is the one tongue muscle it supplies; it is the motor (efferent) limb of the gag reflex; and it gives parasympathetic supply to thoracic + abdominal viscera down to the left colic (splenic) flexure. (L06 Pharyngeal Apparatus)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Recurrent laryngeal nerve ascends on either side of the grooves between trachea and esophagus.”— Dr. Maini · L05 Cervical Viscera · RLN course
🎯 Cement it

🧪 Quick self-check
Which laryngeal muscle is NOT supplied by the recurrent laryngeal nerve, and what happens with a one-sided RLN injury?
The cricothyroid (supplied by the external laryngeal nerve). One-sided RLN injury → hoarseness; both RLNs → aphonia + inspiratory stridor. Sensation is internal laryngeal above the folds, recurrent laryngeal below.
CN XI
State the accessory nerve (CN XI) — its two muscles, its unusual foramen-magnum route, its cervical contribution, and how it’s tested.
source: Dr. Maini · L03 Cervical Triangles / L05 Cervical Viscera / L28 Pharynx
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Name its two muscles: the spinal root of CN XI supplies the sternocleidomastoid (SCM) and trapezius; it runs its exposed, surgically vulnerable course in the posterior triangle. (L03 Cervical Triangles)
  • Trace the odd route: the spinal root first ascends through the foramen magnum, then exits the skull via the jugular foramen (with IX and X). (L28 Pharynx)
  • Add the joiners: C2, C3, C4 fibers join it to help supply SCM and trapezius; the small cranial root joins CN X. (L03 Cervical Triangles)
  • Test + lesion it: shrug the shoulders and turn the head against resistance; a lesion → weak shrug, difficulty turning the head, drooping shoulder + scapular winging. (SCM acting on one side turns the head to the OPPOSITE side.) (L03/L05)

🧪 Quick self-check
Which two muscles does CN XI supply, and how do you test it clinically?
Sternocleidomastoid and trapezius. Test by shoulder shrug + head turn against resistance; a lesion → drooping shoulder, weak shrug, scapular winging. Its spinal root ascends through the foramen magnum, then exits the jugular foramen.
CN XII
Nail the hypoglossal nerve (CN XII) — its canal, the one tongue muscle it skips, its lesion sign, and the C1 hitchhikers.
source: Dr. Maini · L03 Cervical Triangles / L06 Pharyngeal Apparatus / L01 Osteology
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • Exit it: purely motor; leaves the cranial cavity through the hypoglossal canal (in the occipital bone, near the foramen magnum). (L01 Osteology)
  • Give the exception: it supplies ALL tongue muscles EXCEPT palatoglossus (that one = the vagus, CN X). Genioglossus is the chief protruder. (L06 Pharyngeal Apparatus; L29 Lab)
  • Read the lesion: a unilateral CN XII lesion makes the tongue deviate TOWARD the affected side on protrusion (with wasting) — “the tongue points at the lesion.” (CN XII clinical)
  • Track the hitchhikers: C1 fibers ride along CN XII, then peel off to the superior root of the ansa cervicalis (infrahyoid muscles) plus thyrohyoid and geniohyoid. (L03 Cervical Triangles)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“The motor innervation of tongue muscles comes from the hypoglossal nerve… except the palatoglossus muscle.”— Dr. Maini · L06 Pharyngeal Apparatus · tongue muscles

🧪 Quick self-check
Which tongue muscle does CN XII NOT supply, and which way does the tongue deviate with a one-sided lesion?
Palatoglossus (that one is CN X / vagus). A unilateral CN XII lesion → the tongue deviates toward the affected side on protrusion. It exits through the hypoglossal canal in the occipital bone; genioglossus is the protruder.
BIG PICTURE
Synthesize the whole set — the 12-nerve count, the skull-base foramina map, the four parasympathetic nerves and their ganglia, the tongue map, and the pure motor/sensory nerves.
source: Dr. Maini · L01 Osteology / L11 Infratemporal Fossa / L14 Cranial Meninges · CN overview
✅ To prove you’ve got it, you can:
  • State the count + foramina map: 12 pairs of cranial nerves. Jugular foramen = IX, X, XI; hypoglossal canal = XII; internal acoustic meatus = VII, VIII; superior orbital fissure = III, IV, VI, V1. (The sigmoid sinus becomes the internal jugular vein at the jugular foramen.) (L01 Osteology / L28)
  • List the parasympathetics: the 4 cranial-outflow nerves = III, VII, IX, X, with their ganglia — ciliary = III, otic = IX (parotid). The cavernous sinus carries III, IV, VI and V1/V2. (L11/L14)
  • Draw the tongue map: anterior 2/3 — general sensation = lingual (V3), taste = chorda tympani (VII); posterior 1/3 — general + taste = IX. (L06 Pharyngeal Apparatus)
  • Sort the fiber types: purely sensory = I, II, VIII; purely motor includes XII (also III, IV, VI, XI). Sensory ganglia (geniculate, trigeminal, spiral/vestibular) hold afferent cell bodies. (CN overview)
🎤 Dr. Maini said
“Ciliary ganglion is associated with third nerve… Otic ganglion… ninth nerve carries preganglionic parasympathetic fibers to this ganglion.”— Dr. Maini · L10/L11 Infratemporal Fossa · ganglia

🧪 Quick self-check
Which nerves share the jugular foramen, and which four cranial nerves carry parasympathetic fibers?
Jugular foramen = IX, X, XI. Parasympathetics = III, VII, IX, X (ganglia: ciliary III, otic IX). Purely sensory = I, II, VIII; hypoglossal canal = XII; internal acoustic meatus = VII, VIII.

⚠️ Don’t mix up the “exception” muscles. Palatoglossus is supplied by CN X (vagus), not CN XII. Stylopharyngeus is supplied by CN IX, not the vagus. The cricothyroid is supplied by the external laryngeal nerve, not the recurrent laryngeal. And among the palatine muscles, tensor veli palatini is the odd one out — supplied by V3, not CN X. (Dr. Maini, L29 Lab: “tensor veli palatini is not innervated by 10th nerve… V3.”)
